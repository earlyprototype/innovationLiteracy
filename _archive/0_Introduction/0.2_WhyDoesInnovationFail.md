# 0.2 Why Does Innovation Fail?

## Purpose
Confront the reality that the majority of innovation initiatives fail, and examine the root causes -- not to discourage, but to equip participants with the awareness needed to avoid the most common and most damaging pitfalls.

## Key Learning Points

### Innovation Is Hard
- Approximately 70% of innovation initiatives fail to deliver their intended outcomes (McKinsey, BCG, various industry studies)
- This is not because organisations lack talent or ambition -- it is because innovation is inherently uncertain, and most organisations are structurally optimised for certainty
- Innovation requires navigating ambiguity, making decisions with incomplete information, and sustaining effort through periods where progress is not visible
- The organisational immune system -- the structures, habits, and incentives that protect operational stability -- actively works against the conditions innovation needs
- Failure is not an aberration in innovation; it is a structural feature. The question is not how to eliminate failure but how to make it productive (explored further in Section 1.1)

### Cargo Culting
- The term originates from post-World War II Pacific island cultures that built replica airstrips and control towers in the belief that the form would produce the function -- that mimicking the visible artefacts of air traffic would cause planes to land
- In innovation, cargo culting is the adoption of innovation practices in form without substance:
  - Post-it notes on walls without structured problem framing
  - Innovation labs without governance or connection to strategic objectives
  - Hackathons without follow-through mechanisms
  - Design Thinking workshops without iterative implementation
  - Innovation titles and roles without authority, resources, or mandate
- Cargo culting is not always cynical -- it often arises from genuine intent combined with insufficient understanding of what the practices actually require
- It is one of the most pervasive and damaging patterns in organisational innovation because it creates the *appearance* of innovation activity while consuming resources and eroding trust
- The antidote is literacy: understanding not just what innovative organisations do, but *why* those practices work and *what conditions* they require

### The Myth of the Lone Innovator
- The popular narrative of innovation is dominated by singular figures: Edison, Jobs, Musk
- This narrative is historically inaccurate -- Edison ran a laboratory of dozens; the Macintosh was a team of hundreds; SpaceX's achievements are the product of thousands of engineers
- More critically, it is actively harmful as an organisational model:
  - It concentrates expectation on individuals rather than building systemic capability
  - It discourages participation by implying innovation is the province of exceptional people
  - It undermines the diversity of perspective that is essential to effective innovation
  - It creates single points of failure
- Meaningful innovation is a team-led process that depends on the integration of diverse perspectives, skills, and domains of expertise
- The shift from "who is our innovator?" to "how do we innovate together?" is foundational to everything that follows in this workshop

## Discussion Prompts
- Have you seen examples of innovation cargo culting in practice? What did it look like, and what was the impact?
- When innovation initiatives have stalled or failed in your experience, what were the underlying causes?
- How does the "lone innovator" myth manifest in your organisation or industry?

## Key Takeaways
1. Innovation failure is common, structural, and largely predictable -- not a sign of organisational weakness but of misalignment between intent and conditions
2. Cargo culting -- adopting innovation practices in form without substance -- is among the most widespread root causes
3. The myth of the lone innovator is a barrier to building genuine innovation capability; innovation is a team-led, diversity-dependent discipline
