# 0.4 Why Innovation Literacy Is Not Optional

## Purpose
Establish that innovation literacy is an increasingly critical organisational and individual competency -- not a desirable extra. Validate that participants likely already possess elements of it, while making clear that partial literacy carries real risks.

## Key Learning Points

### You Already Have Some of This
- Most professionals engaged in R&D, technology adoption, or organisational development already practise elements of innovation literacy -- problem identification, experimentation, iterative refinement, collaborative exploration
- This existing capability is real and should be recognised, not dismissed
- The challenge is that it is often informal, inconsistent, and context-dependent -- it works in familiar situations but may not transfer to new or more complex challenges
- Formalising and extending what already exists is significantly more effective than starting from scratch

### The Problem with Partial Literacy
- Partial innovation literacy creates blind spots that are often invisible to those who have them:
  - A team may excel at ideation but lack structured evaluation -- generating many ideas but unable to identify which are worth pursuing
  - An organisation may embrace experimentation but lack the frameworks to capture learning -- repeating the same failures without accumulating insight
  - Leadership may support "innovation" in the abstract but lack sufficient understanding to protect the conditions it requires -- inadvertently undermining the work they have sanctioned
  - Individuals may have strong process skills but lack the behaviours (active listening, constructive challenge) that make those skills effective in a team context
- Partial literacy is not neutral -- it can be actively harmful, because it creates confidence without completeness

### The Broader Context: Why Now
- The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report consistently identifies the skills underpinning innovation literacy -- analytical thinking, creative thinking, resilience, flexibility, curiosity, lifelong learning -- among the fastest-growing competency requirements across industries
- This is not sector-specific: the demand for these capabilities is rising in manufacturing, technology, services, and the public sector alike
- The accelerating pace of technological change (including AI, immersive technologies, and digital transformation more broadly) means that the ability to innovate effectively is shifting from a competitive advantage to a baseline requirement
- Organisations that lack innovation literacy at sufficient depth and breadth will find themselves increasingly unable to respond to changing conditions, adopt new technologies effectively, or retain the talent that drives growth

### Completing the Picture
- The objective is not to replace what participants already know -- it is to complete the picture:
  - **Developing what is latent** -- making conscious and deliberate the innovation practices that are currently intuitive or informal
  - **Filling what is absent** -- addressing the gaps that create blind spots and limit effectiveness
  - **Making it deliberate** -- moving from innovation-by-accident to innovation-by-design, where the full range of elements (process, tools, skills, mindset, behaviours, teams, facilitation, environment) are understood and intentionally cultivated

## Discussion Prompts
- Where do you feel your innovation literacy is strongest? Where are the gaps you are most aware of?
- Have you experienced situations where partial understanding of innovation led to poor outcomes -- either for you or for your organisation?
- What would "complete enough" innovation literacy look like for your team?

## Key Takeaways
1. Most participants already possess real innovation capability -- the task is to formalise, extend, and complete it
2. Partial literacy creates blind spots that can be actively harmful, not merely limiting
3. The skills underpinning innovation literacy are among the fastest-growing competency requirements globally -- this is not optional
4. The workshop aims to develop what is latent, fill what is absent, and make the whole thing deliberate
