# 0.3 What Do We Mean by 'Literacy'?

## Purpose
Define innovation literacy as a distinct concept -- neither casual familiarity nor deep specialism -- and establish why this level of understanding is the appropriate target for the majority of people involved in innovation work.

## Key Learning Points

### Literacy vs Expertise
- Literacy is the ability to understand, navigate, and participate effectively in a domain without being a specialist in it
- Consider the analogy of financial literacy: a financially literate person can read a balance sheet, ask informed questions about budgets, and make sound decisions about expenditure -- without being an accountant
- Innovation literacy operates on the same principle: the ability to recognise good practice, participate meaningfully in innovation processes, ask the right questions, and make informed decisions about where and how to invest effort
- It is not about turning everyone into an innovation specialist -- it is about ensuring that the people involved in, affected by, or making decisions about innovation have sufficient understanding to do so effectively

### What Innovation Literacy Enables
- **Informed participation** -- the ability to contribute to innovation processes rather than simply being present in them
- **Quality questioning** -- knowing what questions to ask at each stage (Is the problem well-framed? Have we validated assumptions? What are we actually testing?)
- **Recognition of good practice** -- being able to distinguish between genuine innovation activity and cargo culting (see 0.2)
- **Sound decision-making** -- understanding the trade-offs, timelines, and uncertainties involved well enough to make or support resource allocation decisions
- **Constructive challenge** -- the confidence to push back on weak thinking, unsupported claims, or premature commitment without derailing the process

### What Innovation Literacy Is Not
- It is not a credential or a certification
- It is not about memorising frameworks or methodologies
- It is not a substitute for deep domain expertise (which remains essential -- innovation literacy complements it)
- It is not a one-time acquisition -- like any literacy, it develops through practice, reflection, and application

### The Literacy Spectrum
- Participants will sit at different points on the literacy spectrum, and that is expected
- Some will have strong intuitive practice but lack the language or frameworks to articulate it
- Some will have theoretical knowledge but limited practical application
- Some will be encountering these concepts for the first time
- The objective is not to bring everyone to the same point, but to ensure everyone has a complete enough picture to participate effectively and to identify where their own development priorities lie

## Discussion Prompts
- Where would you place yourself on the innovation literacy spectrum? What aspects feel familiar, and where are the gaps?
- Can you think of a situation where lack of innovation literacy -- in yourself or others -- led to poor decisions or missed opportunities?
- What does "good enough" innovation literacy look like for your role?

## Key Takeaways
1. Innovation literacy is about understanding, navigating, and participating -- not about becoming a specialist
2. It enables informed participation, quality questioning, recognition of good practice, and sound decision-making
3. It exists on a spectrum, and the goal is a complete-enough picture to participate effectively
