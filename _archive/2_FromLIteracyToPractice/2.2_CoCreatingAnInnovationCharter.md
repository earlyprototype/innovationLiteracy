# 2.2 Co-creating an Innovation Charter

## Purpose
Introduce the innovation charter as the organisational agreement that governs how innovation will be approached, resourced, and protected. Establish that co-creation is essential -- a charter imposed from above lacks the ownership and operational grounding needed to survive contact with reality.

## Key Learning Points

### What Is an Innovation Charter?
- An innovation charter is a written, agreed document that defines the terms under which innovation will operate within an organisation
- It is not a strategy document (which defines *what* to innovate towards) -- it is an operating agreement (which defines *how* innovation will be conducted, supported, and governed)
- It serves multiple functions:
  - **Clarity** -- everyone involved understands the scope, expectations, and rules of engagement
  - **Protection** -- it provides a reference point when operational pressures threaten to encroach on innovation activity
  - **Accountability** -- it establishes what the organisation has committed to, making it harder to retroactively withdraw support
  - **Alignment** -- it connects innovation activity to organisational objectives, preventing it from being perceived as disconnected or self-indulgent

### Why Co-creation Matters
- A charter that is written by leadership and handed to teams will be treated as a mandate -- complied with at best, resisted at worst
- A charter that is co-created -- with meaningful participation from the people who will do the innovation work, their managers, and leadership -- produces:
  - **Ownership** -- participants who helped create the charter are invested in its success
  - **Operational realism** -- front-line contributors surface practical constraints that leadership may not see
  - **Shared language** -- the process of creating the charter builds common understanding of what innovation means in this organisation
  - **Early buy-in** -- involvement in the creation process is itself a form of permission-building (see 2.1)
- Co-creation does not mean consensus on every detail -- it means genuine, structured engagement that ensures the charter reflects multiple perspectives

### What the Charter Should Address

#### Scope and Boundaries
- What kinds of innovation activity are in scope? (Product, process, technology adoption, business model, organisational?)
- What is explicitly out of scope?
- How does innovation activity relate to existing R&D, continuous improvement, or digital transformation initiatives?

#### Time and Resources
- How much time is allocated to innovation work, and how is it protected?
- What budget is available for prototyping, tools, external expertise, and research?
- Who is involved, and what proportion of their time is committed?
- How are resource conflicts between innovation and operations resolved?

#### Decision-Making and Governance
- How are innovation priorities set?
- What stage gates exist, and what criteria are used at each?
- Who has decision-making authority at each stage?
- How are decisions to stop or pivot communicated and handled?

#### Behaviours and Norms
- What behaviours are expected within innovation work? (Drawing on Section 1.6)
- How is psychological safety maintained?
- How is constructive challenge encouraged and protected?
- What norms govern the sharing of unfinished work?

#### Capturing and Communicating Outcomes
- How are outcomes (including learning, not only outputs) documented?
- How is innovation progress communicated to leadership, to teams, and to the wider organisation?
- What metrics are used, and how are they stage-appropriate? (Drawing on Section 1.1)
- How is the value of innovation articulated to different audiences? (Drawing on Section 2.3)

### The Charter as a Living Document
- The charter is not a one-time exercise -- it should be reviewed and revised as the organisation's innovation maturity evolves
- Early charters may be cautious and tightly scoped; as experience and confidence grow, they can expand
- Regular review (quarterly or biannually) ensures the charter remains relevant and responsive to changing conditions
- The review process itself is a form of innovation practice -- reflecting on what is working, what is not, and what needs to change

### Practical Considerations for Charter Creation
- **Start small** -- a charter does not need to be comprehensive on day one. Begin with the most critical elements (scope, time, decision-making) and expand over time
- **Make it visible** -- the charter should be accessible to everyone it affects, not buried in a shared drive
- **Reference it regularly** -- use it as a touchpoint in innovation sessions, decision points, and resource discussions
- **Update it honestly** -- if commitments in the charter are not being honoured, that is information about what needs to change, not a reason to quietly abandon the document

## Discussion Prompts
- Does your organisation have anything resembling an innovation charter? If so, how effective is it? If not, what would be the most important elements to include?
- Who should be involved in creating an innovation charter for your context?
- What commitments would be most valuable to codify -- and which would be most difficult to secure?

## Key Takeaways
1. An innovation charter is the organisational agreement that defines how innovation will be conducted, resourced, and protected
2. Co-creation is essential -- participation builds ownership, operational realism, shared language, and early buy-in
3. The charter should address scope, time and resources, decision-making, behaviours, and outcome capture
4. It is a living document that evolves with the organisation's innovation maturity
