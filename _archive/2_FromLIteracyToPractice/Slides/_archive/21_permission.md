## Slide Title
2.1 Permission Through Understanding

### Text
Innovation cannot be sustained on enthusiasm alone. It requires decision-makers who **genuinely understand** what innovation demands.

#### What Decision-Makers Need to Know
- Innovation is a process, not an event -- it takes time and follows a non-linear path
- The early stages look unproductive -- this is normal, not failure
- Failure is a feature -- punishing failure punishes learning
- Operational metrics kill innovation -- ROI on early-stage exploration is the wrong question
- The absence of outputs does not mean the absence of value

#### The Middle Management Layer
Senior leadership may endorse innovation. Front-line teams may be enthusiastic.
Middle managers must reconcile innovation time with delivery targets.

**Without their informed support, top-level endorsement does not reach the teams.**

### Speaker Notes
This is where the workshop becomes directly operational. The five points under "what decision-makers need to know" are the distilled version of everything covered in Sections 0 and 1 -- framed for an audience that makes resource allocation decisions. The middle management point is critical and often overlooked: in most organisations, middle management is where innovation permission is either transmitted or filtered out. This is not a criticism of middle managers; it is a structural observation. They are the ones who must balance the books, and without explicit engagement in the understanding-building process, they will rationally default to protecting operational delivery. Ask participants: who in your organisation needs to understand this? How would you communicate it?
