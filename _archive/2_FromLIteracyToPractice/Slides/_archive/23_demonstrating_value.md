## Slide Title
2.3 Demonstrating Value

### Text
The value of innovation extends well beyond direct outputs.

#### Five Layers of Innovation Value
| Layer | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Capability** | The organisation can do things it could not do before |
| **Learning** | Validated knowledge, regardless of project outcomes |
| **Engagement & Retention** | Meaningful work drives commitment, especially among high-capability people |
| **Pipeline** | A portfolio of opportunities at various stages of readiness |
| **Strategic Positioning** | Visible innovation builds credibility and presence |

#### Match Evidence to Audience
- **Leadership**: pipeline, strategic positioning, risk reduction
- **Team members**: capability, engagement, meaningfulness
- **Middle management**: operational relevance, resource efficiency
- **External stakeholders**: learning, demonstrated impact, alignment

### Speaker Notes
This slide ties together the entire workshop. The five layers directly address the question "how do we justify continued investment in innovation?" -- which is the practical question that determines whether everything discussed today actually survives contact with organisational reality. Walk through each layer with brief examples. The engagement and retention point is particularly powerful: in a competitive labour market, the ability to offer meaningful innovation work is a genuine retention tool, especially for the people organisations can least afford to lose. The "match evidence to audience" framework is immediately actionable. Close by connecting back to the charter: the charter should define not only what the organisation will do, but how it will articulate why it matters.
