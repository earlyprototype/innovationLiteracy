## Slide Title
2.2 Co-creating an Innovation Charter

### Text
An innovation charter is the organisational agreement that defines how innovation will be approached, governed, and protected.

**Co-created, not imposed.**

#### What the Charter Should Address
1. **Scope & Boundaries** -- what is in and out of scope
2. **Time & Resources** -- how they are allocated and protected
3. **Decision-Making** -- stage gates, criteria, authority
4. **Behaviours & Norms** -- the ways of working within innovation activity
5. **Outcomes & Communication** -- how learning, not only outputs, is captured and shared

#### Why Co-creation Matters
- Builds ownership among those who will do the work
- Surfaces practical constraints early
- Ensures the charter reflects operational reality, not aspirational abstraction
- The process of creating the charter is itself a form of alignment

### Speaker Notes
The charter concept gives participants something concrete to take away. Emphasise that it does not need to be comprehensive on day one -- start with the most critical elements and expand over time. The five areas map directly to the content of the workshop: scope relates to process and interventions; time and resources to environment; decision-making to measurement and stage gates; behaviours to the entire human elements discussion; outcomes to demonstrating value. The "co-created, not imposed" point is worth reinforcing: a charter written by leadership and handed down will be complied with at best. A charter co-created with the team will be owned. The charter should be a living document -- reviewed and revised as the organisation's innovation maturity evolves.
