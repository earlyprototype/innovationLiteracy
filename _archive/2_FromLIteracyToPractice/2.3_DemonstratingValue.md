# 2.3 Demonstrating Value

## Purpose
Establish that sustaining innovation requires an ongoing ability to articulate why it matters -- and to do so in terms that resonate with different audiences. Introduce a multi-layered value framework that extends well beyond direct outputs and equip participants with the thinking to apply it in their own context.

## Key Learning Points

### Why Demonstrating Value Is Critical
- Innovation investment is inherently vulnerable: it competes for resources with operational activities that have more immediate, more visible, and more predictable returns
- Without a deliberate, ongoing practice of demonstrating value, innovation budgets and time allocations are the first to be cut when pressure mounts
- The challenge is that innovation value is often diffuse, delayed, and qualitatively different from operational value -- the methods used to demonstrate operational performance (quarterly financials, efficiency metrics, delivery targets) do not capture it
- Organisations that cannot articulate the value of their innovation work will lose the organisational permission to continue it, regardless of how good the work is

### The Five Layers of Innovation Value

#### 1. Capability Value
- The organisation can now do things it could not do before
- This includes: the ability to run structured experiments, frame problems rigorously, evaluate opportunities systematically, prototype and test concepts, and make evidence-based decisions under uncertainty
- Capability value persists even if specific projects do not reach implementation -- the organisation is stronger, more adaptable, and better equipped
- Measurement: track the range of innovation methods the team can deploy, the quality of problem framing, the speed and rigour of experimentation

#### 2. Learning Value
- What the organisation now knows that it did not know before
- Validated assumptions, disproven hypotheses, mapped opportunities, identified constraints, and characterised risks all constitute strategic knowledge
- Learning value is real even when -- especially when -- it prevents the organisation from pursuing a path that would have failed at greater cost
- This is perhaps the most under-communicated category of value: organisations routinely fail to articulate "we learned that X does not work, which saved us Y in wasted investment"
- Measurement: documented insights, assumptions tested, decisions informed by innovation-generated evidence

#### 3. Engagement and Retention Value
- Meaningful participation in innovation work has measurable effects on team engagement, job satisfaction, sense of purpose, and retention
- This is particularly pronounced among high-capability employees -- the people most likely to leave if they feel intellectually underutilised or excluded from meaningful work
- Innovation participation signals that the organisation values its people's thinking, not only their operational output
- In a competitive labour market, the ability to offer meaningful innovation work is a genuine retention tool
- Measurement: engagement survey data, retention rates among innovation participants vs non-participants, qualitative feedback

#### 4. Pipeline Value
- A portfolio of concepts, prototypes, and validated opportunities at various stages of maturity
- This is the innovation equivalent of a sales pipeline -- it represents future potential and reduces the organisation's dependence on any single initiative
- Pipeline value is about optionality: having a range of possibilities at different stages of readiness means the organisation can respond to changing conditions with prepared options rather than starting from scratch
- Measurement: number of concepts in pipeline, stage distribution, progression rates, estimated value at maturity

#### 5. Strategic Positioning Value
- Visible innovation activity builds credibility, relationships, and presence in emerging spaces
- For organisations operating in R&D partnerships, technology advocacy, or market development, the act of innovating visibly is itself a form of positioning
- It signals to partners, clients, funders, and talent that the organisation is forward-looking, capable, and engaged with the frontier
- Measurement: partnership opportunities, media and conference presence, reputation indicators, talent attraction

### Matching Evidence to Audience
- The discipline of demonstrating value requires understanding what different stakeholders care about and presenting evidence accordingly:
  - **Leadership and board** -- pipeline value, strategic positioning, capability growth, risk reduction
  - **Team members** -- engagement, skill development, meaningfulness of work, agency
  - **Middle management** -- operational relevance, resource efficiency, alignment with delivery targets
  - **External stakeholders and funders** -- learning outputs, demonstrated impact, pipeline potential, strategic alignment
  - **Clients and partners** -- capability, credibility, responsiveness to emerging challenges
- The same innovation work may produce value across all five layers -- the task is to communicate the right layer to the right audience in terms they recognise and value

### Integrating Value Demonstration into Practice
- Demonstrating value should not be a retrospective exercise conducted when budgets are under threat -- it should be a continuous practice embedded in the innovation process:
  - **Regular reporting** on learning, pipeline status, and capability development -- not only on outputs
  - **Storytelling** -- narratives that connect innovation work to organisational objectives and human impact are more compelling than metrics alone
  - **Visibility** -- making innovation work and its outcomes visible to the wider organisation (exhibitions, internal presentations, shared documentation)
  - **The innovation charter** (see 2.2) should specify how value will be captured and communicated, making it a governance commitment rather than an afterthought

### The Value Articulation Trap
- A caution: demonstrating value is not the same as justifying existence
- If the practice of value demonstration becomes defensive -- constantly proving that innovation "deserves" its resources -- it has already been undermined
- The healthiest state is one where value demonstration is a natural, integrated part of innovation practice, not a separate exercise driven by institutional anxiety
- Building understanding (see 2.1) and codifying commitments (see 2.2) reduces the need for defensive justification

## Discussion Prompts
- Which of the five value layers is most relevant to your organisation's current priorities?
- How do you currently communicate the value of innovation or R&D work? To whom, and in what terms?
- Can you identify examples of valuable innovation learning that was never communicated -- and what the consequence was?

## Key Takeaways
1. Innovation value extends well beyond direct outputs -- it encompasses capability, learning, engagement, pipeline, and strategic positioning
2. Different stakeholders care about different layers of value; the discipline is matching evidence to audience
3. Value demonstration should be continuous and integrated into practice, not retrospective and defensive
4. The innovation charter should codify how value will be captured and communicated, making it a governance commitment
