# 1.1 Process

## Purpose
Establish that innovation is a structured, repeatable process -- not a flash of inspiration -- and introduce Design Thinking as a practical framework. Embed the principles of iteration, failure-as-data, and stage-appropriate measurement as core features of how the process operates.

## Key Learning Points

### Innovation as a Structured Process
- The single most important shift in understanding: innovation is a *process*, not an event
- It can be learned, practised, and improved -- it is not dependent on innate talent or personality
- Structure does not constrain creativity; it provides the scaffold within which creative work becomes productive
- Without process, innovation efforts tend to oscillate between unstructured brainstorming and premature commitment to the first plausible idea

### Design Thinking as a Framework
- Design Thinking is a human-centred approach to innovation that integrates the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and the requirements for organisational viability
- It provides a practical, widely adopted framework that is accessible to non-specialists while remaining rigorous enough for complex challenges

#### The Five Phases
1. **Empathise** -- deeply understand the people affected by the challenge: their needs, constraints, behaviours, and context. This is where active listening (see 1.6) begins. The quality of the empathy phase determines the quality of everything that follows.
2. **Define** -- synthesise empathy findings into a clear, actionable problem statement. A well-framed problem is more than half the solution. Poor problem framing is one of the most common causes of innovation failure.
3. **Ideate** -- generate a wide range of potential solutions. The emphasis is on quantity, diversity, and deferring judgement. This is the phase where operational thinking (convergent, evaluative, risk-averse) must be deliberately suspended.
4. **Prototype** -- build quick, low-fidelity representations of promising ideas. The purpose is to make thinking tangible and testable, not to produce finished solutions. Prototypes are questions, not answers.
5. **Test** -- put prototypes in front of real users or stakeholders, gather feedback, and learn. Testing is not validation -- it is investigation. The goal is to discover what works, what does not, and why.

#### Key Characteristics
- **Non-linear** -- the phases are not a strict sequence. Teams may move back and forth between phases as understanding deepens.
- **Divergent and convergent** -- the process alternates between expanding possibilities (divergent thinking) and narrowing to decisions (convergent thinking). Both modes are essential; collapsing them together is a common error.
- **Human-centred** -- the process begins and ends with people, not technology or organisational convenience.

### Iteration and Failure as Data
- Design Thinking is fundamentally iterative: the expectation is that ideas will be tested, will often fall short, and will be refined through repeated cycles
- Each iteration narrows uncertainty and builds the evidence base for better decisions
- Failure within this context is not a setback -- it is data. An experiment that disproves a hypothesis has produced valuable information.
- The discipline is in ensuring that every iteration captures learning: what was tested, what was observed, what was concluded, what changed as a result
- Organisations that treat iteration as a sign of indecision or failure-as-waste will struggle to sustain innovation processes

### Measurement: What to Evaluate and How
- Innovation effort should be evaluated by what is done, what is captured, and what is learned -- not solely by outputs
- **Stage-appropriate measurement** is essential:
  - Early stages (Empathise, Define): measure breadth of inquiry, quality of problem framing, diversity of perspectives consulted
  - Middle stages (Ideate, Prototype): measure volume of ideas, speed of prototyping, number of assumptions tested
  - Later stages (Test, implementation): measure validated outcomes, user/stakeholder feedback, learning captured
- Applying operational metrics (ROI, revenue impact, efficiency gains) to early-stage innovation work is a form of cargo culting -- it produces the appearance of rigour while systematically killing exploratory work
- The guiding principle: measure to learn and improve, not to justify or control

## Discussion Prompts
- Have you used Design Thinking or similar structured processes before? What worked, and what did not?
- How does your organisation currently treat failure in the context of innovation or experimentation?
- What metrics are currently applied to innovation work in your context? Are they stage-appropriate?

## Key Takeaways
1. Innovation is a structured, repeatable process -- not a personality trait or a lucky accident
2. Design Thinking provides a practical, human-centred framework built on empathy, problem framing, ideation, prototyping, and testing
3. The process is iterative: failure is data, and each cycle builds the evidence base for better decisions
4. Measurement must be stage-appropriate -- evaluating early-stage work with operational metrics will systematically undermine innovation
