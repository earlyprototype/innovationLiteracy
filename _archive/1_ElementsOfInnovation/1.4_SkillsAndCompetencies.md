# 1.4 Skills and Competencies

## Purpose
Identify the specific capabilities that effective innovation demands, map them against broader workforce trends, and establish that these skills can be developed intentionally rather than left to chance.

## Key Learning Points

### Innovation Skills Are Identifiable and Developable
- Innovation is often treated as if it depends on an indefinable quality -- "creativity" or "vision" -- that some people have and others do not
- In reality, the skills that underpin effective innovation are identifiable, specific, and learnable
- They can be developed through deliberate practice, structured experience, and reflection
- Organisations that treat innovation skills as innate rather than developable systematically underinvest in their own capability

### Core Innovation Skills

#### Analytical Thinking
- The ability to break complex problems into component parts, identify patterns, and evaluate evidence
- Essential at every stage of the innovation process, from problem framing through to evaluation
- Not the opposite of creative thinking -- effective innovation requires both in alternation

#### Creative Thinking
- The ability to generate novel ideas, make unexpected connections, and challenge established patterns
- Includes both generative creativity (producing new ideas) and adaptive creativity (finding new applications for existing concepts)
- Constrained creativity -- working within defined boundaries -- is often more productive than unconstrained ideation

#### Communication
- The ability to articulate ideas clearly, listen actively, and adapt messaging to different audiences
- Encompasses visual communication (sketching, diagramming, prototyping) as well as verbal and written
- Innovation frequently fails not because the idea was poor but because it was poorly communicated -- to teams, to leadership, to stakeholders

#### Experimentation
- The ability and willingness to design and run tests, gather evidence, and learn from results
- Includes comfort with the possibility that the experiment will disprove the hypothesis
- Distinct from "trial and error" -- experimentation is structured, purposeful, and documented

#### Comfort with Ambiguity
- The ability to function effectively when outcomes are uncertain, information is incomplete, and the path forward is unclear
- This is not a personality trait -- it is a practised capability that develops through repeated exposure to ambiguous situations within supportive structures
- Organisations that demand certainty at every stage will struggle to sustain innovation, regardless of the skills their people possess

#### Collaboration
- The ability to work effectively with others, particularly across disciplines, functions, and perspectives
- Includes the willingness to share ideas in unfinished form, build on others' contributions, and subordinate individual ownership to collective outcomes
- Closely linked to active listening (see 1.6) and team dynamics (see 1.7)

#### Critical Evaluation
- The ability to assess ideas, evidence, and proposals rigorously and constructively
- Includes distinguishing between personal preference and objective assessment, and between constructive challenge and destructive criticism
- Essential at stage gates and decision points -- without critical evaluation, innovation processes generate activity without discrimination

### The WEF Future of Jobs Perspective
- The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report identifies the following among the top growing skills globally:
  - Analytical thinking
  - Creative thinking
  - Resilience, flexibility, and agility
  - Motivation and self-awareness
  - Curiosity and lifelong learning
  - Technological literacy
  - Empathy and active listening
- These align closely with the innovation skills outlined above -- the implication is clear: innovation literacy and workforce readiness are converging
- Organisations investing in innovation skills are simultaneously investing in broader workforce resilience and adaptability

### Developing Innovation Skills Intentionally
- Skills develop through a cycle of experience, reflection, and deliberate practice -- not through exposure alone
- Structured innovation processes (such as Design Thinking) provide the experiential component
- Facilitated reflection (see 1.8) ensures that experience translates into learning
- Organisations that create regular opportunities for innovation work -- not as a one-off event but as an ongoing practice -- see sustained skill development
- Individual development plans should include innovation skills alongside technical and operational capabilities

## Discussion Prompts
- Which of these skills do you feel most confident in? Which represent development areas?
- How does your organisation currently support the development of innovation skills -- formally or informally?
- Where do you see the overlap between innovation skills and the broader skills your team needs for the future?

## Key Takeaways
1. Innovation depends on identifiable, specific skills -- not indefinable talent
2. The core skills include analytical thinking, creative thinking, communication, experimentation, comfort with ambiguity, collaboration, and critical evaluation
3. These skills align closely with the fastest-growing competency requirements identified by the WEF -- innovation literacy and workforce readiness are converging
4. Skills develop through deliberate practice and structured experience, not through exposure alone
