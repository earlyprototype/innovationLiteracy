# 1.7 Teams

## Purpose
Establish that innovation is fundamentally a team activity, and that the dynamics of how people work together are as important as individual capability. Explore the characteristics of effective innovation teams and how they can be deliberately composed, structured, and supported.

## Key Learning Points

### Why Teams, Not Individuals
- As established in Section 0.2, the myth of the lone innovator is one of the most persistent and damaging misconceptions in innovation
- Innovation requires the integration of diverse knowledge, perspectives, and skills that no single individual possesses
- The cognitive demands of innovation -- empathy, creativity, analysis, evaluation, communication -- are distributed capabilities; they are stronger in combination than in any one person
- Effective innovation teams consistently outperform talented individuals working alone, provided the team dynamics are right

### Characteristics of Effective Innovation Teams

#### Psychological Safety
- The shared belief that the team environment is safe for interpersonal risk-taking -- that members can speak up, ask questions, challenge ideas, admit mistakes, and share unfinished thinking without fear of punishment or humiliation
- This is not "being nice" -- psychologically safe teams can have rigorous, even heated, debate. The safety lies in the knowledge that challenge is directed at ideas, not at people
- Research (notably Amy Edmondson's work, and Google's Project Aristotle) consistently identifies psychological safety as the single most important factor in team effectiveness
- It is established through leader behaviour, explicit norms, and consistent reinforcement -- and it can be destroyed by a single public punishment of vulnerability
- Without psychological safety, the innovation behaviours outlined in 1.6 (constructive challenge, sharing work in progress, active listening) cannot be sustained

#### Cognitive Diversity
- The inclusion of people who think differently -- who bring different mental models, disciplinary backgrounds, problem-solving approaches, and life experiences
- Distinct from demographic diversity (though often correlated) -- cognitive diversity is specifically about variation in how people perceive, interpret, and respond to challenges
- Cognitive diversity is the raw material of innovation: it produces the range of perspectives needed to frame problems comprehensively, generate non-obvious ideas, and identify blind spots
- It also produces friction -- people who think differently will disagree, misunderstand each other, and find collaboration harder. This friction is productive only when managed through the behaviours (1.6) and facilitation (1.8) discussed elsewhere

#### Role Clarity
- Each team member should understand their role, their contribution, and how their work connects to the broader innovation effort
- Role clarity is not the same as rigidity -- in innovation teams, roles may shift as the process evolves
- The critical distinction: clarity about *purpose and contribution* (why am I here, what am I expected to bring) rather than rigid *task assignment* (do exactly this and nothing else)
- Without role clarity, cognitive diversity becomes confusion rather than strength

#### The Expertise/Fresh Perspective Tension
- Innovation teams benefit from both deep domain expertise (people who understand the problem space thoroughly) and fresh perspective (people who are not constrained by existing assumptions about the domain)
- This is a productive tension: experts contribute knowledge, rigour, and contextual understanding; newcomers contribute naivety, unexpected questions, and analogies from other domains
- The danger of expertise-only teams: solutions tend to be incremental, anchored in what is already known
- The danger of outsider-only teams: solutions tend to be impractical, disconnected from operational reality
- The most effective innovation teams deliberately include both, and facilitate the interaction between them

### Team Size and Structure
- Innovation work is most effective in small teams (typically 3-7 members) where communication is direct and frequent
- Larger groups can be structured into sub-teams with clear interfaces and regular integration points
- Cross-functional composition -- deliberately bringing together people from different departments, disciplines, or domains -- is one of the most reliable ways to increase cognitive diversity
- Temporary, project-based team composition (rather than permanent innovation departments) can maintain freshness and prevent the formation of new orthodoxies

### Supporting Innovation Teams
- Teams do not become effective by accident -- they require:
  - **Explicit norms** -- agreed behaviours and ways of working (see 1.6)
  - **Skilled facilitation** -- particularly in the early stages when norms are being established and cognitive diversity is producing friction (see 1.8)
  - **Protected time and space** -- innovation teams that are expected to innovate on top of their operational responsibilities will default to operational work (see 1.9)
  - **Appropriate authority** -- the team must have sufficient decision-making power to act on its work, or a clear and responsive escalation path
  - **Access to resources** -- information, expertise, prototyping materials, and stakeholder access

## Discussion Prompts
- How are innovation teams currently composed in your organisation? Is it deliberate or ad hoc?
- Can you identify situations where cognitive diversity produced a breakthrough -- or where lack of diversity produced a blind spot?
- What does psychological safety look like in your team? What would need to change to strengthen it?

## Key Takeaways
1. Innovation is a team activity -- the myth of the lone innovator is a barrier to building genuine capability
2. Psychological safety is the foundational condition for effective innovation teams
3. Cognitive diversity provides the range of perspectives innovation requires, but must be managed through behaviours and facilitation to be productive
4. Effective teams balance deep expertise with fresh perspective, and require explicit norms, facilitation, protected time, and appropriate authority
