# 1.3 Tools

## Purpose
Provide a practical survey of the tools available to support innovation work -- both traditional and digital -- and establish the principle that tools are enablers within a broader system, not solutions in themselves.

## Key Learning Points

### Tools in Context
- Tools serve the process; the process does not serve the tools
- A common cargo-culting pattern is the adoption of innovation tools (software platforms, canvases, templates) without the process understanding, skills, or behaviours required to use them effectively
- The right tool applied at the wrong stage, or without the right facilitation, produces noise rather than insight
- Tool selection should always be guided by the question: "What do we need to learn or decide at this point, and what tool best supports that?"

### Strategic Analysis Tools
- **SWOT Analysis** -- strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats. Simple and widely understood, but often poorly executed. Most effective when informed by genuine evidence rather than assumptions.
- **PESTLE Analysis** -- political, economic, social, technological, legal, environmental factors. Useful for scanning the external environment and identifying forces that may create opportunities or constraints.
- **Porter's Five Forces** -- competitive analysis framework. Relevant where innovation intersects with market positioning and competitive strategy.
- **Business Model Canvas** -- a structured tool for mapping and testing the key components of a business model or value proposition. Particularly useful at the point where an innovation concept needs to be translated into a viable implementation plan.

### Problem-Framing Tools
- **Empathy Maps** -- structured capture of what users think, feel, say, and do. Supports the Empathise phase of Design Thinking.
- **"How Might We" Statements** -- reframing challenges as open questions that invite creative exploration without prescribing solutions.
- **5 Whys** -- iterative questioning to move from surface symptoms to root causes.
- **Stakeholder Maps** -- identifying who is affected by, involved in, or influential over the challenge being addressed.
- **Problem Statement Templates** -- structured formats for articulating the problem clearly enough that it can be acted upon.

### Ideation Tools
- **Brainstorming** (structured) -- time-bounded, rule-governed group ideation with deferred judgement and quantity emphasis.
- **Brainwriting / 6-3-5 Method** -- silent, written ideation that ensures every participant contributes without social dynamics filtering ideas prematurely.
- **SCAMPER** -- Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse. A structured provocation technique for generating variations on existing concepts.
- **Random Stimulus / Forced Association** -- using unrelated inputs to break habitual thinking patterns and generate unexpected connections.
- **Affinity Mapping** -- grouping and organising ideas by natural relationships to identify themes and patterns.

### Evaluation and Decision Tools
- **Impact/Effort Matrices** -- plotting ideas against likely impact and required effort to identify quick wins, strategic priorities, and resource sinks.
- **Decision Matrices** -- weighted criteria frameworks for comparing options systematically.
- **Assumption Mapping** -- identifying the critical assumptions underlying each option and prioritising which to test first.
- **Pre-mortem Analysis** -- imagining the initiative has failed and working backwards to identify the most likely causes.

### Digital and Emerging Tools
- **Collaborative Platforms** -- tools such as Miro, Mural, and FigJam that enable distributed teams to work together visually and in real time.
- **AI-Assisted Tools** -- large language models and generative AI tools that can support research, ideation, synthesis, and communication. These are emerging enablers with significant potential, but their effective use depends on the same literacy principles that govern all tools: understanding what they are good at, where they fall short, and how to evaluate their outputs critically.
- **Digital Prototyping Tools** -- ranging from simple wireframing tools to immersive technologies (VR/AR) that allow concepts to be experienced rather than merely described.
- **Data and Analytics Platforms** -- tools that support evidence-based decision-making by making relevant data accessible and interpretable.

### Principles for Tool Selection
1. **Purpose first** -- select the tool based on what you need to learn or decide, not based on what is fashionable or available
2. **Simplicity** -- use the simplest tool that achieves the objective. Complexity in tooling adds friction without necessarily adding value
3. **Accessibility** -- the tool should be usable by all relevant participants, not only specialists
4. **Integration** -- tools should connect to the broader process rather than existing in isolation

## Discussion Prompts
- Which of these tools are you already using? How effective are they in practice?
- Have you experienced a situation where a tool was adopted without sufficient understanding of how to use it? What happened?
- Where do you see the greatest potential for digital tools (including AI) to support your innovation work?

## Key Takeaways
1. Tools serve the process -- adopting tools without process understanding is a form of cargo culting
2. The innovation toolkit spans strategic analysis, problem framing, ideation, evaluation, and digital enablement
3. Tool selection should be guided by purpose, simplicity, accessibility, and integration
4. AI and digital platforms are emerging enablers with genuine potential, but their value depends on the literacy of the people using them
