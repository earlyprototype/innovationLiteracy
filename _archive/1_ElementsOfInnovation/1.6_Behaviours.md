# 1.6 Behaviours

## Purpose
Bridge the gap between mindset and action. Identify the specific, observable behaviours that translate innovation mindsets into productive practice -- with particular emphasis on active listening as a foundational innovation behaviour.

## Key Learning Points

### From Mindset to Behaviour
- Mindset without behaviour is aspiration; behaviour without mindset is performance
- The innovation behaviours outlined here are the observable, practicable expressions of the mindsets discussed in 1.5
- They are habits that can be developed through deliberate practice, reinforced by facilitation (see 1.8), and sustained by environment (see 1.9)
- Critically, they are *team* behaviours as much as individual ones -- they only produce their full value when practised collectively

### Core Innovation Behaviours

#### Active Listening
- Active listening is not simply hearing what someone says -- it is the deliberate, disciplined practice of receiving, processing, and integrating another person's perspective before responding
- It is a foundational innovation behaviour because:
  - **In the Empathise phase**: the quality of insight depends entirely on the quality of listening. Interviews, observations, and stakeholder conversations are only as valuable as the listener's ability to hear what is actually being said (and what is not being said)
  - **In Ideation**: building on others' ideas -- the engine of collaborative ideation -- requires first understanding those ideas fully. Premature evaluation, interruption, and selective hearing all undermine this
  - **In Testing and Evaluation**: feedback is only useful if it is genuinely heard. Confirmation bias -- hearing only what supports the existing idea -- is one of the most common evaluation failures
  - **In team dynamics**: active listening is the behavioural foundation of psychological safety. People contribute more freely when they believe they will be heard
- Active listening involves:
  - Full attention -- not formulating a response while the other person is still speaking
  - Clarifying questions -- confirming understanding before building on or responding to what was said
  - Paraphrasing -- reflecting back what was heard to confirm accuracy
  - Suspending judgement -- receiving the contribution fully before evaluating it
  - Attending to non-verbal cues -- tone, hesitation, body language
- It is a skill that can be practised and improved, and it benefits from being made explicit (naming it as a norm) rather than assumed

#### Constructive Challenge
- The practice of questioning ideas, assumptions, and proposals in a way that strengthens rather than undermines them
- Distinct from criticism (which evaluates) or disagreement (which opposes) -- constructive challenge asks "have we considered...?", "what if...?", "what evidence supports...?"
- It requires both courage (willingness to raise uncomfortable questions) and skill (framing challenges as contributions rather than attacks)
- Teams that lack constructive challenge fall victim to groupthink; teams that lack the skill to challenge constructively descend into conflict

#### Disciplined Experimentation
- The behaviour of testing ideas through structured, bounded experiments rather than debating them in the abstract
- "Let's test it" rather than "let's discuss it further"
- Includes: clearly defining what is being tested, how success and failure will be recognised, and what will be done with the results
- Disciplined experimentation is not recklessness -- it is a structured commitment to learning through action

#### Reflective Practice
- The habit of pausing, reviewing, and extracting learning from experience -- not just at project milestones but as a regular rhythm
- Includes both individual reflection (what did I observe, what did I learn, what would I do differently) and team reflection (structured retrospectives, learning capture sessions)
- Reflective practice converts experience into capability; without it, teams accumulate hours but not insight
- It is often the first behaviour to be dropped under time pressure, which is precisely when it is most needed

#### Sharing Work in Progress
- The willingness to make thinking visible before it is "finished" or "ready"
- Innovation processes depend on early, frequent feedback -- ideas that are kept private until polished miss the opportunity for iterative improvement
- This behaviour requires trust (see 1.7 on psychological safety) and explicit norms (making it clear that unfinished work is expected and welcomed)
- The corollary behaviour: responding to shared work-in-progress with curiosity and contribution, not evaluation

#### Building on Others' Ideas
- The practice of using others' contributions as starting points rather than always generating from scratch
- Expressed through language: "Yes, and..." rather than "No, but..."
- This is the behavioural mechanism through which cognitive diversity (see 1.7) produces value -- different perspectives only combine when people actively engage with perspectives that are not their own
- It requires active listening as a prerequisite

### Behaviours as Team Norms
- Individual behaviours are fragile; team norms are resilient
- The most effective approach is to establish these behaviours as explicit, shared expectations rather than hoping individuals will adopt them independently
- Facilitation (see 1.8) plays a critical role in establishing and maintaining these norms, particularly in the early stages when they are not yet habitual

## Discussion Prompts
- Which of these behaviours are already present in your team? Which are absent or inconsistent?
- When was the last time you felt genuinely listened to in a professional context? What made the difference?
- What prevents your team from sharing work in progress or challenging ideas constructively?

## Key Takeaways
1. Innovation behaviours are the observable, practicable bridge between mindset and productive action
2. Active listening is the foundational behaviour -- it underpins empathy, ideation, evaluation, and psychological safety
3. Constructive challenge, disciplined experimentation, reflective practice, sharing work in progress, and building on others' ideas complete the core set
4. These behaviours are most effective when established as explicit team norms, not left to individual initiative
