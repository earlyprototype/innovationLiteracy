# 1.2 Interventions

## Purpose
Introduce the concept of deliberate, structured interventions at key points in the innovation process. Demonstrate how targeted actions reduce risk, increase the likelihood of meaningful outcomes, and provide the discipline that separates productive innovation from undirected exploration.

## Key Learning Points

### What Is an Intervention?
- An intervention is a deliberate, structured action taken at a specific point in the innovation process to shape, test, or redirect outcomes
- Interventions are not improvised -- they are planned responses to the questions that arise at each stage of the process
- They range in scale from a 15-minute assumption-mapping exercise to a multi-week prototype deployment
- The common thread: they are purposeful, bounded, and designed to produce actionable information

### Types of Intervention Across the Innovation Process

#### Problem Framing Interventions
- Stakeholder interviews and empathy mapping
- "How Might We" question formulation
- Problem reframing exercises -- challenging initial assumptions about what the real problem is
- Root cause analysis and systems mapping
- The quality of problem framing determines the quality of everything that follows; these interventions are among the highest-value activities in the entire process

#### Opportunity Identification Interventions
- Environmental scanning and trend analysis
- Analogous domain exploration -- looking at how similar challenges have been addressed in unrelated fields
- Constraint mapping -- identifying what limits current approaches and where those limits might be shifted
- User journey mapping and pain point identification

#### Ideation Interventions
- Structured brainstorming with clear rules (defer judgement, build on others' ideas, aim for quantity)
- Brainwriting and silent ideation techniques (ensuring all voices are heard, not only the loudest)
- Provocation and random stimulus techniques to break habitual thinking patterns
- Cross-functional ideation sessions that deliberately bring diverse perspectives together

#### Prototyping and Validation Interventions
- Rapid prototyping -- building the simplest possible representation of an idea to make it tangible and testable
- "Try before you test" approaches -- deploying concepts in controlled, low-risk environments before committing to full implementation
- Staged commitment models -- a series of progressively larger investments, each gated by evidence from the previous stage
- Assumption testing -- identifying the critical assumptions that underpin an idea and designing specific experiments to validate or disprove them

#### Evaluation Interventions
- Structured feedback collection from users, stakeholders, and team members
- Decision matrices and prioritisation frameworks
- Pre-mortem analysis -- "if this initiative fails, what will have caused it?"
- Learning capture sessions -- ensuring that insights from each intervention are documented and accessible

### The Principle of De-risking
- Every intervention is fundamentally an act of de-risking: reducing uncertainty by generating evidence before committing resources
- The earlier interventions occur, the less expensive and less disruptive they are
- Staged commitment models embody this principle: rather than a single large investment decision, resources are committed incrementally as evidence accumulates
- This approach aligns naturally with "try before you test" programme models, where concepts are explored in contained environments before broader deployment

### Interventions vs Activities
- Not every activity in an innovation process is an intervention
- The distinction: an intervention is designed to answer a specific question or test a specific assumption; an activity may be useful but lacks this deliberate framing
- Reframing activities as interventions (by clarifying what question they are answering) significantly increases their value

## Discussion Prompts
- Which of these intervention types are already present in your work? Which are absent?
- Can you identify a situation where a well-timed intervention would have changed the outcome of a project?
- How does your organisation currently approach de-risking -- through staged commitment, or through large upfront decisions?

## Key Takeaways
1. Interventions are deliberate, structured actions designed to answer specific questions at specific points in the innovation process
2. They span the full process from problem framing through to evaluation, and each serves a distinct purpose
3. Every intervention is an act of de-risking: generating evidence before committing resources
4. The distinction between an intervention and an activity is intentionality -- knowing what question you are answering
