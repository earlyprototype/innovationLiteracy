# Fundamentals of Innovation Literacy - FXC Service - Creative Spark

## Introduction
    "Fundamentals of Innovation Literacy" workshop equips teams with the foundational skills to drive impactful change. Learn to effectively surface high-value ideas and de-risk process improvements, product development, or technology adoption through structured methods. This comprehensive one-day session demystifies the innovation journey, providing practical tools and fostering a collaborative mindset crucial for navigating change and succeeding within the Irish manufacturing context.

## Overview
This two-part, 3-hour per session workshop addresses the fundamental requirements for effective innovation. It operates at two levels: innovation as a *process* -- the structured, repeatable activity of generating and validating novel value -- and innovation *management* -- the organisational capability to direct, sustain, and build capacity in that process over time. Both are essential; neither is sufficient alone.

The workshop frames innovation as a creative, collaborative endeavour that is founded in best practice, and while integral to all sustainable organisations, is one that has distinct and often contradictory requirements from day-to-day operations. Across these sessions we explore the human elements, the processes, and the tools (including the emerging space of AI-assisted learning and innovation) that create novel, impactful, and lasting value-added change. We examine why 70% of innovation journeys end in failure and the enduring phenomenon of innovation 'cargo-culting' as a root cause -- before turning to the practical question of how to move from understanding innovation to embedding it as an organisational practice.

## Contents

### 0 - Introduction
Setting the foundation: establishing a shared language and understanding of innovation before exploring its constituent parts.

**0.1. What is innovation?**
Defining innovation as the deliberate creation of novel value -- distinguishing it from invention, improvement, and general change. We clarify the critical difference between innovation as a *process* (structured, repeatable, learnable) and innovation *management* (the organisational capability to sustain and direct that process over time).
- Process vs Management

**0.2. Why does innovation fail?**
Exploring why approximately 70% of innovation initiatives fail to deliver intended outcomes. We examine the inherent difficulty of navigating uncertainty, the widespread phenomenon of innovation 'cargo-culting' -- the adoption of innovation practices in form without substance, where organisations replicate the visible artefacts of innovative companies without understanding or implementing the underlying principles -- and the persistent myth of the lone innovator. The image of the solitary genius remains one of the most damaging misconceptions in innovation: in reality, meaningful innovation is a team-led process that depends on the integration of diverse perspectives, skills, and domains of expertise. Dismantling this myth early is essential, as it shapes expectations for everything that follows.
- Innovation is hard
- Cargo Culting
- The Myth of the Lone Innovator

**0.3. What do we mean by 'literacy'?**
Distinguishing literacy from expertise. Innovation literacy is not about becoming an innovation specialist -- it is about developing sufficient understanding of the full innovation landscape to participate effectively, ask the right questions, recognise good practice, and make informed decisions about where and how to invest effort.

**0.4. Why innovation literacy is not optional**
Most teams and individuals already possess elements of innovation literacy, whether through experience, instinct, or informal learning. The challenge is that partial literacy creates blind spots -- teams may excel at ideation but lack structured evaluation, or embrace experimentation without the frameworks to capture and scale what works. The objective is to complete the picture: developing what is latent, filling what is absent, and making the whole thing deliberate rather than accidental. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs data underscores the urgency -- the skills that underpin innovation literacy are among the fastest-growing competency requirements across industries globally.

### 1 - Elements of Innovation
A structured exploration of the core components that underpin effective innovation practice. Each element is examined both independently and in relation to the others, reflecting the reality that innovation capability is systemic -- no single element is sufficient on its own.

**1.1 Process**
Understanding innovation as a structured, repeatable process rather than a series of fortunate accidents. We focus on Design Thinking as a practical framework, exploring its phases and how they apply to real challenges in R&D, product development, and technology adoption. The emphasis is on process as a scaffold for creativity, not a constraint upon it. Central to this is the iterative nature of Design Thinking -- the expectation that ideas will be tested, will often fall short, and will be refined through repeated cycles of experimentation and feedback. Failure within this context is not a setback but data: each iteration narrows uncertainty and builds the evidence base for better decisions. We also address the question of measurement -- innovation effort should be evaluated by what is done, what is captured, and what is learned, not solely by outputs. Stage-appropriate measurement disciplines the process without distorting it, and guards against the common error of applying operational metrics to work that is fundamentally exploratory.

**1.2 Interventions**
The specific, deliberate actions taken at key points in the innovation process to shape outcomes -- from problem framing and opportunity identification through to prototyping and validation. We examine how structured interventions reduce risk and increase the likelihood of meaningful results, with particular attention to "try before you test" approaches and staged commitment models.

**1.3 Tools**
A practical survey of the traditional and digital tools available to support innovation work, including strategic analysis methods, problem-framing techniques, ideation approaches, and evaluation frameworks. We address the emerging role of AI and collaborative digital platforms, positioning them as enablers within a broader toolkit rather than solutions in themselves.

**1.4 Skills and Competencies**
Identifying the specific capabilities that effective innovation demands -- from analytical and creative thinking to communication, experimentation, and comfort with ambiguity. We draw on the World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs research to map these skills against broader workforce trends, and explore how they can be developed intentionally rather than left to chance.

**1.5 Mindset**
Exploring the cognitive orientations that distinguish effective innovators: curiosity, tolerance for uncertainty, willingness to challenge assumptions, and the ability to hold multiple perspectives simultaneously. We examine how mindset differs from personality -- it is cultivable, not fixed -- and how organisational culture either enables or inhibits the mindsets innovation requires.

**1.6 Behaviours**
Translating mindset into observable, practicable action. This section addresses the specific behaviours that support innovation -- constructive challenge, disciplined experimentation, reflective practice, and crucially, *active listening*. Active listening is examined not as a soft skill but as a foundational innovation behaviour: the ability to genuinely receive and integrate perspectives different from one's own is essential to every stage of the innovation process, from problem discovery through to evaluation.

**1.7 Teams**
Innovation is inherently collaborative, and the dynamics of how people work together are as important as individual capability. This section explores the characteristics of effective innovation teams: psychological safety, cognitive diversity, role clarity, and the productive tension between deep expertise and fresh perspective. We examine how teams can be composed, structured, and supported to maximise their collective innovation capacity.

**1.8 Facilitation**
The role of skilled facilitation in unlocking the potential of innovation processes and teams. Facilitation is presented not as a peripheral support function but as a critical enabler -- the means by which diverse perspectives are surfaced, constructive conflict is managed, and groups move from divergent exploration to convergent decision-making. Effective facilitation is also a learning function: it structures experiences through which participants build capability in real time, ensuring that innovation work develops the team's capacity as well as producing outcomes. We explore what effective facilitation looks like in practice and why it is often the missing ingredient in otherwise well-resourced innovation efforts.

**1.9 Environment**
The organisational and physical conditions that either support or undermine innovation. Innovation demands behaviours that are often directly at odds with the requirements of day-to-day operations -- experimentation over predictability, quantity over quality in early ideation, bias towards action over thorough analysis, tolerance for failure over risk avoidance. These contradictions cannot be resolved through willpower alone; they require deliberate boundaries. Dedicated time, distinct physical or virtual spaces, and explicit permission structures create the separation necessary for innovation behaviours to operate without colliding with operational norms. Without these boundaries, operational culture invariably wins -- not through malice, but through the sheer gravitational pull of the urgent over the important. Organisations that sustain innovation capability treat it as a managed discipline -- investing not only in individual projects but in the ongoing development of the conditions, structures, and learning systems that allow innovation to become repeatable rather than episodic. We examine how organisations can establish and protect these boundaries in practice, alongside the broader structural requirements of resources, leadership behaviours, and cultural conditions that allow innovation work to thrive.

### 2 - From Literacy to Practice
Understanding innovation is necessary but not sufficient. This section addresses the critical transition from knowing *about* innovation to embedding it as a sustained organisational practice. Without deliberate action to bridge this gap, workshop learning remains theoretical -- valuable in the moment but unlikely to produce lasting change.

**2.1 Permission Through Understanding**
Innovation cannot be sustained without informed support from leadership and the wider organisation. This is not about seeking permission to innovate in the abstract -- it is about building genuine comprehension among decision-makers of what innovation requires, why it demands different conditions from operations, and what realistic expectations look like at each stage. When leadership understands the process, they are better equipped to protect it: to defend time allocation, to tolerate productive ambiguity, and to resist the pressure to apply operational logic to exploratory work prematurely.

**2.2 Co-creating an Innovation Charter**
An innovation charter is the organisational agreement that defines how innovation will be approached, governed, and protected. Critically, it must be co-created rather than imposed -- engaging team members in its development builds ownership, surfaces practical constraints early, and ensures the charter reflects operational reality rather than aspirational abstraction. The charter should address: the scope and boundaries of innovation activity; how time and resources are allocated and protected; decision-making processes and stage gates; the behaviours and norms expected within innovation work; and how outcomes (including learning, not only outputs) will be captured and communicated.

**2.3 Demonstrating Value**
Sustaining stakeholder commitment requires an ongoing ability to articulate why innovation investment matters -- and to do so in terms that resonate with different audiences. The value of innovation extends well beyond direct outputs:
- **Capability value** -- the organisation can now do things it could not do before, from structured experimentation to rigorous problem framing.
- **Learning value** -- validated assumptions, disproven hypotheses, and mapped opportunities constitute strategic knowledge regardless of whether individual projects succeed.
- **Engagement and retention value** -- meaningful participation in innovation work drives higher engagement, stronger sense of purpose, and improved retention, particularly among high-capability team members.
- **Pipeline value** -- a portfolio of concepts and validated opportunities at various stages of maturity reduces dependence on any single initiative.
- **Strategic positioning value** -- visible innovation activity builds credibility, relationships, and presence in emerging spaces.

The discipline of demonstrating value requires matching evidence to audience: leadership may respond to pipeline and strategic positioning; team members to capability and engagement; external stakeholders and funders to learning and output metrics. The innovation charter should define not only what the organisation will do, but how it will articulate why it matters.
