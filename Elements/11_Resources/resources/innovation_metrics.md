# Innovation Metrics and Accounting

## Why It Matters
The quickest way to kill an innovation project is to measure it using operational metrics. Operations demand predictability, Return on Investment (ROI), and Net Present Value (NPV). Innovation deals with extreme uncertainty. If you demand a 5-year ROI projection for an idea in the Discovery phase, the team will simply fabricate the numbers to secure the budget, or worse, they will only propose safe, incremental ideas. 

## Key Takeaways
- **Different Metrics for Different Stages:** Early-stage innovation should be measured on *activity* and *learning* (e.g., "How many assumptions did we test this week?"). Only as an idea matures and uncertainty decreases should the metrics shift toward operational financials.
- **Learning as a Deliverable:** In innovation, acquiring data that disproves a hypothesis is a success, not a failure. Validated learning has explicit financial value: spending £5k on prototypes to prove a £500k idea won't work represents a £495k saving. 
- **Metered Funding:** Resources (time and budget) should not be granted as large, upfront lump sums based on a business case. They should be released in small tranches (metered funding) based on the evidence generated at each stage-gate of the process.

## Practical Application Tips
- **Do not promise early ROI:** When defending resource requirements to a sponsor during the early stages, promise "de-risking" rather than financial returns.
- **Ask for metered funding:** Ask for a specific, small amount of time and money to test the most critical assumption (e.g., "Give us two weeks and £500 for prototyping materials").
- **Frame evidence as the deliverable:** Make it clear that the initial investment will yield the evidence needed to decide whether the idea warrants a larger, subsequent investment, rather than a finished product.
