# Tool Selection Framework

## Core Principle: Fidelity Matching

Match the fidelity of the tool to the certainty of the idea.

| Stage | Certainty | Appropriate Fidelity | Examples |
|-------|-----------|---------------------|----------|
| **Empathise / Ideate** | Low | Low-fidelity | Paper, whiteboards, pipe cleaners, sticky notes |
| **Define** | Growing | Low-to-medium | Digital whiteboards, simple diagrams |
| **Prototype** | Medium | Medium | Digital mockups, slide prototypes, Wizard of Oz |
| **Test / Validate** | High | High | Functional prototypes, pilot systems |

## Why This Matters

- **High-fidelity too early** → premature convergence (nobody throws away a polished Figma design)
- **Low-fidelity too late** → insufficient rigour for proper evaluation
- Tool selection is an act of **process protection**

## Key Principles

**Tools Require Interventions:** A tool on its own does nothing. A digital whiteboard is useless without a structured intervention (e.g., "50 ideas in 15 minutes") driving the interaction.

**AI as Synthesis Engine:** In Define and Test phases, use AI not to *generate* the answers, but to synthesise large volumes of qualitative human data to spot patterns you might miss.

## Practical Application

- **Audit your toolkit:** Ensure tools match the stage you're in
- **Downgrade when diverging:** If the team is building polished Figma screens during Discovery, stop them — force paper sketches
- **Delay high-fidelity work:** Only permit polished tools once problem and concept are validated
