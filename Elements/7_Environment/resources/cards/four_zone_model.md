# The Four-Zone Model for Innovation Spaces

**Source:** Gesher School Makerspace Case Study (Planning Learning Spaces, Thom Conaty)

## Why It Matters

Innovation spaces that fail to zone for distinct cognitive activities force teams to do divergent, convergent, and reflective work in the same conditions. This creates cognitive friction where the environment should provide cognitive support.

## The Four Zones

| Zone | Purpose | Affordances |
|------|---------|------------|
| **1. Collaborative Hub** (Node) | Communication, alignment, team coordination | Open, visible, social |
| **2. Active Making Zone** (Factory) | Prototyping, building, tangible experimentation | Materials, tools at arm's reach |
| **3. Ideation Zone** (Design Thinking) | Whiteboards, divergent thinking, visual synthesis | Wall space, writable surfaces |
| **4. Reflective Retreat** (Reimagine) | Individual processing, research, recovery | Quiet, sensory-neutral |

## Key Principles

- **Visual Connection with Acoustic Separation:** Teams feel connected whilst protected from overload (e.g., sliding acoustic panels)
- **Sensory Regulation:** Neurodivergent members need sensory retreats — without this, loudest voices dominate
- **Tools at Arm's Reach:** Locked-away materials = abstract discussion. Visible tools = hands-on experimentation
- **Adaptable Boundaries:** Moveable partitions reconfigure as team moves through process stages

## Practical Application

- **Explicit permission through design:** Unfinished surfaces, writable walls, and modular furniture signal experimentation is expected
- **Match zone to phase:** Discovery in Ideation Zone, prototyping in Active Making, synthesis in Collaborative Hub, reflection in Retreat
