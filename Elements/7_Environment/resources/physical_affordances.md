# Physical Affordances and Making Space

## Why It Matters
Space is the "third teacher" in innovation. The physical environment dictates the cognitive behaviour of the people within it. You cannot expect teams to engage in highly divergent, creative, and collaborative behaviours if you place them in a traditional boardroom designed for sequential, hierarchical reporting. The environment must explicitly grant permission to act differently and actively support every phase of the innovation process.

## Key Takeaways
- **Posture Dictates Mindset (Make Space):** Sitting behind laptops encourages defensive, independent, and operational thinking. Standing up, moving around, and gathering at a whiteboard triggers collaborative, active, and exploratory cognitive modes.
- **Zoning for the Innovation Process:** Different cognitive tasks require distinct physical affordances. Effective spaces provide specific zones for distinct phases: a collaborative area for alignment, an active space for ideation and prototyping, a dedicated area for focused execution, and a quiet, sensory-friendly retreat for reflection. You cannot successfully reflect in a noisy manufacturing space, and you cannot freely ideate in a rigid boardroom.
- **Modularity Grants Permission:** Spaces that feel overly polished or immovable implicitly tell users "do not touch." Spaces that are highly modular (furniture on wheels), unfinished, or rough grant users implicit permission to hack, prototype, and break things.
- **Visual Thinking and Accessibility:** Innovation requires making thinking visible. If ideas only live in digital documents, they cannot be passively absorbed or serendipitously connected. Wall space is a critical innovation tool. Furthermore, having prototyping tools and materials clearly visible and at arm's reach lowers the barrier to entry—shifting the culture towards a direct "hands-on" approach.
- **Sensory Regulation for Psychological Safety:** Designing spaces with varying acoustic properties, lighting, and visual barriers isn't just an architectural flourish; it supports cognitive diversity. Providing a physical retreat accommodates different sensory needs and introverted thinking styles, ensuring that the most dominant voices don't dictate the process.

## Practical Application Tips
- **Abandon the boardroom:** Never conduct an innovation session in a standard meeting room if you can avoid it.
- **Hack the environment:** If you must use a standard room, radically alter it before the team arrives: move tables to the edges, remove chairs, and cover the walls in paper to create a physical "threshold" signaling different rules apply.
- **Design for duality:** When designing longer-term project spaces, ensure you have actively planned for both divergence (active, loud, visually stimulating areas) and convergence/reflection (quiet, focused, sensory-neutral areas).
