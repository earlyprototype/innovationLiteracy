# What are Skills and Competencies?

## Definition

Innovation skills are the identifiable, specific, and learnable capabilities that underpin effective innovation work. They can be developed through deliberate practice, structured experience, and reflection.

Organisations that treat innovation skills as innate rather than developable systematically underinvest in their own capability.

## Why It Matters

Innovation is often treated as if it depends on an indefinable quality - "creativity" or "vision" - that some people have and others do not. This is false. The skills that underpin effective innovation are identifiable and learnable.

These skills align closely with the fastest-growing competency requirements identified by the World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs research. Innovation literacy and workforce readiness are converging - organisations investing in innovation skills are simultaneously investing in broader workforce resilience and adaptability.

## How It Shows Up in Practice

**Analytical Thinking:** Ability to break complex problems into component parts, identify patterns, and evaluate evidence. Essential at every stage from problem framing through evaluation. Not the opposite of creative thinking - effective innovation requires both in alternation.

**Creative Thinking:** Ability to generate novel ideas, make unexpected connections, and challenge established patterns. Includes generative creativity (producing new ideas) and adaptive creativity (finding new applications for existing concepts). Constrained creativity - working within defined boundaries - is often more productive than unconstrained ideation.

**Communication:** Ability to articulate ideas clearly, listen actively, and adapt messaging to different audiences. Encompasses visual communication (sketching, diagramming, prototyping) as well as verbal and written. Innovation frequently fails not because the idea was poor but because it was poorly communicated to teams, leadership, or stakeholders.

**Experimentation:** Ability and willingness to design and run tests, gather evidence, and learn from results. Includes comfort with the possibility that experiment will disprove hypothesis. Distinct from "trial and error" - experimentation is structured, purposeful, and documented.

**Comfort with Ambiguity:** Ability to function effectively when outcomes are uncertain, information is incomplete, and path forward is unclear. Not a personality trait - a practised capability that develops through repeated exposure to ambiguous situations within supportive structures. Organisations demanding certainty at every stage will struggle to sustain innovation regardless of people's skills.

**Systems Thinking:** Cognitive capability to see interconnections, feedback loops, and leverage points rather than isolated problems and linear cause-effect. Emerges naturally during problem framing, failure analysis, and charter work. Named when observable, not taught explicitly in this workshop. Problem Space Map (homework) introduces systems thinking tool for self-paced exploration.

**Critical Evaluation:** Ability to assess ideas, evidence, and proposals rigorously and constructively. Includes distinguishing between personal preference and objective assessment, and between constructive challenge and destructive criticism. Essential at stage gates and decision points.

## In This Workshop

**Session 1 Morning:**  
Wallet Project requires all skills under pressure: analytical thinking (problem framing), creative thinking (ideation), communication (empathy interviews, prototype presentation), experimentation (testing phase).

Systems thinking named when it emerges: "You connected your partner's wallet to their broader life system - that's systems thinking as a skill."

**Session 1 Afternoon:**  
Failure analysis applies analytical and systems thinking to real-world examples. Intervention Speed-Dating develops tactical application skills. Problem Space Map homework introduces systems thinking tool.
