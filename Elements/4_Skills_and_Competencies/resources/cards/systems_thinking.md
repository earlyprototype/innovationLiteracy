# Systems Thinking and Systemic Design

## Why It Matters

Systems thinking enables teams to see interconnections, feedback loops, and wider impacts rather than isolated problems. Failure to think systemically leads to solutions that fix one problem whilst creating three others.

## Key Concepts

**Beyond the User:** Shifts from purely "user-centric" to "system-centric" design, integrating all stakeholders including environment, adjacent communities, and future generations.

**Feedback Loops:** Systems resist change through balancing loops (homeostasis) and amplify change through reinforcing loops (exponential growth or collapse).

**Leverage Points:** Not all interventions are equal. Small changes at high-leverage points produce disproportionate impact.

**Orientation and Zooming:** Seamlessly "zoom in" on immediate user needs and "zoom out" to broader systemic impact. Both perspectives are necessary; neither alone is sufficient.

**Emergence:** Complex systems exhibit properties unpredictable from component analysis alone. Test in context, not just controlled conditions.

## Practical Application

- **Map the ecosystem during Define:** Stakeholder maps, causal loop diagrams, ecosystem sketches before committing to solutions
- **Force wider perspective:**
  - "What are the second-order effects of this solution?"
  - "Who is not in the room who will be affected?"
  - "What system dynamics might resist this change?"
- **Identify feedback loops:** When problems persist despite intervention, assume a feedback loop is maintaining them
- **Test in context:** Prototype the system it operates within, not just the solution itself
