# Sharing Work in Progress

## Why It Matters
Innovation depends on early, frequent feedback. Ideas kept private until polished miss the opportunity for iterative improvement when course correction is still cheap. Yet most organisational cultures punish unfinished work - "don't bring me problems, bring me solutions" and "come back when it's ready" actively suppress the behaviour innovation requires.

The result: teams work in isolation until they've invested substantial effort, then present polished work that's too far developed to change direction without significant waste. By the time thinking is made visible, it's emotionally and politically difficult to pivot.

Sharing work in progress is not natural behaviour - it requires deliberate cultivation through explicit norms, leadership modelling, and environmental design that makes unfinished thinking visible and valued.

## Key Takeaways

**The Vulnerability Paradox:** Sharing incomplete work feels risky (exposing half-formed thinking to judgement) but is actually risk-reducing (catching flawed assumptions early when change is cheap). The courage required is front-loaded; the value accrues throughout the process.

**Permission Must Be Explicit:** Simply saying "it's okay to share unfinished work" is insufficient. Teams need explicit norms: "In this space, rough beats polished" or "10% thinking is welcome." Without clear permission, operational defaults (only share when ready) dominate.

**Visual Availability Transforms Collaboration:** Work visible on walls, shared boards, or open documents can be passively absorbed and serendipitously connected. Ideas trapped in individual notebooks or private files cannot be built upon. Making thinking visible isn't about status updates - it's about creating material for distributed cognition.

**Quality Improves Through Iteration, Not Isolation:** The belief that individuals should develop perfect thinking alone before sharing is false. Rough ideas subjected to feedback produce better outcomes than polished ideas developed in isolation. Iteration beats genius.

**Feedback Must Match Fidelity:** When someone shares work in progress, responding with "but what about..." or jumping to solution-mode shuts down the behaviour. Early-stage sharing requires generative feedback: "I noticed..." or "Have you considered..." or "This reminds me of..." Evaluative feedback comes later, when explicitly invited.

## Practical Application Tips

**Set Explicit Ground Rules:** At project start, establish norms: "We share work when it's 10% complete, not 90%" or "Unfinished work is welcome - we'll be explicit when we're ready for evaluation." Write these visibly. Reference them when violated.

**Model It From Leadership:** If senior people only share polished thinking, teams learn that's the standard. Leaders who visibly share half-formed ideas, ask for input on incomplete work, and demonstrate comfort with messiness give permission for others to do the same.

**Create Persistent Visible Workspaces:** Use wall space, digital boards, or shared documents where work-in-progress lives openly. Not for formal presentations - for ongoing visibility. Team members walking past can see what others are wrestling with and offer spontaneous connections.

**"Work in Progress" Reviews:** Schedule regular sessions (weekly?) where people share unfinished thinking explicitly for input, not evaluation. Make it clear the purpose is improvement, not assessment. "Here's where I'm stuck" or "I have three directions, which resonates?" becomes normal.

**Protect From Premature Evaluation:** When someone shares early-stage work and another person immediately evaluates it ("that won't work because..."), intervene: "We're in generative mode right now - let's hold evaluation for the convergent phase." This protects the behaviour from being punished.

**Normalise "I Don't Know Yet":** Innovation requires operating before you have answers. If "I don't know yet" is treated as failure or incompetence, people will hide uncertainty. Make it explicit that "I don't know yet, I'm exploring" is expected and valued at appropriate phases.

**Lower the Barrier:** If sharing requires booking rooms, preparing slides, or formal presentations, it won't happen. Make sharing frictionless: throw something on the wall, drop a sketch in the channel, share a quick voice note. Remove ceremony.
