# What is Mindset?

## Definition

Mindset refers to the habitual patterns of thinking, assumptions, and cognitive orientations that shape how a person approaches challenges, processes information, and responds to uncertainty.

Mindset is not personality. It is not fixed, innate, or immutable. Mindsets are shaped by experience, environment, and deliberate practice. The same person may exhibit different mindsets in different contexts - someone curious and exploratory in personal life may become risk-averse and convergent in work environments that penalise failure.

## Why It Matters

Innovation requires people to take risks, tolerate ambiguity, persist through failure, and challenge established assumptions. None of this happens without mindsets that support these behaviours.

Individual mindsets exist within - and are profoundly shaped by - organisational culture. An organisation that claims to value innovation but punishes failure, demands certainty, and rewards conformity will systematically suppress the mindsets innovation requires.

Cultivating innovation mindsets is therefore not solely an individual development challenge. It is an environmental design challenge. The most effective approach is to create conditions where innovation mindsets are the natural, rational response to the environment.

## How It Shows Up in Practice

**Curiosity:** Genuine orientation towards understanding. Not just "what?" but "why?" and "what if?". Drives the Discovery phase and sustains engagement through ambiguous early stages. Cultivated by creating space for exploration and modelling it in leadership behaviour. Suppressed by environments that reward only answers and penalise "unnecessary" questions.

**Tolerance for Uncertainty:** Ability to remain functional and engaged when outcome is unknown and path forward is unclear. Distinct from comfort with chaos - it is capacity to hold open questions without rushing to premature closure. Develops through repeated, supported exposure to uncertain situations where outcomes are not punitive.

**Willingness to Challenge Assumptions:** Readiness to question established beliefs, conventional wisdom, and "the way things have always been done". Includes self-challenge - questioning one's own assumptions, not only others'. Essential at Define and Test stages. Requires psychological safety to practise in team contexts.

**Perspective-Taking:** Ability to hold multiple perspectives simultaneously and genuinely consider viewpoints that differ from one's own. Cognitive (not emotional) capability - deliberate effort to understand how situation appears from another vantage point. Essential for problem framing, stakeholder engagement, and cross-functional collaboration.

**Growth Orientation:** Belief that capabilities develop through effort, practice, and learning - opposed to fixed orientation that treats ability as innate and unchangeable. Sustains engagement through setbacks and iterations. Reinforced by environments celebrating learning (including from failure).

**Bias Towards Action:** Preference for learning through doing rather than exhaustive analysis. Recognises that in uncertain environments, action generates information that analysis alone cannot. Must be paired with disciplined reflection to avoid activity without learning.
