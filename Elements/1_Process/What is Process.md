# What is Process?

## Definition

Process refers to a structured set of steps or boundaries that are useful for orientation during the larger innovation process. 

An interesting element of the processes that help structure innovation is that, although they do indicate sequence they are not necessarily linear and are gated rather than time bound (although this might be a real limitation in practice). Often failure to pass through a gate to the next process step indicates a need to return to an earlier step, thus creating an iterative, looping process that incrementally drives towards high quality outputs. This has the additional effect of removing the concept of 'failure' from the process, replacing it with a new, better informed process loop.

## Why It Matters

Engaging in process-led innovation enables:
 - Team alignment
 - Coherence and efficiency
 - Expectation setting
 - The ability to integrate appropriate interventions to support the process
 - Avoiding putting the horse before the cart (or solutions before problems, for example)

## How It Shows Up in Practice

We present two primary frameworks because they serve complementary purposes. Design Thinking provides the philosophical approach and the specific phases of human-centred problem solving, while the Double Diamond provides the cognitive map for *how* to think during those phases.

- **Design Thinking:** A human-centred, iterative methodology that shifts the starting point of innovation away from technical feasibility and toward human desirability. It is typically structured in five non-linear phases: Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test. It prevents teams from jumping straight to solutions.
- **Double Diamond:** Developed by the UK Design Council, this framework visually maps the process across two "diamonds": the Problem Space (Discover, Define) and the Solution Space (Develop, Deliver). It explicitly illustrates the necessity of alternating between divergent thinking (exploring widely) and convergent thinking (focusing and deciding) at each stage.
