# The Double Diamond Framework

## Why It Matters
The Double Diamond makes visible what most teams do wrong: they converge too soon, on the wrong problem, with insufficient exploration. The framework's genius is its simplicity - two diamonds, four phases, one critical insight: you must open up (diverge) before you close down (converge), and you must do this twice - once for the problem, once for the solution.

Teams that skip the first diamond produce elegant solutions to problems nobody has. Teams that skip divergence produce obvious solutions because they never explored alternatives. The framework provides both a map (where are we?) and permission structure (we're in divergence - evaluation is explicitly banned right now).

## Key Takeaways
- **Two Diamonds, Two Jobs:** First diamond (Discover, Define) exists to ensure you're solving the right problem. Second diamond (Develop, Deliver) exists to ensure you're solving it well. The space between them is the hinge - get the problem wrong and everything downstream is waste, no matter how brilliantly executed.
- **Diverge Before You Converge, Every Time:** You cannot evaluate options you haven't generated. You cannot select the best path if you've only considered one. Opening the diamond (divergence) generates the raw material; closing it (convergence) selects from that material. Do them simultaneously and you get neither - just premature commitment to the first acceptable idea.
- **The Visual Does the Work:** When a team argues about features before agreeing on the problem, you don't need to lecture them on process - you point at the diagram and ask: "Which diamond are we in? Are we trying to converge on a solution before we've agreed on the problem?" The framework's visual clarity makes dysfunction visible without blame.

## Practical Application Tips
- **Print it large, keep it visible:** The Double Diamond earns its place on the wall. When teams get lost, they can locate themselves visually. "We're here" (pointing at opening of first diamond) "not here" (pointing at closing of second) becomes sufficient correction.
- **Interrupt jumps between diamonds:** If someone proposes a specific feature (second diamond) during problem discovery (first diamond), name it: "That's a great solution - let's hold it. We're still in problem space." Write it down, park it visibly, return to exploration. Most solution-jumping is anxiety-driven; visible parking demonstrates the idea won't be lost.
- **Ban evaluation during divergence:** When the diamond is opening (Discover, Develop phases), the words "yes, but..." and "that won't work" are forbidden. Write "No buts" on a visible sign if required. Evaluation during divergence kills exploration before it produces anything worth evaluating.
