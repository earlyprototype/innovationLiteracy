# Design Thinking Framework

## Why It Matters
Design Thinking shifts the starting point of innovation away from "what's technically feasible" or "what would be profitable" and begins instead with "what do humans actually need?" This reordering matters because the most elegant technical solution is worthless if it solves the wrong problem, and the most profitable product in concept means nothing if nobody wants it.

The framework provides structure to prevent the most common innovation failure mode: jumping straight to solutions before understanding the problem. Every organisation believes it doesn't do this. Nearly every organisation does it constantly.

## Key Takeaways
- **Non-Linear by Design:** Presented as five phases (Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test) but deliberately iterative. New information at any stage sends you back to earlier phases. This isn't failure - it's the mechanism that produces quality. Teams that proceed linearly without looping back produce polished solutions to poorly understood problems.
- **Empathy as Foundation:** Empathy here is not sentiment - it's structured inquiry. Observing what people do, not what they say they do. Asking "why" repeatedly. Treating assumptions as hypotheses requiring evidence, not facts requiring confirmation.
- **Test to Learn, Not to Validate:** Prototypes exist to generate evidence, not to prove you were right. Teams that defend their prototypes during testing learn nothing. Teams that treat prototypes as disposable learn everything.

## Practical Application Tips
- **Name the phase explicitly:** At any given moment, the entire team should know which phase they're in and what that phase permits. "We're in Empathise - no solutions yet" becomes a sentence you'll repeat often.
- **Protect Discovery from operational pressure:** Operations demands solutions immediately. Design Thinking demands problem understanding first. Without explicit protection (governance, charters, leadership behaviour), operations wins by default through sheer gravitational pull. Defend the early phases or don't bother starting.
- **Use phases as evidence of progress:** When stakeholders ask "what have you accomplished?", the answer during Empathise is "we've gathered 20 hours of user observation" and during Define is "we've reframed the problem three times." Demonstrating systematic movement through phases is how you manage business expectations when you haven't yet produced The Solution.
