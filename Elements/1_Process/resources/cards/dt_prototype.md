# Prototype

## What Happens Here

You make the idea tangible enough to test. A prototype is not a product — it's a question rendered in physical or digital form. The purpose is to learn, not to impress.

**The discipline:** Match prototype fidelity to what you need to learn, not to what looks professional.

## Prototypes Are Questions

Every prototype should have a testable hypothesis behind it:

| Prototype | The Question It Asks |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Paper sketch of an interface | "Does this flow make sense to users?" |
| Cardboard model of a device | "Is this the right size and shape for this context?" |
| Role-played service interaction | "Does this process feel right from the user's perspective?" |
| Clickable wireframe | "Can users complete the key task without guidance?" |

If you can't articulate the question your prototype asks, you're building a demo, not a prototype.

## Fidelity Matching

**Low fidelity** (sketches, cardboard, role-play):
- Fast to make, cheap to discard
- Users critique the *idea*, not the execution
- Best for early-stage concept testing

**High fidelity** (coded prototypes, designed mockups):
- Slower to make, harder to discard
- Users critique the *execution*, not the idea
- Best for usability testing of validated concepts

**The mistake:** Going high-fidelity too early. Teams invest 3 days building a clickable prototype for an idea that a 20-minute paper prototype would have killed in testing.

## Time Constraints as a Feature

Parkinson's law applies: prototypes expand to fill the time available. Constrain deliberately:

- **15-minute prototypes** force ruthless prioritisation of what matters
- **"Prototype sprint"** — build 3 different prototypes of the same idea in 1 hour, test all three
- **Material constraints** (only paper, only Lego, only post-its) prevent over-investment

## Common Mistakes

- Falling in love with a prototype because it took effort to build
- Presenting prototypes *to* stakeholders instead of *testing them with* users
- Skipping prototype entirely because "we already know what we need to build"
- Building one prototype — always build at least two to prevent anchoring
