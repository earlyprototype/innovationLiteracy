# Ideate

## What Happens Here

You open the second diamond. With a clear problem statement from Define, you now generate as many potential solutions as possible before selecting any. This is pure divergence — volume and variety matter more than quality.

**The discipline:** Separate generation from evaluation. Completely. Not "mostly" — completely.

## The Rules of Divergence

1. **Defer judgement:** No evaluation during generation. "Yes, but..." is banned. "Yes, and..." is encouraged
2. **Go for quantity:** Set explicit targets. "We need 50 ideas in 20 minutes" changes behaviour more than "let's brainstorm"
3. **Build on others' ideas:** Use other people's ideas as springboards, not targets
4. **Encourage wild ideas:** The impractical idea often contains the seed of the practical breakthrough
5. **One conversation at a time:** Parallel conversations fragment energy

## From Generation to Selection

The transition from divergent to convergent is the most dangerous moment. Methods for doing it well:

- **Dot voting:** Quick, democratic, reveals collective intuition
- **Clustering:** Group similar ideas to see themes before choosing
- **Impact/Effort matrix:** Plot ideas on feasibility vs. desirability
- **"Dark horse" rule:** Always carry forward at least one idea that feels risky or unlikely — it often becomes the breakthrough

## The Quantity Problem

Teams consistently underestimate how many ideas they need. Research shows:

- The first 10 ideas are obvious — things everyone already knew
- Ideas 10-30 start getting interesting — combinations and variations
- Ideas 30+ enter unfamiliar territory — where genuine innovation lives

If you stopped at 10, you got a brainstorm. If you reached 50, you got ideation.

## Common Mistakes

- Evaluating during generation ("that would never work" kills the session)
- Anchoring on the first idea spoken (it sets the frame for everything after)
- Confusing quantity of *similar* ideas with genuine variety
- Running ideation without a clear problem statement from Define
