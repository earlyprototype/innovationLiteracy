# Design Thinking Framework

## Why It Matters

Design Thinking shifts the starting point of innovation away from "what's technically feasible" or "what would be profitable" and begins instead with "what do humans actually need?" The framework provides structure to prevent the most common innovation failure mode: jumping straight to solutions before understanding the problem.

Every organisation believes it doesn't do this. Nearly every organisation does it constantly.

## Key Principles

**Non-Linear by Design:** Presented as five phases (Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test) but deliberately iterative. New information at any stage sends you back to earlier phases. This isn't failure — it's the mechanism that produces quality. Teams that proceed linearly without looping back produce polished solutions to poorly understood problems.

**Empathy as Foundation:** Empathy here is not sentiment — it's structured inquiry. Observing what people do, not what they say they do. Asking "why" repeatedly. Treating assumptions as hypotheses requiring evidence.

**Test to Learn, Not to Validate:** Prototypes exist to generate evidence, not to prove you were right. Teams that defend their prototypes during testing learn nothing. Teams that treat prototypes as disposable learn everything.

## Practical Application

- **Name the phase explicitly:** "We're in Empathise — no solutions yet" becomes a sentence you'll repeat often
- **Protect Discovery from operational pressure:** Without explicit protection (governance, charters, leadership behaviour), operations wins by default through sheer gravitational pull
- **Use phases as evidence of progress:** "We've gathered 20 hours of user observation" is a valid deliverable during Empathise
