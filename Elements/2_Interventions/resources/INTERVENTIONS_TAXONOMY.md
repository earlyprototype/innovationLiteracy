# Interventions Taxonomy

## Definition

Interventions are stage-dependent structured methods that advance the innovation process. They are deliberate pattern interrupts - instructions or frameworks applied at specific moments to prevent common failure modes and move work forward more effectively.

**Key distinction:** Interventions are HOW you're instructed to use tools and engage with the process, not the tools themselves.

---

## Structure by Design Thinking Stage

### Discovery (Empathise)

**Purpose:** Understand people affected by the challenge - their needs, constraints, behaviours, context.

**Common failure modes:**
- Surface-level inquiry (stopping at first answer)
- Solution-jumping (designing before understanding)
- Assumption validation (seeking confirmation not discovery)
- Proxy user substitution (talking about users not with them)

**Interventions used in this stage:**

**Pattern interrupts:**
- "Dig deeper" instruction after first round (forces move beyond surface)
- "Ask 'why' five times" (root cause analysis)
- "Show me" not "tell me" (observational research prompt)
- Silence discipline (wait 5 seconds after answers)

**Structured methods:**
- Empathy interviews (scripted open-ended question sequences)
- Shadowing protocols (observation without interpretation)
- Journey mapping (experience documentation across touchpoints)
- Context immersion (spend time in user environment)

**Technology adoption specific:**
- Current state workflow mapping (document existing process before intervention)
- Pain point inventory (catalogue friction in current technology/process)
- Workaround documentation (what people do when systems fail them)
- Adjacent technology audit (what tools already in use, how adopted)

**Further exploration:**

- PipDecks Storyteller deck (narrative extraction): https://www.pipdecks.com/
- d.school bootcamp bootleg: https://dschool.stanford.edu/resources/the-bootcamp-bootleg

### Define (Frame)

**Purpose:** Synthesise discovery findings into clear, actionable problem statement.

**Common failure modes:**
- Problem/solution confusion (framing solution as problem)
- Too broad (everything is the problem)
- Too narrow (missing system context)
- Stakeholder misalignment (different people solving different problems)

**Interventions used in this stage:**

**Pattern interrupts:**
- "Reframe that as a question" (linguistic forcing function)
- "Remove your solution from that problem statement" (catch solution-jumping)
- "What's the problem behind that problem?" (move upstream)

**Structured methods:**
- How Might We statements (problem reframing as possibility)
- Point of View statements (user + need + insight)
- Problem space mapping (system context visualisation)
- Five Whys (root cause identification)
- Assumption inventory (document what must be true)

**Technology adoption specific:**
- As-Is vs To-Be mapping (current state → desired state)
- Adoption barrier analysis (why current tech/process persists despite pain)
- Change readiness assessment (organisational capacity to absorb new technology)
- Value proposition framing (benefit articulation for different user groups)

**Further exploration:**
- d.school "Map the Problem Space" tool: https://dschool.stanford.edu/tools/map-the-problem-space
- PipDecks Problem Solving deck: https://www.pipdecks.com/
- Stanford d.school "Reframe the Problem" methods: https://dschool.stanford.edu/resources

### Ideate (Generate)

**Purpose:** Generate wide range of potential solutions. Quantity, diversity, deferred judgement.

**Common failure modes:**
- Premature convergence (first "good enough" idea wins)
- Evaluation during generation (convergent behaviour in divergent phase)
- Expert dominance (senior/loud voices prevail)
- Incremental thinking (only small improvements on current state)

**Interventions used in this stage:**

**Pattern interrupts:**
- "Don't explain, don't defend, don't evaluate" (suspend judgement)
- "Bad ideas welcome" (permission to think freely)
- "Build on that" not "but..." (additive thinking)
- Quantity targets ("50 ideas in 15 minutes")
- Silence requirements (individual writing before group sharing)

**Structured methods:**
- Brainstorming protocols (structured turn-taking)
- SCAMPER (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse)
- Analogous inspiration (how do other domains solve this?)
- Worst possible idea (reverse thinking to break constraints)
- Forced connections (combine random elements)

**Technology adoption specific:**
- Technology scouting (what exists that could be applied?)
- Pilot scenario generation (how could we test this at small scale?)
- Implementation pathway options (different routes to same outcome)
- Hybrid solution exploration (blend new/existing technology)

**Further exploration:**
- IDEO brainstorming methods: https://www.designkit.org/methods.html
- PipDecks Idea Tactics deck: https://www.pipdecks.com/
- Gamestorming book (Gray, Brown, Macanufo): https://gamestorming.com/ and https://davegray.com/gamestorming/

### Prototype (Build)

**Purpose:** Make thinking tangible and testable. Prototypes are questions, not answers.

**Common failure modes:**
- Over-production (building too complete, too early)
- Attachment to solution (defending rather than testing)
- Perfectionism (aesthetic over function)
- Analysis paralysis (planning instead of making)

**Interventions used in this stage:**

**Pattern interrupts:**
- "Build it in 7 minutes" (time constraint forces simplicity)
- "It should break" (permission for rough)
- "What's the riskiest assumption?" (focus prototype on uncertainty)
- Material constraint ("use only paper and tape")

**Structured methods:**
- Fidelity spectrum (paper → digital → functional)
- Experience prototyping (simulate the interaction)
- Wizard of Oz (manual backend, polished frontend)
- Storyboarding (sequence the experience)
- Landing page tests (value proposition validation)

**Technology adoption specific:**
- Process mockups (show new workflow without building system)
- Data structure prototypes (information architecture before development)
- Integration pathways (map how new tech connects to existing)
- Training simulation (test adoption support before deployment)
- Pilot scoping (define smallest testable implementation)

**Further exploration:**
- IDEO prototyping methods: https://www.designkit.org/methods.html
- PipDecks Prototype deck: https://www.pipdecks.com/
- "Sprint" book (Knapp, Zeratsky, Kowitz) - Google Ventures method: https://www.thesprintbook.com/book and https://www.gv.com/sprint/

### Test (Validate)

**Purpose:** Put prototypes in front of real users, gather feedback, learn. Testing is investigation, not validation.

**Common failure modes:**
- Confirmation bias (seeking validation not learning)
- Defensive posture (explaining rather than observing)
- Unstructured feedback (no plan for what to learn)
- Missing documentation (insights lost immediately)

**Interventions used in this stage:**

**Pattern interrupts:**
- "Don't defend your prototype" (observe, don't justify)
- "Ask 'what did you notice?' not 'did you like it?'" (observation over opinion)
- "What surprised you?" (catch unexpected responses)
- Silent observation protocols (watch behaviour not just words)

**Structured methods:**
- Feedback capture templates (structured observation recording)
- Think-aloud protocols (users narrate their thinking)
- A/B testing frameworks (compare alternatives systematically)
- Success/failure criteria (define learning targets before testing)
- Iteration planning (what changes based on what feedback?)

**Technology adoption specific:**
- Pilot user selection (representative but early-adopter mindset)
- Training effectiveness testing (can users actually use the new technology?)
- Workflow integration assessment (does it fit existing work patterns?)
- Support requirement identification (what help do users need?)
- Resistance pattern documentation (why do users reject/workaround?)

**Further exploration:**
- IDEO testing methods: https://www.designkit.org/methods.html
- "Testing with Humans" - Kowitz & Knapp (part of Sprint method): https://www.thesprintbook.com/
- PipDecks User Research deck: https://www.pipdecks.com/

## Cross-Stage Interventions

These apply across multiple stages or at transition points:

**Diverge/Converge Management:**
- 2x2 prioritisation matrices (converge on best options)
- Dot voting (democratic convergence)
- "Yes, and..." vs "Yes, but..." (divergent vs convergent language)
- Parking lot (defer off-topic valuable ideas)

**Reflection and Learning Capture:**
- Start/Stop/Continue retrospectives
- What worked, what didn't, what surprised (structured reflection)
- Learning documentation templates (capture insights systematically)
- "What would you do differently?" (forward-looking learning)

**Stakeholder Engagement:**
- Stakeholder mapping (identify who matters, why, when)
- Communication adaptation (tailor message to audience)
- Show-and-tell sessions (make work visible incrementally)
- Permission and feedback loops (check alignment continuously)

**Technology Adoption Specific:**
- Change impact assessment (who's affected, how, preparation needed)
- Adoption pathway planning (phased rollout vs big bang)
- Champion identification (early adopters who can support others)
- Resistance anticipation (map likely objections, prepare responses)

## Toolset Resources

**PipDecks** - https://www.pipdecks.com/
Physical/digital card decks with structured methods for each stage:
- Storyteller (discovery and narrative)
- Problem Solving (define and frame)
- Idea Tactics (ideation)
- Prototype (build and test)
- User Research (validate)

**Gamestorming** by Gray, Brown, and Macanufo
- Website: https://gamestorming.com/
- Book resources: https://davegray.com/gamestorming/
Comprehensive methods library spanning all stages. Particularly strong on visual thinking and group facilitation techniques. 80+ games organized by category (opening, fresh thinking, planning, design, team-building, strategy, problem-solving).

**IDEO Design Kit** - https://www.designkit.org/
Free resource with 50+ methods across discovery, ideation, and implementation. Human-centred design focus. Includes comprehensive field guide, courses, and facilitator resources.

**d.school Method Cards and Resources** - https://dschool.stanford.edu/resources
Curated collection from Stanford d.school. Particularly strong on reframing and synthesis.
- Bootcamp Bootleg: https://dschool.stanford.edu/resources/the-bootcamp-bootleg
- Design Thinking tools: https://dschool.stanford.edu/resources

**Strategyzer Tools** - https://www.strategyzer.com/
Business model and value proposition design. Useful for Define and Test stages when connecting innovation to organisational strategy.
- Business Model Canvas: https://www.strategyzer.com/canvas/business-model-canvas
- Value Proposition Canvas and other strategy tools available

**Sprint Method** (Google Ventures) - https://www.thesprintbook.com/ and https://www.gv.com/sprint/
5-day process for solving big problems and testing new ideas by Knapp, Zeratsky, and Kowitz. Particularly strong on rapid prototyping and validation.

**Technology Adoption Frameworks:**
- **Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations**: Theory of how innovations spread through social systems, adoption curve (innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards), and five attributes driving adoption speed. Key academic reference for technology adoption patterns.
  
- **Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)**: Information systems theory explaining user acceptance through perceived usefulness and perceived ease-of-use. Extended through TAM2 and UTAUT models. Developed by Davis and Venkatesh.
  
- **Kotter's 8-Step Change Management**: https://www.kotterinc.com/methodology/8-steps/
  Structured process for organizational transformation. Steps include creating urgency, building coalition, forming vision, enlisting volunteers, removing barriers, generating wins, sustaining acceleration, and instituting change.
  
- **Lean Change Management**: https://leanpub.com/leanchangemanagement and https://blog.leanchange.org/
  Agile approach to organizational change by Jason Little. Uses feedback-driven methods adapting to people's reactions. Combines Lean Startup, Agile, and neuroscience principles. Emphasizes co-creation and experimentation.

## Design Notes

Interventions are consistently undervalued in innovation training. Teams receive process maps (Design Thinking phases) and tools (Miro, prototyping materials) but no instruction in HOW to move through the process effectively.

Result: groups stall in empathy (surface-level inquiry), jump to solutions (weak problem framing), produce low-quality ideation (first acceptable idea wins), over-invest in prototypes (perfectionism), and extract no learning from testing (confirmation bias).

Interventions are the active ingredient that makes process work. They're small, deliberate, and stage-specific. "Dig deeper" is three words but changes the entire quality of discovery. "Don't defend your prototype" restructures testing from validation-seeking to learning-focused.

Most effective interventions are linguistic or temporal:
- Linguistic: question framing, instruction phrasing, conversational rules
- Temporal: time constraints, silence requirements, sequencing

Physical interventions (materials, space configuration) typically belong to Tools (Element 3) or Environment (Element 7).
