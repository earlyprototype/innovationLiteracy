# Pattern Interrupts: The Micro-Interventions That Change Behaviour

## Why It Matters
Pattern interrupts are the smallest, most tactical form of intervention - single phrases or instructions that disrupt default behaviours in the moment they're about to occur. They're the "dig deeper" after a first answer, the "don't defend your prototype" before testing begins, the "build on that" instead of "yes, but..." during ideation.

These micro-interventions appear trivial - just a few words - but they're the mechanism through which process actually changes behaviour. Without them, teams know intellectually what good innovation practice looks like but default to habitual patterns under pressure: surface-level inquiry, solution-jumping, premature evaluation, defensive positioning.

Pattern interrupts work because they're applied at the exact moment behaviour is forming, before it solidifies. They're the tactical implementation layer beneath strategic process frameworks.

## Key Takeaways

**"Dig Deeper" (Discovery Phase)**
Applied after: First-level answers in user research ("I need it to be faster")
Effect: Prevents stopping at surface symptoms. Forces inquiry toward root causes.
Why it works: Most initial answers are rehearsed, socially acceptable responses. Actual needs live beneath the surface. "Why is speed important? What would faster enable you to do?" unlocks real insight.

**"Ask Why Five Times" (Discovery/Define Phases)**  
Applied during: Problem analysis, root cause exploration
Effect: Moves from symptoms to systemic causes through recursive questioning.
Why it works: Each "why?" moves one level deeper in causality. Surface problem: "Users abandon checkout." Three whys later: "We've optimised for new user acquisition but created friction for returning customers." Different problem, different solution.

**"Don't Explain, Don't Defend, Don't Evaluate" (Ideate Phase)**
Applied during: Divergent ideation when teams start justifying or critiquing ideas
Effect: Protects generative thinking from evaluative shutdown.
Why it works: Explaining activates defence mechanisms. Evaluation triggers convergent thinking prematurely. Banning both keeps the team in genuine exploration mode.

**"Build on That" (Ideate Phase)**
Applied when: Someone is about to say "yes, but..." to another's idea
Effect: Forces additive rather than subtractive contribution.
Why it works: "Yes, and..." thinking compounds ideas. "Yes, but..." kills them. Structurally forcing the additive pattern produces better ideation outcomes even when people don't naturally think that way.

**"Show Me, Don't Tell Me" (Discovery Phase)**
Applied during: User research when participants describe behaviour verbally
Effect: Shifts from self-reported behaviour (unreliable) to observed behaviour (evidence).
Why it works: People are poor at accurately reporting what they do. They report what they think they do, or what they wish they did, or what sounds socially acceptable. Observation reveals actual behaviour.

**"What's the Riskiest Assumption?" (Prototype Phase)**
Applied when: Teams are building comprehensive prototypes testing everything
Effect: Focuses prototyping on highest-uncertainty elements only.
Why it works: Prototyping everything is expensive and slow. Identifying and testing ONLY the riskiest assumption produces maximum learning per unit of effort. Everything else remains assumption until this is validated.

**"Don't Defend Your Prototype" (Test Phase)**
Applied before: Testing sessions when creators are present
Effect: Prevents teams from explaining, justifying, or rescuing prototypes during user testing.
Why it works: If creators intervene during testing, they learn how the prototype works *with expert support*, not how it works in reality. Silence forces honest observation of user struggle - which is where learning happens.

**"It Should Break" (Prototype Phase)**
Applied when: Teams are over-investing in prototype fidelity and polish
Effect: Gives explicit permission for low-fidelity, disposable prototyping.
Why it works: If prototypes are too polished, teams become attached and resist learning that contradicts the design. "It should break" reframes prototypes as learning tools, not creations to protect.

## Practical Application Tips

**Introduce Pattern Interrupts as Explicit Rules:** Don't assume teams will naturally adopt these phrases. Write them visibly: "Rules for this phase: Don't explain, don't defend, don't evaluate."

**Intervene Immediately When Violated:** Pattern interrupts only work if enforced. When someone starts explaining during ideation, interrupt gently: "Remember our rule - no explanations yet, just ideas." Consistency matters more than politeness.

**Name Why They Matter:** Don't just instruct, explain briefly: "We're using 'dig deeper' because first answers are usually surface-level. The insight is underneath."

**Model Them in Facilitation:** Use the language yourself. "I'm going to ask why five times now..." or "I'm building on your idea: what if we..."

**Rotate Enforcement:** Designate different team members to watch for pattern violations. "Your job today is to catch anyone who says 'yes, but' and remind them to try 'yes, and'." Makes the entire team responsible for protecting the pattern.

**Debrief Why They Worked:** After a session using pattern interrupts, reflect: "What changed when we used 'don't defend your prototype'?" Making the mechanism visible helps teams internalise the principle, not just the tactic.

**Adapt to Context:** These are examples, not exhaustive. Create pattern interrupts for your specific failure modes. If your team rushes to convergence, "We need 20 more ideas before we evaluate" becomes your pattern interrupt.
