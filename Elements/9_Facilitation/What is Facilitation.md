# What is Facilitation?

## Definition

Facilitation is the practice of guiding a group through a process, enabling participation, managing dynamics, and ensuring that the process achieves its intended outcomes.

In innovation, facilitation is not optional - it is the mechanism through which diverse perspectives are surfaced, innovation behaviours are established and maintained, the process stays on track without becoming rigid, conflict is managed productively, and learning is captured and made explicit.

Facilitation is the synthesis capability that orchestrates all other elements.

## Why It Matters

Innovation processes without facilitation tend to default to familiar patterns: dominant voices prevail, convergence happens prematurely, the group reverts to operational modes of thinking, and outcomes go undocumented.

Even the best processes and most capable participants underperform without skilled facilitation, particularly in early stages when norms are being established and cognitive diversity is producing friction.

The facilitation gap - the assumption that groups can self-facilitate - is one of the most common reasons innovation efforts underperform.

## How It Shows Up in Practice

**Before the session:**
- Clear purpose (defined objective participants understand)
- Structured agenda (planned sequence with clear transitions, time allocations, intended outcomes)
- Participant preparation (context provided, expectations clear)
- Environment setup (space configured to support work)

**During the session:**
- Managing energy and attention (reading the room, adjusting pace)
- Equalising participation (round-robins, silent writing, small group work, structured turn-taking)
- Holding the process (keeping group in right mode at right time - divergent when diverging, convergent when converging)
- Managing dynamics (addressing dominance, drawing out quiet participants, navigating disagreement, maintaining psychological safety)
- Making thinking visible (capturing ideas, decisions, questions in real time)
- Asking, not telling (primary tool is the question)

**After the session:**
- Capturing outcomes (documenting what was produced, decided, learned)
- Identifying next steps (connecting outputs to subsequent actions)
- Reflective review (what worked, what didn't, for both content and process)

**Stage-specific approaches:**
- Discovery/Define: focus on inquiry, deep listening, synthesis
- Ideate: focus on energy, volume, deferred judgement, equalising participation
- Prototype/Test: focus on structure, feedback capture, maintaining objectivity
- Evaluation/Decision: focus on rigour, ensuring all perspectives considered, managing convergence
