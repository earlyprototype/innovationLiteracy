# What is Governance?

## Definition

Governance is the bidirectional communication infrastructure between innovation practice and the broader organisation. It mediates the shared organisational purpose inward to practitioners and enables legitimate articulation of innovation requirements outward to the organisation.

Governance provides insulation for innovation practice from operational expectations through formal organisational agreement. It is the bridge between Elements 1-9 (innovation practice) and the rest of the organisation.

## Why It Matters

Even the most capable innovation team, with the best processes and tools, will fail if the organisation around them does not understand or support what they are doing.

The most common pattern: leadership endorses innovation enthusiastically, then systematically undermines it by reclaiming time, demanding premature results, or penalising productive failure. This happens because decision-makers lack sufficient understanding of how innovation actually works.

Without governance infrastructure, innovation operates on goodwill and enthusiasm - which are not sustainable organisational resources. Governance creates the formal mechanisms and shared understanding that allow innovation to survive contact with operational reality.

Innovation requires people to take risks, tolerate ambiguity, persist through failure. None of this happens without compelling answer to "why am I doing this?" Governance is how organisations create and sustain that answer.

## How It Shows Up in Practice

**Inward Flow (Organisation → Innovation Practitioners):**
- Shared understanding of organisational purpose and strategic objectives
- Legitimacy and permission to operate differently from operations
- Alignment with broader mission
- Clear connection between innovation work and organisational why

**Outward Flow (Innovation Requirements → Organisation):**
- Evidence of value and contribution to shared mission (using Elements 1-9 as evidence base)
- Articulation of what innovation needs to function (temporal, physical, resource requirements)
- Demonstration of alignment between innovation work and organisational objectives
- Documentation of learning and capability development

**Multi-directional Function:**  
Not just vertical (leadership to practitioners) but lateral (across teams, roles, departments). Enables discussions transversely across different work cultures without solving underlying siloing or interpersonal issues.

**Formal Mechanisms:**

**Innovation Charter:** Written, agreed document defining terms under which innovation operates. Provides clarity (everyone understands scope and expectations), protection (reference point when operational pressures threaten), accountability (organisational commitments harder to retroactively withdraw), and alignment (connects innovation to organisational objectives).

Charter should address: scope and boundaries, time and resources, decision-making and governance, behaviours and norms, outcome capture and communication.

Charter is living document reviewed regularly (quarterly or biannually) as innovation maturity evolves. Co-creation essential - participation builds ownership, operational realism, shared language, and early buy-in.

**Decision Rights:** Clear authority at each stage of innovation process. Who sets priorities, who has decision-making power at stage gates, how are stop/pivot decisions handled, what criteria are used for evaluation.

**Permission Structures:** Explicit, communicated agreements about what is sanctioned within innovation work. Includes permission to experiment (and to fail), permission to challenge assumptions (including those of senior leaders), permission to spend time on exploration that may not produce immediate results.

**Leadership Understanding:** Decision-makers who genuinely comprehend what innovation demands day-to-day. Built through participation (involving leadership in process as contributors), communication in their language (strategic objectives, risk management, organisational resilience), realistic expectation-setting (agree on success criteria at each stage), and reporting on learning not just outputs.

**Middle Management Engagement:** Understanding gap often most acute at middle management level. Senior leadership may endorse strategically, front-line teams may be enthusiastic, but middle managers reconcile innovation time with operational delivery targets. Without explicit engagement, innovation permission granted from above is filtered out before reaching teams. Middle managers who understand innovation become its most effective protectors.
