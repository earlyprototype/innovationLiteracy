# Innovation Charters and Boundaries

## Why It Matters
Innovation and daily operations operate under fundamentally opposed paradigms. Operations seek to reduce variance and maximise efficiency; innovation seeks to explore variance and tolerate inefficiency to find new value. Without explicit governance—a shared agreement on the rules of engagement—the gravitational pull of operational demands will always crush innovation work. 

## Key Takeaways
- **The Innovation Charter:** A formal, written agreement between the innovation team and leadership. It defines the scope of exploration, the protected resources (time/budget), and crucially, the metrics of success (which must be learning-based, not ROI-based in the early stages).
- **Shedding Authority at the Door:** When senior leadership enters an innovation space (e.g., during a prototype review), they must consciously shed their operational authority. If they act as a traditional "decision-maker," they will inadvertently shut down psychological safety. They must act as a "curious contributor."
- **Temporal Ring-Fencing:** "Innovation on Friday afternoons" fails because it is the first thing sacrificed when operational deadlines loom. Governance requires officially ring-fencing time that cannot be reallocated by middle management.

## Practical Application Tips
- **Co-create your charter:** Before launching an innovation sprint, develop an Innovation Charter with the project sponsor and explicitly agree on what a "productive failure" looks like.
- **Ring-fence time:** Formalise an agreement on how the team's time will be protected from the demands of their line managers.
- **Brief sponsors before reviews:** When a sponsor joins a session, remind them: "Your role today is not to evaluate whether this will make money, but to ask questions that help the team see their blind spots."
