## Core Pedagogical Approach

**Principle:** Collapse theory and practice. Participants learn by doing, not by listening. All conceptual frameworks are handed off AFTER participants have experienced the need for them.

**Method:** Point to things, indicate where they sit in the framework, contextualise through experiential learning. This is foundations work - not mastery, but sufficient literacy to recognise the landscape, know what matters, and build capability through practice.

**Structure:** Series of focused, experiential interventions (IDEO-style micro-cycles) rather than one sustained design challenge. Trivial topics (wallets, gift-giving) keep cognitive load low so participants can focus on process, not content.

**Content positioning:** All theoretical material becomes framework handoffs (1-2 page cards at strategic moments) and post-workshop reference pack. Slides are visual anchors and provocation triggers, not information delivery vehicles.

**Core philosophy:**  
This is not a lecture series about innovation. Participants don't need more information transfer - they need muscle memory. The workshop embodies the process being taught. Each section has: provocative anchor, hands-on intervention, structured reflection, framework handoff (in that order). Theory handed off after participants experience the need for it.

**Learning mechanism:**  
Participants experience all elements in compressed cycles, then spend remaining time pointing: "That moment when you struggled to frame the problem? Process. That instruction to 'dig deeper'? Intervention. The Miro board? Tool. Your frustration when your partner didn't listen? Behaviour. The way tight timing helped rather than hurt? Environment."

They'll remember what empathy feels like when done poorly vs well, the cognitive shift when problems are properly framed, the chaos-to-clarity of good facilitation. That's transferable capability, not abstract knowledge.

## The Framework: 11 Elements of Innovation Literacy

### The What (Elements 1-3)

**1. Process**  
Innovation as structured, repeatable activity. Design Thinking framework as primary model (Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test).

**2. Interventions**  
Stage-dependent structured mini-processes that help participants move to the next stage more effectively. Deliberate pattern interrupts at key moments.

**Examples from IDEO exercises:**
- False-start (interrupt assumption-driven design)
- "Dig deeper" instruction after first empathy round (interrupt surface-level inquiry)
- Forced iteration after feedback (interrupt "first idea is best" thinking)
- "Don't defend your prototype" instruction (interrupt attachment to solutions)
- "Build your solution in 7 minutes" (timed prototyping constraint)

**Tactical methods/frameworks:**
- How Might We statements (linguistic framing)
- 2x2 prioritisation matrices (decision framework)
- Assumption mapping (structured thinking method)
- SWOT analysis, stakeholder mapping
- "Ask ChatGPT 'What makes this art interesting?'" (An Other Point of View exercise structure)

**Key distinction:** Interventions are HOW you're instructed to use tools, not the tools themselves. Example: Miro board = Tool; "Draw your ideal innovation process in 3 minutes" = Intervention.

**3. Tools**  
Physical objects and technologies that enable innovation work. Includes: Miro/Mural, AI assistants (ChatGPT for ideation/observation), Kanban boards, prototyping materials (paper, pens, tape, scissors), sticky notes.

**Key distinction:** Tools are WHAT you use. Document-based "toolkits" and method guides are signposted in relevant elements' resources, not treated as tools.

### The How (Elements 4-9)

**4. Skills and Competencies**  
Analytical thinking, creative thinking, communication, experimentation, comfort with ambiguity, **systems thinking**.

Systems thinking positioned as cognitive capability to see interconnections, feedback loops, leverage points. Emerges naturally during problem framing, failure analysis, and charter work. Named when observable, not taught explicitly.

**5. Mindset**  
Curiosity, tolerance for uncertainty, challenging assumptions, holding multiple perspectives. Cognitive orientations that enable innovation behaviours.

**6. Behaviours**  
Active listening, constructive challenge, disciplined experimentation, reflective practice. Observable actions that support innovation work.

**7. Environment**  
Physical spaces, temporal boundaries, and organisational conditions that protect innovation behaviours from operational gravity. Includes: dedicated spaces, protected time allocations, cultural norms that tolerate ambiguity/iteration, boundaries against operational interruptions.

**Key distinction:** Environment (Element 7) focuses on conditions and container. Governance (Element 10) addresses formal mechanisms including permission structures, charters, and leadership understanding.

**8. Collaboration**  
Effective teamwork and creative collaboration. Includes: psychological safety, cognitive diversity, role clarity, productive tension, distributed cognition, constructive conflict that advances thinking.

**Not identity definition:** Collaboration is interpersonal competence, not team composition. Role architecture (functional archetypes like problem owner, subject matter expert, champion) belongs in advanced innovation management contexts, not foundations work. Role clarity within Collaboration covers dynamic role negotiation during innovation work.

**9. Facilitation**  
Unlocking potential of processes and collaboration, managing constructive conflict, enabling learning. The synthesis capability that orchestrates all other elements.

### The Why (Elements 10-11)

**10. Governance**  
Bidirectional communication infrastructure between innovation practice and the broader organisation. Completes innovation literacy by addressing how organisations create and sustain conditions for innovation.

**Inward flow:** Organisational why → innovation practitioners  
- Shared understanding of organisational purpose
- Legitimacy and permission to operate differently
- Alignment with broader mission

**Outward flow:** Innovation requirements/contributions → organisation  
- Evidence of value and contribution to shared mission
- Articulation of what innovation needs to function
- Demonstration of alignment

**Multi-directional function:**  
Not just vertical (leadership to practitioners) but lateral (across teams/roles/departments). Enables discussions transversely across different work cultures without solving underlying siloing or interpersonal issues (but doesn't hurt).

**Mechanisms:** Charters (or simpler mechanisms), decision rights, permission structures, leadership understanding.

**Function:** Provides insulation for innovation practice from operational expectations through formal organisational agreement. Creates legitimate channel for both flows.

**11. Resources**  
Time, budget, people, and materials required for innovation work.

**Dynamic:** Continuously contested against operational priorities, requires ongoing negotiation and defence. Resources are never "secured" - they're continuously negotiated.

**Validation:** Requirements justified within governance framework established by Element 10. Uses lived experience of Elements 1-9 as evidence base.

**What participants can articulate by end of Day 2:**
- "We need protected time because Behaviours (6) collapse under operational interruptions"
- "We need this budget because Tools (3) enable what manual methods can't"
- "We need facilitation support because managing all elements simultaneously requires meta-capability"
- "I need X because without it, Element Y breaks - and I know that because I experienced it"

**Evidence-based negotiation:** Lived experience becomes validation for requesting resources. Not theoretical advocacy; empirical justification.

**Sequence:** Governance establishes validation framework before resource requirements specified.

### Day 1: Afternoon (14:00-16:10)

**Note:** 10-minute extension flagged in advance with participants.

**00:00-00:30** Deep Reflection on Wallet Project  
Map morning's experience onto Design Thinking phases (Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test). Framework handoff: IDEO process visual (give them the map). Name systems thinking when it emerges: "You connected your partner's wallet to their broader life system - that's systems thinking as a skill."  
**Elements:** Process (explicit), Skills (systems thinking named), Facilitation (reflection structure)

**00:30-01:00** Failure Analysis Workshop  
Small groups analyse their homework examples (failed innovations from their context). Map failures onto the Design Thinking process: where did it break? What was missing? Identify patterns: cargo-culting, wrong metrics, missing empathy, weak problem framing. Framework handoff: "Why Innovation Fails" diagnostic framework.  
**Elements:** Process (diagnostic), Skills (systems thinking applied), Interventions (identifying missing pattern interrupts)

**01:00-01:30** Intervention Speed-Dating  
5 stations with tactical innovation methods to advance the process, 5-6 mins each, rotate through all. Examples: How Might We statements, 2x2 prioritisation matrices, assumption mapping, stakeholder mapping, SWOT analysis. Each station: "Use this method to analyse the Wallet Project challenge." Debrief: Which felt natural? Which felt forced? When would each be useful?  
**Elements:** Interventions (hands-on with methods and frameworks), Skills (analytical thinking)

**01:30-01:50** The Measurement Game  
Scenario: Three innovation projects at different stages (early empathy, mid-prototyping, late validation). Given: Completely stage-inappropriate metrics (ROI for empathy phase, volume of ideas for validation phase). Task: Allocate funding based on these metrics. Debrief: What happened? What metrics would be better? Why does measurement matter? Framework handoff: Stage-appropriate measurement principles.  
**Elements:** Tools (measurement frameworks), Environment (operational pressure), Skills (analytical thinking)

**01:50-02:10** Week Gap Homework Brief + Problem Space Map Introduction (20 mins total)

**01:50-02:00** (10 mins) Problem Space Map Demo  
Show the tool: screen share template or display print version. Walk through 1-2 layers together using familiar example (e.g., Wallet Project): "What are the Products in the wallet space? The Systems? The Implications?" "This helps you see how your problem connects to larger patterns - that's systems thinking in action." Don't do the full exercise - just demonstrate how it works.  
**Elements:** Tools (strategic framing), Skills (systems thinking), Facilitation (scaffolding homework)

**02:00-02:10** (10 mins) Homework Assignment  
Three tasks for the week gap:
1. **Read:** Design Thinking framework document (full process overview)
2. **Map:** Use Problem Space Map to analyse YOUR challenge (30-45 mins at home)
   - Template provided in handout pack
   - Link: https://dschool.stanford.edu/tools/map-the-problem-space
   - You've seen how it works - now apply it to something real in your context
   - Focus: See interconnections across data, tech, product, experience, system, and implications
3. **Notice:** Identify 2-3 moments in your work where Design Thinking elements would change outcomes
   - Bring specific examples to Session 2

**Purpose:** Bridge abstract learning with applied thinking, introduce strategic framing, practice systems thinking, set up Day 2 discussions.

**Elements emphasised across session:** Process, Skills (including systems thinking), Tools, Interventions, Facilitation, Environment

- Wrong metrics (operational measures applied to exploratory work)
- Missing empathy (solution-jumping without user understanding)
- Weak problem framing (unclear what problem is actually being solved)
- Process without defence capability (skilled practitioners crushed by operational pressure)

## Critical Design Decisions

### Framework Architecture

**11 Elements organised in three clusters:**
- **The What (1-3):** What innovation requires as activity
- **The How (4-9):** How it's executed at individual and collective levels
- **The Why (10-11):** Why organisations support it and what that requires

**Rationale for 11 Elements (not 9 or 10):**
- Elements 1-9 provide process literacy (execution capability)
- Element 10 (Governance) bridges to management capability
- Element 11 (Resources) provides the tangible evidence/commitment piece
- Governance must precede Resources: can't justify resource requests without governance infrastructure

**Why 11 Elements not 9:**
Foundational innovation literacy requires exposure to both execution (Elements 1-9) and organisational dimension (Elements 10-11). Most training stops at process skills. This workshop provides foundations for the complete picture - participants gain baseline capability to recognise what innovation requires AND awareness of how to advocate for organisational conditions that support it.

### Key Conceptual Distinctions

**Tools vs Interventions:**
- Tools = physical objects and technologies (WHAT you use)
- Interventions = structured instructions/methods (HOW you're instructed to use them)
- Example: Miro is a tool; "Draw your ideal innovation process in 3 minutes" is an intervention

**Environment vs Governance:**
- Environment = physical/temporal/cultural container (the conditions)
- Governance = bidirectional communication infrastructure and formal mechanisms (the legitimising structure)
- Environment creates conditions; Governance creates organisational agreement and insulation

**Governance vs Resources:**
- Governance = communication infrastructure mediating shared organisational why (the agreement)
- Resources = time, budget, people, materials flowing from that validation (the tangible proof)
- Sequence: Governance establishes legitimacy structure before resource requirements specified

**Why Three Categories (What/How/Why) Not Four:**
Original clustering considered "The Who" for Elements 4-6 (Skills, Mindset, Behaviours). Rejected because "The Who" implies identity definition - prescribing who participants need to become, which is alienating. 

By merging into "The How," Skills, Mindset, Behaviours become capabilities you develop (not identities you adopt). Framework shows what innovation requires (What), how it's executed at individual and collective levels (How), and why organisations support it (Why).

Explicit individual (4-6) → collective (7-9) distinction exists for pedagogical clarity but doesn't need participant-facing labels. They experience the progression without it being announced.

**Roles:**
Formal role definition (beyond Facilitator) belongs in advanced innovation management contexts, not foundations work. Role clarity within Collaboration (8) covers dynamic role negotiation during innovation work.

### Pedagogical Design Principles

**Framework handoff timing:**  
Theory delivered after experience, not before. Participants feel the need before receiving the map.

**Challenge selection:**  
Trivial challenges (wallet, gift-giving) over strategic challenges focuses participants on process (HOW) rather than content (WHAT).

**Multiple micro-cycles:**  
Repeated short cycles build fluency more effectively than single extended challenge. Wallet Project (Session 1) = unconscious immersion; Gift-Giving Project (Session 2) = conscious competence.

**Element visibility:**  
Colour-coding/tagging system allows participants to identify which capability they're building at any moment. Present but not intrusive during activities; explicit during reflections.

**Timing management:**  
Extensions flagged in advance with participants (Day 1 Afternoon runs to 16:10).

**Formal role archetypes:**  
Functional roles in innovation work (problem owner, subject matter expert, champion, skeptic, etc.) are implementation-level knowledge, not foundations-level. Beyond scope. ## Theoretical Grounding (Design Notes - Not for Participants)

**Initial design state:**  
Original structure was lecture-based: elements presented as definition, why it matters, discussion prompts, key takeaways. University seminar format. Information transfer that wouldn't build capability or change Monday morning behaviour. Participants would leave with excellent notes and do nothing differently. Teaching Design Thinking by talking about Design Thinking.

**Fundamental pivot:**  
Restructured to collapse theory and practice. Workshop now embodies the process being taught rather than teaching about innovation. Each section: provocative anchor → hands-on intervention → structured reflection → framework handoff (in that order).

**Pedagogical foundation:**  
Built on d.school (Stanford) and IDEO methodology. The d.school doesn't lecture about empathy - they put you in front of a human for 20 minutes and force you to discover what you don't know how to ask. Learn by failing in structured, reflected ways. Battle-tested approach, not invented pedagogy.

**Framework convergence with Sinek's Golden Circle:**  
The What/How/Why structure emerged independently but converges with Sinek's work. This is not coincidental - both frameworks describe fundamental organisational truths about meaningful work. The Why (Governance + Resources) addresses both organisational purpose and individual motivation simultaneously. Good governance doesn't just create permission structures; it creates conditions where individuals find purpose in their work.

**Key insight:** Sinek's Why (individual purpose/motivation) and organisational Why (governance/values) are co-constitutive, not separate. When organisation has clear purpose (Governance) backed by real commitment (Resources), individual motivation aligns with organisational purpose.

**Pedagogical approach:** Framework embodies these principles. Don't make convergence explicit to participants - let them discover resonances themselves. Use What/How/Why structure because it's memorable and functional, not because it aligns with Sinek. The power is in the silence.

**Innovation requirement:** Innovation requires people to take risks, tolerate ambiguity, persist through failure. None of this happens without compelling answer to "why am I doing this?" Governance and Resources are how organisations create and sustain that answer.

## Design Rationale

**Opening provocation value:**  
"Draw your ideal innovation process" functions as diagnostic, exposes knowledge gaps, introduces digital collaboration tools, creates productive humility. Non-negotiable activity.

**Challenge selection rationale:**  
Trivial challenges (wallet, gift-giving) over strategic challenges focuses participants on process (HOW) rather than content (WHAT). Cognitive load stays low so learning is about method, not domain expertise.

**Why IDEO exercises specifically:**
- 60-90 minutes for complete cycle (fits time constraints)
- Trivial topic makes process visible, not content
- Tight timing creates productive pressure (part of the learning)
- Reflection-driven learning extraction built into structure
- Battle-tested methodology from d.school/IDEO - proven effective
- False-start in Wallet Project deliberately demonstrates human-centred vs assumption-driven approach

**Multiple micro-cycles rationale:**  
Repeated short cycles build fluency more effectively than single extended challenge. 

**First cycle (Wallet Project - Day 1):** Unconscious immersion. Pure experience, minimal scaffolding. Participants will default to solution-jumping and surface-level inquiry. Let them. Failure is part of the learning. They feel what innovation is like without the map.

**Second cycle (Gift-Giving Project - Day 2):** Conscious competence. Same process structure, but participants now understand the phases. Watch how differently they perform with awareness. Can surface elements more explicitly: mindset (curiosity, deferring judgement), behaviours (active listening, asking why), facilitation (how structure enables creativity).

**Progressive complexity:** Not carrying strategic challenge for two weeks. Building fluency through repeated cycles with increasing sophistication of reflection.

**Framework handoff timing rationale:**  
Theory delivered after experience. Participants feel the need before receiving the map. "Here's the framework for what you just experienced" is more powerful than "here's what you're about to do."

**Element visibility rationale:**  
Colour-coding/tagging system allows participants to identify which capability they're building at any moment without disrupting immersion. The architecture becomes visible through reflection, not announcement.

**Governance before Resources rationale:**  
Governance establishes the legitimate communication channel and validation framework. Resource requirements are then specified within that context. Can't justify "we need X" without "our charter says innovation requires Y, and X delivers Y."

## Important Notes for Collaborators

**Documentation style requirements:**  
Factual, professional, not self-referential. Record outputs and design decisions, not the process to get there. Avoid bombastic language (words like "watershed," "critical," "breakthrough"). Don't speak to why something is important for us - record what it is and what it does. Documentation serves three purposes: important notes to remember, creating final workshop materials, informing collaborators.

**Language to avoid:**
- Self-referential process notes ("let's capture this")
- Dramatic emphasis ("Critical Distinction," "The Problem," "What's Actually Good")
- Explaining steps to reach conclusions
- Over-the-top validation or hedging language
- Anything that sounds like selling benefits rather than stating facts

**Language:**  
UK/Irish English only. Professional consultant tone delivering definitive content.

**Content philosophy:**  
Avoid obvious, redundant descriptions. Participants don't need everything explained - they need provocations, structures, and reflection moments. Trust the experiential learning to do its work.

**Timing discipline:**  
Activities have tight time constraints by design. Productive pressure is part of the learning. Extensions must be flagged in advance.

**Facilitation stance:**  
Provocateur-guide, not expert-presenter. Managing the container, introducing frameworks at strategic moments, extracting learning through reflection. Let participants discover, don't tell them what they should be discovering.
