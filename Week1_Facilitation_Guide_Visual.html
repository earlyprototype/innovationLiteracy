00. Overview

Week 1 delivers complete experiential immersion in all 11 elements of innovation literacy through the IDEO Wallet Project, followed by systematic reflection and diagnostic framework development.

Session Details

Total Duration: 4h 10m

Morning: 9:00-11:00 (2h)

Afternoon: 14:00-16:10 (2h 10m)

Format: Remote via Zoom/Teams with Mural

Participants

Team: Eirmersive (Camille + team)

Facilitator: Thom Conaty (Creative Spark)

Pre-Session Setup

  • Mural board tested
  • d.school ACTIVE playlist ready
  • Slide deck loaded
  • Breakout rooms configured
  • Materials confirmed

Workshop Philosophy

Learning Approach: Experiential immersion followed by systematic reflection. Participants learn by doing, then map their experience to frameworks.

Facilitator Stance: Provocateur-guide, not expert-presenter. Hold the container firmly, let participants discover, name what you observe rather than prescribe what they should experience.

Core Principle: Trivial challenges (redesigning a wallet) keep cognitive load low so participants can focus on process, not content. Productive failure is built into the design.

02. Opening Provocation

Deliberately expose knowledge gaps and create productive humility. Participants discover they lack shared vocabulary and understanding before being given frameworks.
00:00-00:15 (9:00-9:15)

Draw Your Ideal Innovation Process

Objective

Expose knowledge gaps, create productive humility, establish diagnostic baseline

Facilitator Script (00:00)

"Right, let's start. No preamble. I'm going to give you an instruction, and I want you to follow it without questioning or discussing. You'll have 5 minutes. Silent. Individual work. Pen and paper. Here's the task: Draw your ideal innovation process. Use whatever format makes sense to you - flowchart, diagram, sketch, words. Go."

What Happens (00:00-00:05)

  • Music: Off (silence is part of the exercise)
  • On screen: Slide - "Draw Your Ideal Innovation Process" (minimal, no hints)
  • Participant activity: Silent individual work on paper
  • Some create linear flows, some loops, some complex systems
  • Some will freeze (productive discomfort)

Gallery Walk (00:05-00:10)

"Time. Hold your drawings up to the camera."

"I'm going to look through these. Don't explain yours yet - just look at each other's. Notice patterns. Notice contradictions."

Facilitation Moves:

  • Screen camera feeds to review drawings
  • Point to patterns: "Three of you have linear flows. Two have loops. One has question marks."
  • Point to contradictions: "This one starts with 'problem.' This one starts with 'brainstorm.' This one starts with 'research.'"
  • Don't judge. Don't correct. Just observe aloud.

Debrief Questions (00:10-00:13)

  • "What assumptions did you make about what 'innovation process' means?"
  • "What did you leave out?"
  • "How confident are you that your drawing would work in practice?"

Facilitator Framing (00:13-00:15)

"Good. You've just discovered the first thing about innovation: we use the same words and mean completely different things. Park your drawings. We'll return to them this afternoon."

05. Wallet Project Phases

Detailed breakdown of each phase in the 90-minute Wallet Project cycle, including facilitator scripts, timing, and expected outcomes.
00:30-00:35

Phase 1: False Start

Objective: Interrupt assumption-driven design, demonstrate human-centred vs solution-jumping

Script: "Right, your challenge: redesign the wallet. You have 3 minutes. Go."

What Happens: Participants jump immediately to solution generation, sketch wallet designs based on assumptions, no user research or problem framing.

At 00:33: "Time. Show me what you've got."

Gallery Walk: "Interesting. Who did you design this for?"

Expected Responses: "Um... everyone?" "People?" "Wallet users?"

Reframe: "Right. You just designed wallets for imaginary people based on your assumptions. That's not innovation - that's guessing. Let's start again. This time, you'll talk to a real human first."

00:35-00:45

Phase 2: Empathy Round 1

Objective: Initial user research, establish paired work, expose surface-level inquiry

Script: "You're going to pair up. One of you is the interviewer, one is the user. Interviewer: your job is to understand your partner's relationship with their wallet. User: answer honestly. You have 5 minutes. Then you'll switch roles. Breakout rooms opening now."

Interview Prompts (posted in Mural):

  • "Tell me about your current wallet"
  • "When do you use it?"
  • "What frustrates you about it?"
  • "What works well?"

Facilitator During Breakouts: Pop into rooms briefly, listen, don't intervene yet. Most will stay surface-level.

00:45-01:00

Phase 3: "Dig Deeper" Intervention + Empathy Round 2

Objective: Pattern interrupt, push beyond surface-level inquiry

Quick Reflection: "Back to the main room. Quick reflection: what did you learn about your partner's wallet?"

Expected Responses: "They keep receipts in it" / "They don't like the colour" / "It's too bulky"

Intervention: "Right. Those are facts. Now I want you to go deeper. Why do they keep receipts? What does their wallet say about how they live? What jobs is their wallet doing beyond holding money? You're going back in for round 2. Dig deeper this time."

New Prompts (posted in Mural):

  • "Why do you organise your wallet that way?"
  • "What would you lose if your wallet disappeared?"
  • "When does your wallet fail you?"
  • "What does your wallet say about you?"

What Participants Do: Deeper questioning, uncover emotional connections, identity aspects, system implications. Aha moments: "I never thought about it that way"

01:00-01:10

Phase 4: Synthesis

Objective: Process interview data, identify patterns and insights

"Right, you've got data. Now make sense of it. Individual work: capture the most interesting things you learned about your partner. Not just facts - insights. What surprised you? What patterns did you notice? What needs are hidden in what they said? You have 7 minutes. Use sticky notes in Mural."

On Screen: Mural synthesis workspace with columns: Facts, Patterns, Insights, Needs

Music: On (low volume)

Brief Share (01:08-01:10): "Quick round: one insight each. What's the most interesting thing you learned?"

01:10-01:20

Phase 5: Problem Framing

Objective: Define the design challenge, practice problem framing

"Now frame the problem you're solving. Not 'design a better wallet' - that's a solution masquerading as a problem. What's the actual need your partner has? Frame it as a question: 'How might we...?'

Example: 'How might we help Sarah keep track of receipts without bulk?' or 'How might we make Mark feel more organised when he travels?'

You have 7 minutes. Write multiple versions. Pick the one that feels most generative."

Brief Share (01:18-01:20): "Quick round: read your HMW statement."

01:20-01:30

Phase 6: Ideation

Objective: Generate multiple solutions, defer judgement

"Now generate solutions. Quantity over quality. Defer judgement - don't critique your ideas yet. Sketch, write, diagram - whatever gets ideas out fast. You have 8 minutes. Go for volume."

Music: On (higher energy)

At 01:28: "Last minute. Circle your top 3 ideas."

01:30-01:50

Phases 7-8: Sketching + Feedback & Iteration

Sketching (01:30-01:40)

"Pick one idea. Sketch it in enough detail that someone else can understand it. You have 8 minutes. Don't aim for perfection - aim for clarity."

Feedback (01:40-01:46)

"Back to your pairs. You're going to show your sketch to your partner - the person you interviewed. Here's the rule: don't defend your design. Just show it and ask: 'What works? What doesn't? What's missing?' Listen. Take notes. You have 3 minutes each."

Iteration (01:46-01:50)

"You've got feedback. Now iterate. Modify your design based on what you learned. You have 3 minutes."

01:50-02:00

Phases 9-10: Prototyping + Testing

Prototyping (01:50-01:57)

Objective: Build tangible prototype, work under constraint

"Now build it. Use whatever materials you have at hand - paper, pens, scissors, tape, household items. You have 7 minutes. Make something your partner can hold and react to. Go."

Music: On (high energy)

Testing (01:57-02:00)

"Final step. Show your prototype to your partner via camera. Get their reaction. You have 90 seconds. Go."

Closing (01:59-02:00)

"Right. You've just run a complete Design Thinking cycle in 90 minutes. You started with assumptions, threw them out, talked to humans, framed problems, generated ideas, got feedback, iterated, built prototypes, and tested them. That's the process. This afternoon we'll unpack what just happened and why it matters."

07. Deep Reflection

Map morning experience onto Design Thinking framework. Make process explicit by naming what participants experienced and connecting it to the 11 elements.
00:00-00:30 (14:00-14:30)

Framework Handoff

Objective

Map experience to Design Thinking framework, make process explicit

Facilitator Script (00:00-00:05)

"This morning you executed a complete innovation cycle. Now we're going to map what you did onto the Design Thinking framework. This is the handoff - I'm giving you the map for the territory you just travelled."

On Screen: Slide showing Design Thinking phases (Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test) as loop diagram

Facilitation Moves

  • Walk through each phase of Design Thinking
  • Point back to morning activities: "When you interviewed your partner - that was Empathise. When you wrote HMW statements - that was Define."
  • Name what they experienced: "The false-start? That was an intervention. 'Dig deeper'? Another intervention. The 7-minute prototyping constraint? Intervention."

Facilitation Questions (00:05-00:15)

  • "What shifted between empathy round 1 and round 2?"
  • "What happened when you framed the problem as 'How might we...?' versus 'design a better wallet'?"
  • "How did tight timing affect your work - help or hurt?"
  • "What role did the Mural board play versus paper prototyping?"

Naming the 11 Elements (00:15-00:20)

"Right. Let me name what you experienced:

  • Process: Design Thinking gave you a structure - Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. Without it, you'd have stayed in the false-start.
  • Interventions: 'Dig deeper,' 'don't defend your prototype,' '7-minute build' - these are methods that push you to the next stage.
  • Tools: Mural, paper, pens, scissors. WHAT you use.
  • Skills: Observation, questioning, synthesis, creative thinking, systems thinking.
  • Mindset: Curiosity, deferring judgement, challenging assumptions.
  • Behaviours: Active listening, not defending your ideas, disciplined experimentation.
  • Environment: Time boundaries, digital space, permission to fail.
  • Collaboration: Paired work, giving feedback, receiving feedback without defensiveness.
  • Facilitation: I managed the process, kept time, intervened when needed.
  • Governance & Resources: The time you were given, the materials, the permission to experiment - that's what these look like in practice.

All 11 elements were active this morning. These are the foundations of innovation work."

Framework Handoff (00:20-00:30)

Action: Post IDEO Design Thinking Framework PDF in Mural + chat

"This is your reference. When you're back at work and someone says 'we need to innovate,' you'll have a foundational understanding of what process looks like."

08. Failure Analysis Workshop

Apply diagnostic lens to real-world innovation failures. Participants use their new framework to identify where innovation broke in their own contexts.
00:30-01:00 (14:30-15:00)

Diagnosing Real Failures

Objective

Apply diagnostic lens to real-world innovation failures

Facilitator Script (00:30-00:32)

"Right, you brought examples of failed innovations from your context. We're going to analyse them using the framework you now have. Small groups - 3-4 people. You have 20 minutes. For each failure:

  1. Map it onto the Design Thinking process - where did it break?
  2. What was missing? Which elements?
  3. What would have changed the outcome?"

Format: Breakout rooms, small groups, 20 minutes

On Screen: Mural failure analysis workspace (columns for each Design Thinking phase)

What Participants Do

  • Share failure examples
  • Map failures onto Design Thinking phases
  • Identify missing elements using morning's framework
  • Document patterns

Debrief (00:50-00:58)

Each group shares one failure and diagnosis. Facilitator listens for patterns.

Expected Patterns:

  • "We jumped to solutions" (Define phase missing)
  • "We didn't talk to users" (Empathise phase missing)
  • "We had the process but no time to use it" (Environment/Resources missing)
  • "Leadership wanted ROI metrics during discovery" (wrong measurement, operational pressure)

Facilitator Framing (00:58-01:00)

"Notice the patterns. Innovation fails when:

  • Process is cargo-culted (we say we do Design Thinking but skip the hard parts)
  • Empathy is skipped (we design for imaginary users)
  • Problems are poorly framed (we solve the wrong thing well)
  • Wrong metrics are applied (operational measures crush exploratory work)
  • Practitioners have process skills but no organisational defence

You now have a foundational diagnostic lens. You can start recognising where and why innovation breaks."

09. Intervention Speed-Dating

Hands-on exposure to tactical methods and frameworks. Participants rotate through five intervention stations to build awareness of what tools exist.
01:00-01:30 (15:00-15:30)

Five Methods, Five Minutes Each

Objective

Hands-on exposure to tactical methods and frameworks

Facilitator Script (01:00-01:02)

"You've experienced interventions this morning - 'dig deeper,' 'don't defend,' time constraints. Now you're going to try five specific methods that help you move through the innovation process. Five stations, five minutes each. You'll rotate through all of them. Each station has a method and an instruction to apply it to the Wallet Project challenge. Go fast - focus on understanding what each method does, not perfecting it."

Station 1: How Might We

Linguistic reframing for problem definition

Time: 5 minutes

Station 2: 2x2 Prioritisation

Decision-making framework (Impact vs Effort)

Time: 5 minutes

Station 3: Assumption Mapping

Identify and test assumptions

Time: 5 minutes

Station 4: Stakeholder Mapping

See the system around the problem

Time: 5 minutes

Station 5: SWOT Analysis

Analyse strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats

Time: 5 minutes

Format

Self-guided rotation through Mural stations. Each station contains:

  • Method description (1-2 sentences)
  • Instruction: "Apply this to the Wallet Project"
  • Workspace for doing the exercise

Debrief (01:25-01:30)

  • "Which method felt most natural?"
  • "Which felt forced?"
  • "When would you use each one?"

"These are interventions - structured methods that help you advance the process. Some will fit your context, some won't. The point is knowing they exist and beginning to recognise when to deploy them. This is foundations work - you're building awareness of what's available, not mastery of each method."

10. The Measurement Game

Expose stage-inappropriate metrics and operational pressure on innovation. Participants discover how wrong measurement crushes innovation faster than bad ideas.
01:30-01:50 (15:30-15:50)

Funding Committee Exercise

Objective

Expose stage-inappropriate metrics, understand operational pressure on innovation

Facilitator Script (01:30-01:32)

"Right, you're on a funding committee. You have budget to allocate across three innovation projects. Here are the projects:

  • Project A: Early empathy phase - team conducting user interviews
  • Project B: Mid-prototyping phase - team building and testing prototypes
  • Project C: Late validation phase - team assessing market fit

You have these metrics to guide your decision:

  • Total number of ideas generated
  • ROI projection
  • Number of prototypes built
  • Customer satisfaction score
  • Time to market

You have 10 minutes. Decide how to allocate funding. Use the metrics provided."

What Participants Do (01:32-01:42)

  • Review scenarios
  • Apply provided metrics
  • Allocate funding
  • Justify decisions

Debrief (01:42-01:48)

  • "How did you allocate funding?"
  • "Which metrics drove your decisions?"
  • "Did anything feel wrong?"

Expected Responses:

  • "We funded Project C because it had ROI data"
  • "Project A looked weak - no metrics yet"
  • "The metrics didn't fit the projects"

Facilitator Reveal (01:48-01:50)

"Right. Here's what just happened: I gave you completely stage-inappropriate metrics. You can't measure ROI during empathy - you don't have a solution yet. Counting ideas during validation is meaningless - you're testing fit, not generating options. But these are the metrics organisations apply to innovation all the time. And when innovation gets measured wrong, it gets killed.

Stage-appropriate measurement matters:

  • Empathy phase: Quality of insights, depth of understanding
  • Ideation phase: Diversity of concepts, not volume
  • Prototyping phase: Learning velocity, feedback quality
  • Validation phase: Evidence of fit, market signals

Wrong metrics crush innovation faster than bad ideas."
