# Workshop Overview - Fundamentals of Innovation Literacy

## Design Approach

**Core Principle:** Collapse theory and practice. Participants learn by doing, not by listening. All conceptual frameworks are handed off AFTER participants have experienced the need for them.

**Pedagogical Method:** Point to things, indicate where they sit in the framework, contextualise through experiential learning. This is foundations work - not mastery, but sufficient literacy to recognise the landscape, know what matters, and build capability through practice.

**The Narrative Arc:** Innovation fails 70% of the time because organisations build management structures without process capability, or teach process without giving people the tools to defend it organisationally. This workshop provides complete innovation literacy through 11 elements organised as What/How/Why. Elements 1-9 address execution capability (what innovation requires, how it's performed), while Elements 10-11 address organisational capability (why organisations support innovation and what that requires). Participants leave able to innovate AND able to protect the conditions that make innovation possible.

**Structure:** Series of focused, experiential interventions (IDEO-style micro-cycles) rather than one sustained design challenge. Trivial topics (wallets, gift-giving) create low stakes and keep cognitive load low so participants can focus on process, not content.

**Duration:** 4 sessions across 2 days (one week apart)
- Day 1: Sessions 1 & 2 (9:00-11:00, 14:00-16:10)
- Day 2: Sessions 3 & 4 (9:30-11:00, 14:00-16:00)
- Total: 7h 40m

**Delivery:** Fully remote workshop series using Mural and digital collaboration tools

**Client:** Eirmersive (XR advocates, R&D partners, technology adoption specialists), led by Camille. Mix of intuitive innovation understanding and desire for structured upskilling.

## The 11 Elements (Ultimate Learning Objectives)

The workshop activities touch on these elements explicitly providing an opportunity for participants to explore and reflect on them in a tested context. Documentation leverages colour-coding/tagging to show which elements are active at any moment, with routes for deeper exploration.

**The What (Elements 1-3):**

1. **Process** - Innovation as structured, repeatable activity (Design Thinking framework)
2. **Interventions** - Stage-dependent structured mini-processes that help participants move to the next stage more effectively (e.g., "dig deeper" prompt after first empathy round, "don't defend your prototype" instruction)
3. **Tools** - Actual technologies and platforms (Miro/Mural, Kanban boards, AI assistants like ChatGPT for ideation/observation)

**The How (Elements 4-9):**

4. **Skills and Competencies** - Analytical thinking, creative thinking, communication, experimentation, comfort with ambiguity, systems thinking
5. **Mindset** - Curiosity, tolerance for uncertainty, challenging assumptions, holding multiple perspectives
6. **Behaviours** - Active listening, constructive challenge, disciplined experimentation, reflective practice
7. **Environment** - Physical spaces, temporal boundaries, permission structures, and organisational conditions that protect innovation behaviours from operational gravity
8. **Collaboration** - Psychological safety, cognitive diversity, role clarity, productive tension, effective teamwork
9. **Facilitation** - Unlocking potential of processes and collaboration, managing constructive conflict, enabling learning (the synthesis capability)

**The Why (Elements 10-11):**

10. **Governance** - Bidirectional communication infrastructure between innovation practice and the broader organisation. Mediates shared organisational purpose inward to practitioners and enables legitimate articulation of innovation requirements outward. Establishes insulation for innovation practice from operational expectations through formal mechanisms (charters, decision rights, permission structures).

11. **Resources** - Time, budget, people, and materials secured and defended in ongoing competition with operational priorities. Requirements are validated and justified within the governance framework established by Element 10. Uses lived experience of Elements 1-9 as evidence base.

## Session 1 Morning (9:00-11:00)

**Setup Notes (Remote Delivery):**
- Music during work phases: [d.school ACTIVE playlist](https://open.spotify.com/playlist/2beGxzTgxl11D95OTXTJjO) (Scott Doorley) - played via screen share or direct link
- Turn music down to give instructions, back up during work
- Participants encouraged to stand/move during work phases (camera on but flexible positioning)
- Prototyping materials: Participants have basic supplies at home (paper, pens, scissors, tape, household items for prototyping)
- Mural board pre-configured and shared

**00:00-00:15** Opening Provocation: "Draw Your Ideal Innovation Process"
- Individual, 3 minutes, silent, using Miro/Mural
- Gallery walk to spot patterns and contradictions
- **Purpose:** Expose knowledge gaps, introduce digital collaboration tools, create humility, set up the series
- **Elements touched:** Process (diagnostic), Tools (Miro/Mural intro), Mindset (challenging assumptions)

**00:15-00:30** Introductions and Contextualisation
- Who's in the room, why we're here
- **The framing:** "Innovation fails 70% of the time. Why? Organisations build management structures without process capability - or teach process without giving people the tools to defend it organisationally. You end up with innovation labs that can't innovate, or skilled practitioners who get crushed by operational pressure. Both are missing half the picture."
- **The workshop structure:** "This workshop operates at two levels. First, innovation literacy - you'll execute complete innovation cycles and experience the 11 elements that make them work. Second, the bridge to innovation management - you'll learn to articulate what sustained innovation actually requires, using your literacy as evidence. By the end, you won't just know how to innovate - you'll know how to secure what innovation needs to survive in your organisation."
- Workshop objectives: Elements 1-9 (process capability) + Elements 10-11 (organisational advocacy)
- **Elements touched:** Environment (setting the container), Collaboration (building psychological safety)

**00:30-02:00** IDEO Wallet Project (90 mins)
- Full cycle: false-start, empathy interviews (2x via breakout rooms), synthesis, problem framing, ideation, sketching (digital or paper), feedback, iteration, prototyping (using home materials), testing
- Tight timing, upbeat music, active engagement encouraged
- **Remote adaptation:** Paired work in breakout rooms, prototypes shown via camera, digital sketching in Mural optional
- **Elements touched:** All 11 (experiential immersion — Elements 1-9 explicitly, Elements 10-11 implicitly through workshop governance and resource structures)

**02:00-02:00** Debrief and Session 1 Afternoon Preview
- Brief reflection on morning experience
- Preview afternoon session: process unpacking, failure analysis, measurement
- Homework: bring example of failed innovation from participant context
- **Elements:** Process, Facilitation

## Session 1 Afternoon (14:00-16:00)

**00:00-00:30** Deep Reflection on Wallet Project
- Map morning's experience onto Design Thinking phases (Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test)
- Framework handoff: IDEO process visual (give them the map)
- Name systems thinking when it emerges: "You connected your partner's wallet to their broader life system - that's systems thinking as a skill"
- **Elements:** Process (explicit), Skills (systems thinking named), Facilitation (reflection structure)

**00:30-01:00** Failure Analysis Workshop
- Small groups analyse their homework examples (failed innovations from their context)
- Map failures onto the Design Thinking process: where did it break? What was missing?
- Identify patterns: cargo-culting, wrong metrics, missing empathy, weak problem framing
- Framework handoff: "Why Innovation Fails" diagnostic framework
- **Elements:** Process (diagnostic), Skills (systems thinking applied), Interventions (identifying missing pattern interrupts)

**01:00-01:30** Intervention Speed-Dating
- 5 stations with tactical innovation methods to advance the process, 5-6 mins each, rotate through all
- Examples: How Might We statements, 2x2 prioritisation matrices, assumption mapping, stakeholder mapping, SWOT analysis
- Each station: "Use this method to analyse the Wallet Project challenge"
- Debrief: Which felt natural? Which felt forced? When would each be useful?
- **Elements:** Interventions (hands-on with methods and frameworks), Skills (analytical thinking)

**01:30-01:50** The Measurement Game
- Scenario: Three innovation projects at different stages (early empathy, mid-prototyping, late validation)
- Given: Completely stage-inappropriate metrics (ROI for empathy phase, volume of ideas for validation phase)
- Task: Allocate funding based on these metrics
- Debrief: What happened? What metrics would be better? Why does measurement matter?
- Framework handoff: Stage-appropriate measurement principles
- **Elements:** Tools (measurement frameworks), Environment (operational pressure), Skills (analytical thinking)

**01:50-02:10** Week Gap Homework Brief + Problem Space Map Introduction (20 mins - total 25 mins borrowed from session, announced in advance)

**01:50-02:00** (10 mins) Problem Space Map Demo
- Show the tool: screen share template or display print version
- Walk through 1-2 layers together using familiar example (e.g., Wallet Project)
  - "What are the Products in the wallet space? The Systems? The Implications?"
- "This helps you see how your problem connects to larger patterns - that's systems thinking in action"
- Don't do the full exercise - just demonstrate how it works
- **Elements:** Tools (strategic framing), Skills (systems thinking), Facilitation (scaffolding homework)

**02:00-02:10** (10 mins) Homework Assignment
Three tasks for the week gap:

1. **Read:** Design Thinking framework document (full process overview)
2. **Map:** Use Problem Space Map to analyse YOUR challenge (30-45 mins at home)
   - Template provided in handout pack
   - Link: https://dschool.stanford.edu/tools/map-the-problem-space
   - You've seen how it works - now apply it to something real in your context
   - Focus: See interconnections across data, tech, product, experience, system, and implications
3. **Notice:** Identify 2-3 moments in your work where Design Thinking elements would change outcomes
   - Bring specific examples to Session 2

**Purpose:** Bridge abstract learning with applied thinking, introduce strategic framing, practice systems thinking, set up Session 2 discussions

**Session End Time:** 16:10 (10 minutes over - flagged in advance with participants)

**Elements emphasised across session:** Process, Skills (including systems thinking), Tools, Interventions, Facilitation, Environment

- Participant workbooks: colour tags in margins showing active elements

## Design Rationale

**Opening provocation:** "Draw your ideal innovation process" functions as diagnostic, exposes knowledge gaps, introduces digital collaboration tools

**Challenge selection:** Trivial challenges (wallet, gift-giving) over strategic challenges focuses participants on process (HOW) rather than content (WHAT)

**Multiple micro-cycles:** Repeated short cycles build fluency more effectively than single extended challenge

**Framework handoff timing:** Theory delivered after experience, not before. Participants feel the need before receiving the map.

**Element visibility:** Colour-coding/tagging system allows participants to identify which capability they're building at any moment

**Timing management:** Extensions flagged in advance with participants

## Framework Structure: The What/How/Why

Three-category structure (What 1-3, How 4-9, Why 10-11) provides memorable organisation without prescribing participant identity.

### Element 10: Governance

**Function:** Bidirectional communication infrastructure between innovation practice and broader organisation

**Inward flow:** Organisational why → innovation practitioners
- Purpose, legitimacy, permission to operate differently

**Outward flow:** Innovation requirements/contributions → organisation
- Evidence of value, articulation of needs, alignment demonstration

**Mechanisms:** Charters, decision rights, permission structures, leadership understanding

**Purpose:** Provides insulation for innovation practice from operational expectations through formal organisational agreement

### Element 11: Resources

**Definition:** Time, budget, people, and materials

**Dynamic:** Continuously contested against operational priorities, requires ongoing negotiation and defence

**Validation:** Requirements justified within governance framework established by Element 10

**Evidence base:** Lived experience of Elements 1-9 provides justification ("need X because without it, Element Y breaks")

**Sequence:** Governance establishes validation framework before resource requirements specified

### Roles

Formal role definition (beyond Facilitator) belongs in advanced innovation management contexts, not foundations work. Role clarity within Collaboration (8) covers dynamic role negotiation during innovation work.
