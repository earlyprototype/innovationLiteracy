# Innovation Literacy: Glossary of Key Terms

This glossary defines key terminology used throughout the Fundamentals of Innovation Literacy framework. Terms are organised alphabetically for reference.

---

## A

**Ambiguity Tolerance**  
The cognitive capability to remain functional and engaged when outcomes are uncertain, information is incomplete, and the path forward is unclear. Not a personality trait but a practised capability developed through repeated exposure to uncertain situations within supportive structures.

**Assumption Mapping**  
An intervention used during the Define phase to explicitly identify and document what must be true for a proposed solution to succeed. Makes hidden assumptions visible so they can be tested rather than treated as facts.

---

## C

**Cognitive Diversity**  
The inclusion of people who think differently - who bring different mental models, disciplinary backgrounds, problem-solving approaches, and life experiences. Distinct from demographic diversity (though often correlated). The raw material of innovation.

**Constructive Challenge**  
Questioning ideas, assumptions, and proposals in ways that strengthen rather than undermine them. Asks "have we considered...?" or "what evidence supports...?" rather than "that won't work." Requires courage and skill to frame challenges as contributions not attacks.

**Convergent Thinking**  
The cognitive process of narrowing possibilities, making decisions, and focusing effort. Essential during Define and Deliver phases. Must alternate with divergent thinking for effective innovation.

---

## D

**Design Thinking**  
A human-centred, iterative methodology that shifts the starting point of innovation away from technical feasibility toward human desirability. Typically structured in five non-linear phases: Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test.

**Divergent Thinking**  
The cognitive process of exploring widely, generating multiple possibilities, and deferring judgement. Essential during Discover and Develop phases. Must alternate with convergent thinking for effective innovation.

**Double Diamond**  
A process framework developed by the UK Design Council that maps innovation across two "diamonds": the Problem Space (Discover, Define) and the Solution Space (Develop, Deliver). Makes explicit the necessity of alternating between divergent and convergent thinking.

---

## E

**Experimentation**  
Structured, purposeful testing designed to gather evidence and learn from results. Includes comfort with the possibility that experiments will disprove hypotheses. Distinct from "trial and error" - experimentation is documented and intentional.

---

## F

**Facilitation**  
The practice of guiding a group through a process, enabling participation, managing dynamics, and ensuring the process achieves intended outcomes. The synthesis capability that orchestrates all other elements.

**Feedback Loop**  
A system dynamic where outputs influence inputs, creating circular causality. Balancing loops resist change (maintain stability); reinforcing loops amplify change (growth or collapse). Understanding feedback loops is essential for systems thinking.

---

## G

**Gated Process**  
A structured approach where progression to the next phase requires passing through a "gate" - meeting defined criteria or learning objectives. Failure to pass indicates need to return to an earlier phase. Removes concept of "failure" by replacing it with informed iteration.

**Governance (Innovation)**  
The bidirectional communication infrastructure between innovation practice and the broader organisation. Mediates shared organisational purpose inward to practitioners and articulates innovation requirements outward to the organisation.

**Groan Zone**  
The period of confusion, frustration, and ambiguity that occurs as diverse groups move from divergent exploration to convergent decision-making. Where genuine insight emerges if the facilitator holds the space rather than rushing to premature resolution.

**Growth Mindset**  
The belief that capabilities develop through effort, practice, and learning - as opposed to fixed mindset that treats ability as innate and unchangeable. Essential for sustaining engagement through innovation setbacks and iterations.

---

## H

**HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)**  
The dynamic where groups default to following the most senior person's view rather than genuinely considering cognitive diversity. Facilitation structures are required to prevent this pattern.

---

## I

**Innovation Charter**  
A written, agreed document defining the terms under which innovation operates within an organisation. Addresses scope, resources, decision rights, behaviours, and outcome capture. Provides protection and legitimacy for innovation work.

**Innovation Literacy**  
The foundational understanding of innovation as a structured, learnable process - and the capability to recognise, participate in, and support that process effectively. Encompasses Elements 1-11 of this framework.

**Interventions**  
Deliberate, structured actions taken at specific points in the innovation process to shape, test, or redirect outcomes. Pattern interrupts applied at strategic moments to prevent common failure modes. Distinct from tools: interventions are HOW you use tools.

**Iterative Process**  
A looping approach where failure to progress indicates need to return to an earlier phase with new learning. Creates incremental drive towards quality outputs through repeated cycles of building and learning.

---

## L

**Leverage Point**  
A place in a system where small changes produce disproportionate impact. Systems thinking helps identify high-leverage interventions versus low-leverage efforts that consume resources without producing meaningful change.

---

## M

**Metered Funding**  
Releasing resources (time and budget) in small tranches based on evidence generated at each stage-gate, rather than large upfront allocations based on business cases. Reduces risk by matching investment to validated learning.

---

## O

**Operational Gravity**  
The tendency for operational culture and priorities to dominate innovation work through the urgent crowding out the important. Requires deliberate environmental design and governance to counteract.

---

## P

**Problem Space**  
The first diamond of the Double Diamond framework (Discover, Define). The work of understanding and framing the problem before moving to solutions. Teams that skip proper problem space work produce solutions to the wrong problems.

**Process (Innovation)**  
A structured set of steps or boundaries useful for orientation during the larger innovation process. Although sequential, not necessarily linear. Often gated rather than time-bound.

**Prototype**  
A tangible representation of an idea, built to test assumptions and learn. Should be appropriate fidelity for stage: low-fidelity (paper, cardboard) during exploration; high-fidelity only after concepts are validated.

**Psychological Safety**  
Shared belief that the team environment is safe for interpersonal risk-taking. Members can speak up, ask questions, challenge ideas, admit mistakes, and share unfinished thinking without fear of punishment or humiliation. The single most important factor in team effectiveness.

---

## R

**Reflective Practice**  
Pausing, reviewing, and extracting learning from experience as regular rhythm. Converts experience into capability. Often first behaviour dropped under time pressure, precisely when most needed.

---

## S

**Solution Space**  
The second diamond of the Double Diamond framework (Develop, Deliver). The work of generating and implementing solutions after the problem has been properly framed. Only entered after problem space work is complete.

**Stage-Gate**  
Decision points in gated processes where teams must demonstrate learning or meet criteria before progressing. Used to prevent advancing with insufficient understanding or evidence.

**Systems Thinking**  
The cognitive capability to see interconnections, feedback loops, and leverage points rather than isolated problems and linear cause-effect. Essential for understanding how innovations will behave in complex real-world contexts.

---

## T

**Tools (Innovation)**  
Physical objects and useful technologies that enable innovation work. Includes everything from pencils and sticky notes to digital collaboration platforms and AI assistants. Distinct from interventions: tools are WHAT you use.

---

## V

**Validated Learning**  
Evidence-based knowledge acquired through testing that either confirms or disproves assumptions. In innovation, disproving a hypothesis is success (acquiring valuable data) not failure. Has explicit financial value in risk reduction.
