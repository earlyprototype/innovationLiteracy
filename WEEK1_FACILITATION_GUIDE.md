### 00:00-00:15 (9:00-9:15): Opening Provocation

**Objective:** Expose knowledge gaps, create productive humility, diagnostic baseline

**Facilitator script (00:00):**
"Right, let's start. No preamble. I'm going to give you an instruction, and I want you to follow it without questioning or discussing. You'll have 5 minutes. Silent. Individual work. Pen and paper. Here's the task: Draw your ideal innovation process. Use whatever format makes sense to you — flowchart, diagram, sketch, words. Go."

**Music:** Off (silence is part of the exercise)

**On screen:** Slide — "Draw Your Ideal Innovation Process" (minimal, no hints, no frameworks)

**What participants do (00:00-00:05):**
- Silent individual work on paper
- Draw, sketch, write their interpretation of "ideal innovation process"
- Some will create linear flows, some loops, some complex systems
- Some will freeze (productive discomfort)

**Facilitator during drawing:**
- Watch but don't comment
- Note interesting patterns or contradictions visible via camera feeds
- Keep time visible

**Facilitator at 00:05:**
"Time. Hold your drawings up to the camera."

**Gallery Walk (00:05-00:10):**

**Facilitator script:**
"I'm going to look through these. Don't explain yours yet — just look at each other's. Notice patterns. Notice contradictions."

**Facilitation moves:**
- Screen camera feeds to review drawings (participants keep cameras on with drawings visible)
- Point to patterns: "Three of you have linear flows. Two have loops. One has question marks."
- Point to contradictions: "This one starts with 'problem.' This one starts with 'brainstorm.' This one starts with 'research.'"
- Don't judge. Don't correct. Just observe aloud.

**Debrief questions (00:10-00:13):**
- "What assumptions did you make about what 'innovation process' means?"
- "What did you leave out?"
- "How confident are you that your drawing would work in practice?"

**Expected responses:**
- "I wasn't sure where to start"
- "I realised I don't know what innovation process actually means"
- "Mine looks nothing like theirs"

**Facilitator framing (00:13-00:15):**
"Good. You've just discovered the first thing about innovation: we use the same words and mean completely different things. Park your drawings. We'll return to them this afternoon."

**Elements touched:** Process (diagnostic), Mindset (challenging assumptions)

### 00:30-02:00 (9:30-11:00): IDEO Wallet Project (90 mins)

**Objective:** Experiential immersion in all 11 elements

**Resources:**
- IDEO Wallet Project facilitator guide (reference)
- d.school ACTIVE playlist (music during work phases)
- Mural board with Wallet Project workspaces pre-configured
- Breakout rooms for paired work

**Overall structure:**
1. False-start (5 mins)
2. Empathy round 1 (10 mins)
3. "Dig deeper" intervention + Empathy round 2 (15 mins)
4. Synthesis (10 mins)
5. Problem framing (10 mins)
6. Ideation (10 mins)
7. Sketching (10 mins)
8. Feedback + iteration (10 mins)
9. Prototyping (7 mins)
10. Testing (3 mins)

#### Phase 1: False-Start (00:30-00:35)

**Objective:** Interrupt assumption-driven design, demonstrate human-centred vs solution-jumping

**Facilitator script (00:30):**
"Right, your challenge: redesign the wallet. You have 3 minutes. Go."

**Music:** On (moderate volume)

**What participants do:**
- Jump immediately to solution generation
- Sketch wallet designs based on assumptions
- No user research, no problem framing

**Facilitator at 00:33:**
"Time. Show me what you've got."

**Gallery walk (00:33-00:34):**
- Quick scan through solutions
- "Interesting. Who did you design this for?"
- Expected responses: "Um... everyone?" "People?" "Wallet users?"

**Facilitator script (00:34-00:35):**
"Right. You just designed wallets for imaginary people based on your assumptions. That's not innovation - that's guessing. Let's start again. This time, you'll talk to a real human first."

**Purpose:** Deliberate failure. Sets up the need for empathy/discovery.

**On screen:** Mural board, individual workspaces now marked "False Start - Park This"

#### Phase 2: Empathy Round 1 (00:35-00:45)

**Objective:** Initial user research, establish paired work, expose surface-level inquiry

**Facilitator script (00:35-00:36):**
"You're going to pair up. One of you is the interviewer, one is the user. Interviewer: your job is to understand your partner's relationship with their wallet. User: answer honestly. You have 5 minutes. Then you'll switch roles. Breakout rooms opening now."

**Breakout rooms:** Pairs for 5 minutes each (10 mins total)

**Music:** Off during interviews

**Interview prompts (posted in Mural):**
- "Tell me about your current wallet"
- "When do you use it?"
- "What frustrates you about it?"
- "What works well?"

**What participants do:**
- Conduct interviews (5 mins per role)
- Some will ask good questions, some will lead with solutions
- Most will stay surface-level

**Facilitator during breakouts:**
- Pop into rooms briefly, listen, don't intervene yet

**Return to main room (00:45)**

---

#### Phase 3: "Dig Deeper" Intervention + Empathy Round 2 (00:45-01:00)

**Objective:** Pattern interrupt, push beyond surface-level inquiry

**Facilitator script (00:45-00:46):**
"Back to the main room. Quick reflection: what did you learn about your partner's wallet?"

**Expected responses:**
- "They keep receipts in it"
- "They don't like the colour"
- "It's too bulky"

**Facilitator intervention (00:46-00:47):**
"Right. Those are facts. Now I want you to go deeper. Why do they keep receipts? What does their wallet say about how they live? What jobs is their wallet doing beyond holding money? You're going back in for round 2. Dig deeper this time."

**Breakout rooms:** Same pairs, 5 minutes each (10 mins total)

**New prompts (posted in Mural):**
- "Why do you organise your wallet that way?"
- "What would you lose if your wallet disappeared?"
- "When does your wallet fail you?"
- "What does your wallet say about you?"

**What participants do:**
- Deeper questioning
- Uncover emotional connections, identity aspects, system implications
- Aha moments: "I never thought about it that way"

**Facilitator during breakouts:**
- Listen for depth shift
- Note interesting discoveries for debrief

**Return to main room (01:00)**

---

**Music:** On (low volume)

**Facilitator script (01:00-01:01):**

**Facilitator at 01:07:**
"Last minute. Start looking for the insight that will drive your design."

"Quick round: one insight each. What's the most interesting thing you learned?"

**Facilitator listens:** Note themes for afternoon reflection

#### Phase 5: Problem Framing (01:10-01:20)

**Objective:** Define the design challenge, practice problem framing

**Facilitator script (01:10-01:11):**
"Now frame the problem you're solving. Not 'design a better wallet' - that's a solution masquerading as a problem. What's the actual need your partner has? Frame it as a question: 'How might we...?'

Example: 'How might we help Sarah keep track of receipts without bulk?' or 'How might we make Mark feel more organised when he travels?'

You have 7 minutes. Write multiple versions. Pick the one that feels most generative."

**Music:** On

**On screen:** Mural workspace for How Might We statements

**What participants do:**
- Draft multiple HMW statements
- Refine problem framing
- Select one to drive design

**Facilitator at 01:17:**
"Time to commit. Circle your best HMW statement."

**Brief share (01:18-01:20):**
"Quick round: read your HMW statement."

**Facilitator listens:** Track how well problems are framed

### 00:30-01:00 (14:30-15:00): Failure Analysis Workshop

**Objective:** Apply diagnostic lens to real-world innovation failures

**Music:** Off

**On screen:** Mural failure analysis workspace (columns for each Design Thinking phase)

**Facilitator script (00:30-00:32):**
"Right, you brought examples of failed innovations from your context. We're going to analyse them using the framework you now have. You'll work together as one group. You have 20 minutes. For each failure:
1. Map it onto the Design Thinking process - where did it break?
2. What was missing? Which elements?
3. What would have changed the outcome?

**Ground rule:** This is diagnostic work, not blame. We're looking at systems and patterns, not finger-pointing. What you share here stays here."

**Working mode:** Single group of 3 (same company), 20 minutes

**Facilitator note:** Same-company dynamics require careful handling. They may self-censor or conversely overshare organizational dysfunction. Your role is to keep focus on learning patterns, not venting grievances.

**What participants do:**
- Share failure examples
- Map failures onto Design Thinking phases
- Identify missing elements using morning's framework
- Document patterns

**Facilitator during session (00:32-00:50):**
- Stay present but not hovering - give them space to talk openly
- Listen for: Are they analyzing or blaming? Are they connecting to framework or just storytelling?
- Intervene if stuck: "Where on the Design Thinking map does this sit? What phase broke?"
- Watch for: One person dominating, defensiveness, avoiding real issues
- Note patterns for debrief - especially if they identify same failure modes repeatedly

**Probing questions if needed:**
- "Was this an empathy failure, a problem framing failure, or something else?"
- "Which of the 11 elements was missing or weak?"
- "If you ran this project again tomorrow, what would you do differently in the first week?"

**Return to main room (00:50)**

**Debrief (00:50-00:58):**
"Right, what patterns did you see? Not just 'this project failed' - what failure modes keep appearing?"

**Listen for and name:**
- Solution jumping (skipping empathy/define)
- Wrong metrics applied (ROI during discovery)
- Process cargo-culting (say Design Thinking, skip hard parts)
- Missing organizational defence (skills exist but no protection to use them)

**Key facilitation move (00:56-00:58):**
"Here's what I'm hearing: [name 2-3 patterns]. These aren't unique to Eirmersive - they're systemic failure modes in organizations trying to innovate. The question isn't 'why did these projects fail?' The question is: **what conditions would allow them to succeed?** That's governance work - Week 2 territory."

**Purpose:** Shift from problem identification to solution orientation. Plant seed for governance/resources work next week.

**Facilitator framing (00:58-01:00):**
"Notice the patterns. Innovation fails when:
- Process is cargo-culted (we say we do Design Thinking but skip the hard parts)
- Empathy is skipped (we design for imaginary users)
- Problems are poorly framed (we solve the wrong thing well)
- Wrong metrics are applied (operational measures crush exploratory work)
- Practitioners have process skills but no organisational defence

You now have a foundational diagnostic lens. You can start recognising where and why innovation breaks."

**Framework handoff:**
- **On screen:** Slide "Why Innovation Fails" (diagnostic framework)
- **Action:** Post in Mural + chat

**Elements touched:** Process (diagnostic), Skills, Interventions (identifying missing pattern interrupts)

### 01:30-01:50 (15:30-15:50): The Measurement Game

**Objective:** Expose stage-inappropriate metrics, understand operational pressure on innovation

**Music:** Off

**On screen:** Slide with three project scenarios

**Facilitator script (01:30-01:32):**
"Right, you're on a funding committee. You have budget to allocate across three innovation projects. Here are the projects:

**Project A:** Early empathy phase - team conducting user interviews
**Project B:** Mid-prototyping phase - team building and testing prototypes
**Project C:** Late validation phase - team assessing market fit

You have these metrics to guide your decision:
- Total number of ideas generated
- ROI projection
- Number of prototypes built
- Customer satisfaction score
- Time to market

You have 10 minutes. Decide how to allocate funding. Use the metrics provided."

**On screen:** Mural workspace with projects listed, metrics provided, budget allocation sliders

**What participants do (01:32-01:42):**
- Review scenarios
- Apply provided metrics
- Allocate funding
- Justify decisions

**Music:** On (low) during work

**Music:** Off at 01:42

**Debrief (01:42-01:48):**
- "How did you allocate funding?"
- "Which metrics drove your decisions?"
- "Did anything feel wrong?"

**Expected responses:**
- "We funded Project C because it had ROI data"
- "Project A looked weak - no metrics yet"
- "The metrics didn't fit the projects"

**Facilitator reveal (01:48-01:50):**
"Right. Here's what just happened: I gave you completely stage-inappropriate metrics. You can't measure ROI during empathy - you don't have a solution yet. Counting ideas during validation is meaningless - you're testing fit, not generating options. But these are the metrics organisations apply to innovation all the time. And when innovation gets measured wrong, it gets killed.

Stage-appropriate measurement matters:
- Empathy phase: Quality of insights, depth of understanding
- Ideation phase: Diversity of concepts, not volume
- Prototyping phase: Learning velocity, feedback quality
- Validation phase: Evidence of fit, market signals

Wrong metrics crush innovation faster than bad ideas."

**Bridge to their context (01:50):**
"Quick check: What metrics are you measured by at Eirmersive when you're working on innovation projects? Are they stage-appropriate or are you getting crushed by operational measures during exploratory work?"

**Listen for:** Tension between innovation needs and organizational measurement systems. Don't solve it now - note it for Week 2 governance work.

**Purpose:** Make the lesson personal and visceral. They've felt the trap in the exercise; now they see it in their work.

**Framework handoff:**
- **On screen:** Slide "Stage-Appropriate Measurement Principles"
- **Action:** Post in Mural + chat

**Elements touched:** Tools (measurement frameworks), Environment (operational pressure), Skills (analytical thinking)

## Facilitator Notes

**Stance throughout:**
- Provocateur-guide, not expert-presenter
- Hold the container firmly (timing discipline)

- Name what you observe, don't prescribe what they should have experienced

- Morning: High energy, fast-paced, immersive
- Afternoon: Reflective but not slow, maintain momentum through activities

**Intervention points:**
- Only intervene when participants are genuinely stuck
- Let productive failure happen
