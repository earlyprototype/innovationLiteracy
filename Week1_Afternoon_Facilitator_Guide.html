Name the 11 Elements (00:15-00:20)

Framework Handoff

"Right. Let me name what you experienced:

  • Process: Design Thinking gave you a structure - Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test. Without it, you'd have stayed in the false-start.
  • Interventions: 'Dig deeper,' 'don't defend your prototype,' '7-minute build' - these are methods that push you to the next stage. HOW you're told to use tools.
  • Tools: Mural, paper, pens, scissors. WHAT you use.
  • Skills: Observation, questioning, synthesis, creative thinking, systems thinking - when you connected your partner's wallet to their life system, that's systems thinking.
  • Mindset: Curiosity, deferring judgement, challenging assumptions.
  • Behaviours: Active listening, not defending your ideas, disciplined experimentation.
  • Environment: Time boundaries, digital space, permission to fail.
  • Collaboration: Paired work, giving feedback, receiving feedback without defensiveness.
  • Facilitation: I managed the process, kept time, intervened when needed.

All 11 elements were active this morning - the first nine explicitly, and Governance and Resources implicitly. The time you were given, the materials, the permission to experiment and fail - that's what Governance and Resources look like in practice. These are the foundations of innovation work."

2. Failure Analysis

Setup (00:30-00:32)

"Right, you brought examples of failed innovations from your context. We're going to analyse them using the framework you now have. Small groups - 3-4 people. You have 20 minutes. For each failure:

  1. Map it onto the Design Thinking process - where did it break?
  2. What was missing? Which elements?
  3. What would have changed the outcome?"

On screen: Mural failure analysis workspace (columns for each Design Thinking phase)

Breakout rooms: Small groups, 20 minutes

During Breakouts (00:32-00:50)

  • Pop into rooms, listen
  • Ask probing questions if groups are stuck
  • Note common failure patterns for debrief

Debrief (00:50-00:58)

Share Failures

Each group shares one failure and diagnosis.

Listen For Patterns

  • "We jumped to solutions" (Define phase missing)
  • "We didn't talk to users" (Empathise phase missing)
  • "We had the process but no time to use it" (Environment/Resources missing)
  • "Leadership wanted ROI metrics during discovery" (wrong measurement, operational pressure)
  • Cargo-culting, weak problem framing, missing empathy, process without defence capability

Pattern Recognition (00:58-01:00)

Framing Close

"Notice the patterns. Innovation fails when:

  • Process is cargo-culted (we say we do Design Thinking but skip the hard parts)
  • Empathy is skipped (we design for imaginary users)
  • Problems are poorly framed (we solve the wrong thing well)
  • Wrong metrics are applied (operational measures crush exploratory work)
  • Practitioners have process skills but no organisational defence

You now have a foundational diagnostic lens. You can start recognising where and why innovation breaks."

On screen: Slide "Why Innovation Fails" (diagnostic framework)

3. Intervention Speed-Dating

Setup (01:00-01:02)

"You've experienced interventions this morning - 'dig deeper,' 'don't defend,' time constraints. Now you're going to try five specific methods that help you move through the innovation process. Five stations, five minutes each. You'll rotate through all of them. Each station has a method and an instruction to apply it to the Wallet Project challenge. Go fast - focus on understanding what each method does, not perfecting it."

On screen: Mural board with 5 intervention stations pre-configured

Format: Self-guided rotation through Mural stations

Music: ON (low) during work phases

The Five Stations

Station 1: How Might We Statements (5 mins)

Linguistic reframing for problem definition

Station 2: 2x2 Prioritisation Matrix (5 mins)

Decision-making framework (Impact vs Effort)

Station 3: Assumption Mapping (5 mins)

Identify and test assumptions

Station 4: Stakeholder Mapping (5 mins)

See the system around the problem

Station 5: SWOT Analysis (5 mins)

Analyse strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats

During Stations (01:02-01:25)

  • Monitor progress
  • Answer questions as they arise
  • Note which methods resonate, which feel forced

Debrief (01:25-01:30)

Questions

  • "Which method felt most natural?"
  • "Which felt forced?"
  • "When would you use each one?"

Expected responses: "HMW felt useful for problem framing" / "2x2 matrix helped prioritise" / "SWOT felt corporate" (useful observation)

Framing Close (01:29-01:30)

"These are interventions - structured methods that help you advance the process. Some will fit your context, some won't. The point is knowing they exist and beginning to recognise when to deploy them. This is foundations work - you're building awareness of what's available, not mastery of each method."

4. Measurement Game

Setup (01:30-01:32)

"Right, you're on a funding committee. You have budget to allocate across three innovation projects. Here are the projects:

  • Project A: Early empathy phase - team conducting user interviews
  • Project B: Mid-prototyping phase - team building and testing prototypes
  • Project C: Late validation phase - team assessing market fit

You have these metrics to guide your decision:

  • Total number of ideas generated
  • ROI projection
  • Number of prototypes built
  • Customer satisfaction score
  • Time to market

You have 10 minutes. Decide how to allocate funding. Use the metrics provided."

On screen: Mural workspace with projects listed, metrics provided, budget allocation sliders

Music: ON (low) during work

During Work (01:32-01:42)

Let them struggle with the mismatched metrics. Don't hint at the trap.

Music: OFF at 01:42

Debrief (01:42-01:48)

Questions

  • "How did you allocate funding?"
  • "Which metrics drove your decisions?"
  • "Did anything feel wrong?"

Expected responses: "We funded Project C because it had ROI data" / "Project A looked weak - no metrics yet" / "The metrics didn't fit the projects"

The Reveal (01:48-01:50)

Pattern Interrupt

"Right. Here's what just happened: I gave you completely stage-inappropriate metrics. You can't measure ROI during empathy - you don't have a solution yet. Counting ideas during validation is meaningless - you're testing fit, not generating options. But these are the metrics organisations apply to innovation all the time. And when innovation gets measured wrong, it gets killed.

Stage-appropriate measurement matters:

  • Empathy phase: Quality of insights, depth of understanding
  • Ideation phase: Diversity of concepts, not volume
  • Prototyping phase: Learning velocity, feedback quality
  • Validation phase: Evidence of fit, market signals

Wrong metrics crush innovation faster than bad ideas."

On screen: Slide "Stage-Appropriate Measurement Principles"

Action: Post in Mural + chat
