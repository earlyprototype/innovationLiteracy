0. Overview

Afternoon Architecture

Morning = DO. Afternoon = REFLECT, ANALYSE, DIAGNOSE.

This session makes the implicit explicit. You're handing them the map for territory they just travelled. The goal is transferable learning - they should be able to apply this diagnostic lens to their own work.

Facilitator Stance - Afternoon

  • Shift energy: From immersive doing to analytical thinking
  • Still engaging: Reflective doesn't mean slow - maintain momentum
  • Name patterns: Point back to specific morning moments
  • Connect dots: Link experience to conceptual frameworks
  • Trust the learning: They've done the work - you're just labelling what happened
MUSIC: OFF for most of afternoon (only ON during individual work phases)

On screen: Start with Mural board, switch to slides for framework presentations

Session Flow

1. Deep Reflection (30 mins): Map experience to framework, name 11 elements

2. Failure Analysis (30 mins): Apply diagnostic lens to real-world failures

3. Intervention Speed-Dating (30 mins): Hands-on with 5 tactical methods

4. Measurement Game (20 mins): Expose stage-inappropriate metrics

5. Problem Space Map (10 mins): Demo systems thinking tool

6. Homework Brief (10 mins): Set up week gap work

2. Failure Analysis

Same-Company Dynamics

Three participants from one company changes everything. They share context, shared failures, shared organizational challenges. This creates both opportunity (deep relevance) and risk (blame, defensiveness, oversharing dysfunction).

Your role: Keep focus on diagnostic learning, not organizational therapy or venting.

Purpose of This Exercise

Transfer learning from trivial challenge (wallet) to their real work. This is where foundations become practical. Watch for aha moments: "Oh, that's why our project failed - we skipped empathy."

Setup (00:30-00:32)

"Right, you brought examples of failed innovations from your context. We're going to analyse them using the framework you now have. You'll work together as one group. You have 20 minutes. For each failure:

  1. Map it onto the Design Thinking process - where did it break?
  2. What was missing? Which elements?
  3. What would have changed the outcome?"

Ground rule: This is diagnostic work, not blame. We're looking at systems and patterns, not finger-pointing. What you share here stays here."

MUSIC: OFF - focused analytical conversation

On screen: Mural failure analysis workspace (columns for each Design Thinking phase)

Working mode: Single group of 3 (same company), 20 minutes

TIMING: This runs 00:32-00:50 (18 mins working, return at 00:50)

During Session (00:32-00:50)

Stay present but not hovering: Give them space to talk openly about their failures.

Listen for: Are they analyzing or blaming? Connecting to framework or just storytelling?

If they're stuck: "Where on the Design Thinking map does this sit? What phase broke?"

If analyzing not diagnosing: "Which of the 11 elements was missing or weak?"

Watch for: One person dominating, defensiveness, avoiding real issues, or conversely oversharing organizational dysfunction

Probing questions if needed:

  • "Was this an empathy failure, a problem framing failure, or something else?"
  • "If you ran this project again tomorrow, what would you do differently in the first week?"

Common Patterns to Listen For

  • Solution jumping: "We never talked to users, just built what we thought they wanted"
  • Weak problem framing: "We didn't really define the problem properly"
  • Process cargo-culting: "We said we were doing Design Thinking but skipped empathy"
  • Wrong metrics: "Leadership wanted ROI projections during discovery phase"
  • Missing defence: "We had the skills but couldn't protect the time/space to use them"

Debrief (00:50-00:58)

Debrief Opening (00:50)

"Right, what patterns did you see? Not just 'this project failed' - what failure modes keep appearing?"

Listen For and Name These Patterns

  • Solution jumping (skipping empathy/define)
  • Wrong metrics applied (ROI during discovery)
  • Process cargo-culting (say Design Thinking, skip hard parts)
  • Missing organizational defence (skills exist but no protection to use them)

Pattern Recognition & Forward Frame (00:56-00:58)

The Pivot to Solutions

Don't let this become a complaint session. After naming patterns, pivot to conditions for success.

Facilitator Framing (00:56-00:58)

"Here's what I'm hearing: [name 2-3 patterns they identified]. These aren't unique to Eirmersive - they're systemic failure modes in organisations trying to innovate.

The question isn't 'why did these projects fail?' The question is: what conditions would allow them to succeed? That's governance work - Week 2 territory."

Why This Shift Matters

Move from problem identification to solution orientation. Plant seed for governance/resources work next week. They need to leave feeling empowered, not defeated by their organizational constraints.

On screen: Slide "Why Innovation Fails" (diagnostic framework)

Action: Post in Mural + chat

Diagnostic Capability Developing

They can now look at failed innovation and see where and why it broke. This is foundational diagnostic literacy - recognising patterns of failure across their organisation.

3. Intervention Speed-Dating

Purpose: Tactical Exposure

This is NOT mastery training - it's exposure and awareness building. They're building a mental catalogue: "When I need to [X], this method exists." Speed is deliberate - prevents perfectionism, maintains energy.

Setup (01:00-01:02)

"You've experienced interventions this morning - 'dig deeper,' 'don't defend,' time constraints. Now you're going to try five specific methods that help you move through the innovation process. Five stations, five minutes each. You'll rotate through all of them. Each station has a method and an instruction to apply it to the Wallet Project challenge. Go fast - focus on understanding what each method does, not perfecting it."

On screen: Mural board with 5 intervention stations pre-configured

Format: Self-guided rotation through Mural stations

TIMING: 5 mins per station = 25 mins total (01:02-01:27). Debrief 01:27-01:30.

The Five Stations

Station 1: How Might We Statements (5 mins)

What it does: Linguistic reframing for problem definition

Part A: Take the wallet problem and reframe it 5 different ways using "How might we...?"

Part B: Note one place in your current Eirmersive work where HMW reframing could apply

Station 2: 2x2 Prioritisation Matrix (5 mins)

What it does: Decision-making framework (Impact vs Effort)

Part A: Plot your wallet ideas on the matrix - which are high impact/low effort?

Part B: Think of a decision at work that needs prioritization - could this matrix help?

Station 3: Assumption Mapping (5 mins)

What it does: Identify and test assumptions

Part A: List 5 assumptions you made about your partner's wallet needs - which need testing?

Part B: What's one untested assumption in your current work at Eirmersive?

Station 4: Stakeholder Mapping (5 mins)

What it does: See the system around the problem

Part A: Who else is affected by wallet design decisions? Map the stakeholder ecosystem.

Part B: For a current project, who are the stakeholders you haven't talked to yet?

Station 5: 5 Whys (5 mins)

What it does: Root cause analysis through iterative questioning

Part A: Take one wallet frustration. Ask "why?" five times to find root cause.

Example: "My wallet is bulky" → Why? → "I carry too many cards" → Why? → "I don't trust digital payments" → Why? → etc.

Part B: What's a surface-level problem at work that needs deeper analysis?

During Stations (01:02-01:25)

Your role: Light touch facilitation. Answer questions as they arise, but don't over-explain. Watch for which methods click naturally and which feel awkward - this is useful data.

Note: Some participants will want to perfect each method. Remind them: "Focus on understanding what it does, not perfecting it. You have 5 minutes."

Critical: Part B (application to their work) is where the real learning happens. Check their Mural boards - are they making concrete connections or staying abstract?

MUSIC: ON (low) during work phases

Debrief (01:25-01:30)

Questions

  • "Which method felt most natural?"
  • "Which felt forced?"
  • "Which one could you use next week at Eirmersive? What would you apply it to?"

Expected responses: "HMW felt useful for problem framing" / "2x2 matrix helped prioritise" / "5 Whys helped find the real problem" / "Assumption mapping was eye-opening"

Follow-Up Probe

"I saw you noted [specific application from their boards]. What would happen if you actually did that? What's stopping you?"

Purpose of This Probe

Bridge awareness to action. Surface organizational barriers. Set up governance conversation for Week 2. Don't solve the barriers now - just make them visible.

Framing Close (01:29-01:30)

"These are interventions - structured methods that help you advance the process. Some will fit your context, some won't. The point is knowing they exist and beginning to recognise when to deploy them. This is foundations work - you're building awareness of what's available, not mastery of each method."

Building the Toolkit

They now have 5 tactical methods in their awareness. Not mastery - awareness. They know these exist and roughly what they do. That's sufficient for foundations work. Mastery comes through practice.

4. Measurement Game

This is a Trap - By Design

The metrics are deliberately mismatched to the project phases. Participants should feel discomfort. That discomfort is the learning. Do not rescue them. Let them struggle for the full 10 minutes.

Why This Exercise Matters

Innovation gets killed by wrong metrics more often than bad ideas. Operational measures applied to exploratory work crush innovation before it starts. This exercise makes that visible and visceral.

Setup (01:30-01:32)

"Right, you're on a funding committee. You have budget to allocate across three innovation projects. Here are the projects:

  • Project A: Early empathy phase - team conducting user interviews
  • Project B: Mid-prototyping phase - team building and testing prototypes
  • Project C: Late validation phase - team assessing market fit

You have these metrics to guide your decision:

  • Total number of ideas generated
  • ROI projection
  • Number of prototypes built
  • Customer satisfaction score
  • Time to market

You have 10 minutes. Decide how to allocate funding. Use the metrics provided."

TIMING: Work 01:32-01:42 (10 mins). Debrief 01:42-01:48 (6 mins). Reveal 01:48-01:50 (2 mins).

On screen: Mural workspace with projects listed, metrics provided, budget allocation sliders

MUSIC: ON (low) during work

The Trap

Let them struggle with the mismatched metrics. Don't hint. Don't rescue.

This discomfort is pedagogical. They need to feel the mismatch before the reveal lands. Watch for frustration - "These metrics don't make sense" - that's the signal it's working.

MUSIC: OFF at 01:42

Debrief (01:42-01:48)

Questions

  • "How did you allocate funding?"
  • "Which metrics drove your decisions?"
  • "Did anything feel wrong?"

Expected responses: "We funded Project C because it had ROI data" / "Project A looked weak - no metrics yet" / "The metrics didn't fit the projects"

The Reveal (01:48-01:50)

Pattern Interrupt

"Right. Here's what just happened: I gave you completely stage-inappropriate metrics. You can't measure ROI during empathy - you don't have a solution yet. Counting ideas during validation is meaningless - you're testing fit, not generating options. But these are the metrics organisations apply to innovation all the time. And when innovation gets measured wrong, it gets killed.

Stage-appropriate measurement matters:

  • Empathy phase: Quality of insights, depth of understanding
  • Ideation phase: Diversity of concepts, not volume
  • Prototyping phase: Learning velocity, feedback quality
  • Validation phase: Evidence of fit, market signals

Wrong metrics crush innovation faster than bad ideas."

On screen: Slide "Stage-Appropriate Measurement Principles"

Action: Post in Mural + chat

Bridge to Their Context (01:50)

"Quick check: What metrics are you measured by at Eirmersive when you're working on innovation projects? Are they stage-appropriate or are you getting crushed by operational measures during exploratory work?"

Listen For

Tension between innovation needs and organizational measurement systems. Don't solve it now - note it for Week 2 governance work. This makes the lesson personal and visceral. They felt the trap in the exercise; now they see it in their work.

Critical Insight Gained

Innovation requires stage-appropriate metrics. Operational measures (ROI, time to market) during exploratory phases crush the work. They've now felt this tension viscerally - the learning will stick.
