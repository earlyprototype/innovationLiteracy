0. Overview

Why the Wallet Project?

  • Everyone has experience with a wallet or similar object
  • The wallet and its contents evoke meaning and connect to larger life context
  • Enables wide range of innovation outcomes (objects, experiences, services, systems, spaces)
  • Physical artifact allows immediate recall of experiences
  • Trivial challenge keeps cognitive load low - focus on process, not content expertise

Project-Based Learning Philosophy

DO, then REFLECT - not "tell, then do"

Provide limited scaffolding to allow participants to experience first, then facilitate reflection where they extract their own learning. The experience does the teaching.

Critical Facilitator Behaviors

  • Be assertive about timing - Find a fun way to announce "Time's up!" Use a gong, sound effect, or distinctive signal
  • Keep timing TIGHT - this is not negotiable. Rushed timing is pedagogical, not accidental
  • It's normal for people to freeze - remind them of time left to push them to start
  • Use music strategically - it's architectural to the experience (see music cues throughout)
  • Watch, don't intervene - let productive failure happen, debrief patterns afterwards

1. Opening Provocation

Opening (00:00)

"Right, let's start. No preamble. I'm going to give you an instruction, and I want you to follow it without questioning or discussing. You'll have 5 minutes. Silent. Individual work. Pen and paper. Here's the task: Draw your ideal innovation process. Use whatever format makes sense to you - flowchart, diagram, sketch, words. Go."

MUSIC: OFF - Silence is deliberate. Creates stark contrast with the rest of the morning.

On screen: Slide - "Draw Your Ideal Innovation Process" (minimal, no hints)

Timer: 5 minutes visible

Facilitator Behavior During Drawing

It is normal for people to freeze and delay putting anything down. This productive discomfort is intentional. Remind them of time remaining to push them to start, but don't rescue them from the discomfort.

Watch During Drawing (00:00-00:05)

  • Some will create linear flows, some loops, some complex systems
  • Some will freeze (productive discomfort - let it happen)
  • Note visible patterns or contradictions via camera feeds
  • Don't comment - just observe

Gallery Walk (00:05)

"Time. Hold your drawings up to the camera."

"I'm going to look through these. Don't explain yours yet - just look at each other's. Notice patterns. Notice contradictions."

Facilitation Moves (00:05-00:10)

  • Screen camera feeds to review drawings
  • Point to patterns: "Three of you have linear flows. Two have loops. One has question marks."
  • Point to contradictions: "This one starts with 'problem.' This one starts with 'brainstorm.' This one starts with 'research.'"
  • Don't judge. Don't correct. Just observe aloud.

Debrief Questions (00:10-00:13)

  • "What assumptions did you make about what 'innovation process' means?"
  • "What did you leave out?"
  • "How confident are you that your drawing would work in practice?"

Expected responses: "I wasn't sure where to start" / "I realised I don't know what innovation process actually means" / "Mine looks nothing like theirs"

Closing Reframe (00:13-00:15)

"Good. You've just discovered the first thing about innovation: we use the same words and mean completely different things. Park your drawings. We'll return to them this afternoon."

The "False Start" Strategy

Of course, you don't tell participants it's a false start. The intention is to contrast an abstract problem-centric approach (typical for many people) with the human-centred design thinking approach they'll experience in the rest of the project.

This is deliberately problem-solving, not design thinking. They need to feel the difference.

Phase 1: False Start (00:30-00:35)

Opening Challenge (00:30)

"Right, your challenge: redesign the wallet. You have 3 minutes. Go."

MUSIC: ON (moderate volume) - Contrast with the silent opening provocation

What happens: Participants jump to solutions, sketch wallet designs based on assumptions, no research

Facilitator Behavior

Watch silently. Don't intervene. Note who freezes, who jumps in confidently. This is data for the debrief.

Stop & Show (00:33)

"Time. Show me what you've got."

Gallery Walk Question (00:33-00:34)

"Interesting. Who did you design this for?"

Expected responses: "Um... everyone?" / "People?" / "Wallet users?"

The Interrupt (00:34-00:35)

"Right. You just designed wallets for imaginary people based on your assumptions. That's not innovation - that's guessing. Let's start again. This time, you'll talk to a real human first."

On screen: Mural board, individual workspaces now marked "False Start - Park This"

Phase 2: Empathy Round 1 (00:35-00:45)

Setup (00:35-00:36)

"You're going to pair up. One of you is the interviewer, one is the user. Interviewer: your job is to understand your partner's relationship with their wallet. User: answer honestly. You have 5 minutes. Then you'll switch roles. Breakout rooms opening now."

MUSIC: OFF during interviews - enables focus and conversation

Breakout rooms: Pairs for 5 minutes each (10 mins total)

Interview Provocations (posted in Mural as starting points)

These are conversation starters, not scripts. Encourage participants to follow interesting threads.

  • "Tell me about your current wallet"
  • "When do you use it?"
  • "What frustrates you about it?"
  • "What works well?"

Purpose: Start the conversation. Most will stay surface-level - that's expected and necessary for the "dig deeper" intervention to land.

During Breakouts

Pop into rooms briefly, listen. Most will stay surface-level. Don't intervene yet - let it happen. Some will ask good questions, some will lead with solutions. This is data for what comes next.

Phase 3: "Dig Deeper" Intervention + Round 2 (00:45-01:00)

Return & Reflect (00:45-00:46)

"Back to the main room. Quick reflection: what did you learn about your partner's wallet?"

Expected responses: "They keep receipts in it" / "They don't like the colour" / "It's too bulky" (surface-level facts)

THE CRITICAL INTERVENTION (00:46-00:47)

This is THE pivotal teaching moment of the entire morning. Deliver it with conviction.

"Right. Those are facts. Now I want you to go deeper.

  • Why do they keep receipts?
  • What does their wallet say about how they live?
  • What jobs is their wallet doing beyond holding money?

You're going back in for round 2. Dig deeper this time."

What You're Teaching (Without Saying It)

The difference between surface-level facts and deep insights. Between describing and understanding. Between problem-centric and human-centred design. This intervention is why they're here.

MUSIC: OFF - keep focus on conversation depth

Breakout rooms: Same pairs, 5 minutes each (10 mins total)

Deeper Provocations (posted in Mural)

Purpose: Dig for stories, feelings, and emotion. Ask "WHY?" often. Forget about the wallet - find out what's important to your partner.

  • "Why do you organise your wallet that way?"
  • "What would you lose if your wallet disappeared?"
  • "When does your wallet fail you?"
  • "What does your wallet say about you?"

These questions connect the wallet to life context, identity, systems, and meaning.

Listen For The Shift

Depth shift - emotional connections, identity aspects, system implications. Aha moments: "I never thought about it that way." The quality of conversation should change noticeably. Note specific examples for afternoon debrief.

Note Themes

Listen and note themes for afternoon reflection

Phase 5: Problem Framing (01:10-01:20)

HMW Framing (01:10-01:11)

"Now frame the problem you're solving. Not 'design a better wallet' - that's a solution masquerading as a problem. What's the actual need your partner has? Frame it as a question: 'How might we...?'

Example: 'How might we help Sarah keep track of receipts without bulk?' or 'How might we make Mark feel more organised when he travels?'

You have 7 minutes. Write multiple versions. Pick the one that feels most generative."

MUSIC: ON - individual thinking work

On screen: Mural workspace for How Might We statements

Commit (01:17)

"Time to commit. Circle your best HMW statement."

Brief Share (01:18-01:20)

"Quick round: read your HMW statement."

Track Quality

Note how well problems are framed - useful for afternoon reflection

Generate Solutions (01:20-01:21)

Phase 8: Feedback + Iteration (01:40-01:50)

Setup Feedback (01:40-01:41)

"Back to your pairs. You're going to show your sketch to your partner - the person you interviewed. Here's the rule: don't defend your design. Just show it and ask: 'What works? What doesn't? What's missing?' Listen. Take notes. You have 3 minutes each."

MUSIC: OFF - enables focused conversation

Breakout rooms: Pairs, 3 minutes per person (6 mins total)

Key Behaviour to Watch

Participants receiving feedback without defending - this is a critical skill to model. Note who struggles with this - it's useful data for afternoon reflection.

During Breakouts

Pop into rooms and listen for defensiveness. If someone is explaining/defending rather than listening, gently remind: "Just listen - take notes on what they're saying." But mostly, let it play out naturally.

Iterate (01:46-01:47)

"You've got feedback. Now iterate. Modify your design based on what you learned. You have 3 minutes."

MUSIC: ON for quick iteration work

Phase 9: Prototyping (01:50-01:57)

Build It (01:50)

"Now build it. Use whatever materials you have at hand - paper, pens, scissors, tape, household items. You have 7 minutes. Make something your partner can hold and react to. Go."

MUSIC: ON (high energy) - maximum energy for making mode

Keep Energy High

This is the most tactile, kinetic phase. Your energy should match the music. Watch for creative problem-solving and interesting material choices. Call out good making behavior as it happens - "I love how you're using that!" - to maintain momentum.

During Prototyping

  • Watch for creative problem-solving
  • Note interesting material choices
  • Note who gets stuck in perfectionism vs who embraces rough

Final Test (01:57)

Return & Close (01:59-02:00)

"Right. You've just run a complete Design Thinking cycle in 90 minutes. You started with assumptions, threw them out, talked to humans, framed problems, generated ideas, got feedback, iterated, built prototypes, and tested them. That's the process. This afternoon we'll unpack what just happened and why it matters."

What They Experienced (For Afternoon Unpacking)

  • The difference between assumption-driven design (false start) and human-centred design
  • The shift from surface facts to deep insights (dig deeper intervention)
  • The power of constraint (tight timing forced decisions and action)
  • The value of iteration (feedback changed their designs)
  • The role of prototyping (tangible artifacts enable better feedback)
  • The complete innovation cycle - lived, not lectured

Closing
