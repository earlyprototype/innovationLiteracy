# Workshop Overview - Fundamentals of Innovation Literacy

## Introduction
This workshop series provides a route for participants to form a grounding in the foundational elements that comprise a literacy in the process of innovation. Participants execute sandboxed innovation experiences explore what makes them work (and why their attempts intially might have failed). At its conclusion, participants will have a complete picture of the innovation process, its dependancies and how to engage on an organisational level to get it done.

**Client:** Eirmersive (XR advocates, R&D partners, technology adoption specialists), led by Camille. Mix of intuitive innovation understanding and desire for structured upskilling.

---

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this workshop, participants will have:

**Baseline Process Understanding:**
- Begun recognising each element of innovation as process, its value and how it contributes to the overall process
- Awareness of stage-appropriate interventions and methods
- Familiarity with tools for collaboration, prototyping, and problem framing

**Diagnostic Capability:**
- Framework for identifying which elements are present or missing in their context
- Ability to distinguish between cargo-culting (innovation theatre) and substantive practice
- Recognition of common failure patterns: wrong metrics, missing empathy, weak problem framing, process without organisational defence

**Foundational Advocacy Skills:**
- Language to articulate what sustained innovation requires
- Awareness of how to explain innovation contribution across different audiences
- Understanding of governance frameworks and resource justification
- Baseline capability to defend innovation conditions when challenged

**Routes for Continued Learning:**
- Access to framework materials and resources for self-directed development
- Awareness of how process understanding transfers to their own contexts
- Recognition of when to seek deeper expertise in specific elements

## Workshop Design Principles

**Core Principle:** 
Integrate theory and practice. Participants learn by doing, supported by rigorous learning resources and expert facilitation. 

**Pedagogy:** 
Experiental learning supported by guided facilitation - rooted in try first/experiementation, reflection and ties to underlying theory (via adjacent learning material). 

This is foundations work - baseline literacy to recognise the landscape, know what matters, and enable continued capacity building through follow-on reading via supporting resources and integration into daily practice.

**Structure:** 
Series of four focused, experiential workshops across two full day sessions. Interventions targetted to introduce all elements of learning. Trivial innovaiton topics (wallets, gift-giving) in situ create low stakes and keep cognitive load low so participants can focus on process, not content. Homework brings the learning back to their real operations for deeper consieration.

## Purpose and Framing
Innovation, be it process, product, pricing or otherwise is part of every organisations' operational experience. Developing an understanding of the elements that underly effective innovation has broad, empowering impact that drive better outcomes for these organisations. Secondary benefits include increased employee engagement [6][7], work satisfaction [8][9] and the surfacing of latent skills [10][11]. 

Innovation projects fail more than they succeed (40-90% abandonment rate [1]; 70%+ digital transformation failure [2]) and the cost is enormous (UK: £64.7B annually [3]; Global: $2.3T [4]). Why?

 - Innovation Management confused as, or deprived of process expertise [12]
 - Process without context [13]
 - Great teams deprived of vital resources due to poor organisation understanding of value and needs [14]

Despite 70% of senior executives citing innovation as a top-3 growth driver, 65% report low confidence in their innovation decisions [5] 

Increased Innovation Literacy is one route to stemming these failure rates and cost bleeds, while increasing employee confidence and engagement with work.

## Framework: Elements of Innovation Literacy
The workshop activities expose participants to 11 key elements of innovation as process categorise as three distinct components. Learning happens in a contextualised, experiental manner. Learning resources connect elements to experiential learning as they happen, while proviing routes for deeper exploration.

### What (Elements 1-3):
1. **Process** - The Structure - repeatable activity (Design Thinking, Double Diamond)
2. **Interventions** - The Mechanics - Stage-dependent structured mini-processes that move innovation along (e.g., "How Might We..", Future Frontpage, Process Mapping etc..)
3. **Tools** - The Supports:Technologies and digital platforms/tools (Mural, Kanban boards, AI assistants like ChatGPT for ideation/observation).

### How (Elements 4-9):
4. **Skills and Competencies** - Analytical thinking, creative thinking, communication, experimentation, comfort with ambiguity, systems thinking
5. **Mindset** - Curiosity, tolerance for uncertainty, challenging assumptions, holding multiple perspectives
6. **Behaviours** - Active listening, constructive challenge, disciplined experimentation, reflective practice
7. **Environment** - Physical spaces, their design, setup and the power of boundaries (literal and metaphorical)
8. **Collaboration** - Psychological safety, cognitive diversity, role clarity, productive tension, effective teamwork
9. **Facilitation** - Unlocking potential of processes and collaboration, managing constructive conflict, enabling learning (the synthesis capability)

### Why (Elements 10-11):
10. **Governance** - Enabling organisational communication and setting of shared values, vision and goal orientation. Articulation of requirements outward and expectations inwards. Boundary setting. Permissions. (charters, decision rights, permission structures).
11. **Resources** - Requirements - Time, budget, people, and materials. 

**Individual Element Materials stored in:** `./Elements/[0-11]_[ElementName]/What is [ElementName].md`

**00:00-00:15** Opening Provocation: "Draw Your Ideal Innovation Process" 

**Structure:** Individual - 5 minutes - Silent - Pen/paper
**Ask:** 
- "Draw your ideal innovation process. You have 5 minutes. Use whatever format makes sense to you - flowchart, diagram, sketch, words. Go."

**Framing:** None - no context provided before the task
**Purpose:** Expose knowledge gaps, create productive humility, diagnostic baseline
**Elements touched:** Process (diagnostic), Mindset (challenging assumptions)

**Post-Intervention (5 mins):**
- Facilitator screens camera feed to review drawings (participants keep cameras on with drawings visible)
- Point to patterns observed: "Three of you have linear flows. Two have loops. One has question marks."
- Point to contradictions: "This one starts with 'problem.' This one starts with 'brainstorm.' This one starts with 'research.'"
- Ask: "What assumptions did you make about what 'innovation process' means?"
- Don't judge. Don't correct. Just observe aloud.
- Facilitator: "You've just discovered the first thing about innovation: we use the same words and mean completely different things. Park your drawings. We'll return to them this afternoon."

**00:30-02:00** IDEO Wallet Project (90 mins)

**Structure:** Paired work (breakout rooms) - Rapid cycles - Music during work phases
**Ask:** "Redesign the wallet. You have 90 minutes. Full cycle from discovery to testing."
**Framing:** None initially - deliberate false start, then intervention
**Purpose:** Experiential immersion in complete Design Thinking cycle, structured failure, pattern interrupts
**Elements touched:** All 11 (experiential immersion — Elements 1-9 explicitly, Elements 10-11 implicitly through the workshop's own governance and resource structures)

**Activity phases:**
1. False-start (5 mins): "Redesign the wallet. 3 minutes. Go." [Participants jump to solutions]
2. Intervention: "Stop. You designed for imaginary people. Let's start again with a real human."
3. Empathy round 1 (10 mins): Paired interviews in breakout rooms
4. Intervention: "Dig deeper" - push beyond surface-level inquiry
5. Empathy round 2 (10 mins): Same pairs, deeper questions
6. Synthesis (10 mins): Individual processing of interview data
7. Problem framing (10 mins): How Might We statements
8. Ideation (10 mins): Volume over quality, defer judgement
9. Sketching (10 mins): Develop selected concept
10. Feedback + iteration (10 mins): Show partner, revise based on input
11. Prototyping (7 mins): Build using home materials (paper, pens, tape, household items)
12. Testing (3 mins): Show prototype to partner via camera, get reaction

**Remote adaptations:** Breakout rooms for paired work, prototypes shown via camera, digital sketching in Mural optional, music via screen share

**Post-Intervention:**
- Brief reflection: "You just ran a complete Design Thinking cycle in 90 minutes"
- No deep analysis yet - save for afternoon

**00:00-00:30** Deep Reflection on Wallet Project

**Structure:** Facilitated group discussion with visual mapping
**Ask:** "Let's map what you did this morning onto the Design Thinking framework"
**Framing:** "This morning you executed a complete innovation cycle. Now I'm giving you the map for the territory you just travelled."
**Purpose:** Make process explicit, connect experience to framework, name elements
**Elements touched:** Process (explicit), Skills, Facilitation (reflection structure)

**Activity flow:**

5. Name all 11 elements: "Process gave you structure. Interventions pushed you forward. Tools enabled the work. Skills, Mindset, Behaviours were tested. Environment was the container. Collaboration happened in pairs. Facilitation managed it all. And Governance and Resources were present too — the time protection, the materials, the permission to experiment. All 11 elements were in play."

**00:30-01:00** Failure Analysis Workshop

**Structure:** Small groups (3-4 people) in breakout rooms - 20 mins discussion, 10 mins report back
**Ask:** "Share your homework examples - failed innovations from your context. Map them onto Design Thinking process: where did it break? What was missing?"
**Framing:** "You now have a diagnostic lens. Let's use it on real failures."
**Purpose:** Apply framework to real contexts, identify patterns, develop diagnostic capability
**Elements touched:** Process (diagnostic), Skills, Interventions (identifying missing pattern interrupts)

**Activity flow:**
1. Breakout rooms: 3-4 people per group
2. Each person shares their failure example (2-3 mins each)
3. Group maps each failure onto Design Thinking phases - where did it break?
4. Group identifies which elements were missing
5. Group documents patterns in Mural workspace

**Common patterns to surface:**
- Cargo-culting (innovation theatre without substance)
- Wrong metrics (operational measures crushing exploratory work)
- Missing empathy (solution-jumping without user understanding)
- Weak problem framing (unclear what problem is being solved)
- Process without organisational defence (practitioners crushed by operational pressure)

**Post-Activity:**
- Return to main room
- Each group shares one failure and diagnosis (2 mins each)
- Facilitator notes patterns across groups
- Framework handoff: "Why Innovation Fails" diagnostic framework (posted in Mural + chat)

**01:00-01:30** Intervention Speed-Dating

**Structure:** Self-paced rotation through Mural stations - 5 stations, 5 mins each, 5 mins debrief
**Ask:** "Five intervention methods, five minutes each. Apply each one to the Wallet Project. Focus on understanding what each method does, not perfecting it."
**Framing:** "You've experienced interventions this morning - 'dig deeper,' 'don't defend,' time constraints. Now try five specific methods that help you advance the innovation process."
**Purpose:** Hands-on exposure to tactical methods, awareness of when to deploy different interventions
**Elements touched:** Interventions (hands-on with methods and frameworks), Skills (analytical thinking)

**Five stations (pre-configured in Mural):**

Station 1: **How Might We Statements** - Linguistic reframing for problem definition. Instruction: "Reframe the Wallet Project using HMW format."

Station 2: **2x2 Prioritisation Matrix** - Decision framework (Impact vs Effort). Instruction: "Plot your Wallet Project ideas on this matrix."

Station 3: **Assumption Mapping** - Identify and test assumptions. Instruction: "What assumptions did you make about your partner's wallet needs?"

Station 4: **Stakeholder Mapping** - See the system around the problem. Instruction: "Who are all the stakeholders in the wallet ecosystem?"

Station 5: **SWOT Analysis** - Analyse strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats. Instruction: "SWOT your wallet design concept."

**Post-Activity (5 mins):**
- Return to main room
- Quick debrief: "Which method felt most natural? Which felt forced? When would you use each one?"
- Facilitator: "These are interventions. Some will fit your context, some won't. The point is knowing they exist and beginning to recognise when to deploy them."

**01:30-01:50** The Measurement Game

**Structure:** Individual decision-making (10 mins) then group debrief (10 mins)
**Ask:** "You're on a funding committee. Three innovation projects need budget. Allocate funding using the metrics provided."
**Framing:** Deliberate trap - expose how wrong metrics crush innovation
**Purpose:** Understand stage-appropriate measurement, experience operational pressure, recognise metric misalignment
**Elements touched:** Tools (measurement frameworks), Environment (operational pressure), Skills (analytical thinking)

**Scenario (displayed in Mural):**

Project A: Early empathy phase - team conducting user interviews
Project B: Mid-prototyping phase - team building and testing prototypes  
Project C: Late validation phase - team assessing market fit

**Metrics provided (deliberately stage-inappropriate):**
- Total number of ideas generated
- ROI projection
- Number of prototypes built
- Customer satisfaction score
- Time to market

**Activity flow:**
1. Participants review three projects and metrics (2 mins)
2. Allocate funding individually in Mural workspace (8 mins)
3. Share decisions in main room (2 mins)
4. Facilitator reveals the trap (3 mins)
5. Discussion: What metrics would be better? Why does measurement matter? (5 mins)

**Post-Activity:**
- Facilitator: "I gave you completely stage-inappropriate metrics. You can't measure ROI during empathy - you don't have a solution yet. Counting ideas during validation is meaningless - you're testing fit, not generating options. But these are the metrics organisations apply to innovation all the time. Wrong metrics crush innovation faster than bad ideas."
- Framework handoff: Stage-Appropriate Measurement Principles (posted in Mural + chat)

## Design Rationale

**Opening provocation:** "Draw your ideal innovation process" functions as diagnostic, exposes knowledge gaps, introduces digital collaboration tools

**Challenge selection:** Trivial challenges (wallet, gift-giving) over strategic challenges focuses participants on process (HOW) rather than content (WHAT)

**Multiple micro-cycles:** Repeated short cycles build fluency more effectively than single extended challenge

**Framework handoff timing:** Theory delivered after experience, not before. Participants feel the need before receiving the map.

**Element visibility:** Colour-coding/tagging system allows participants to identify which capability they're building at any moment

**Timing management:** Extensions flagged in advance with participants

## Framework Structure: The What/How/Why

Three-category structure (What 1-3, How 4-9, Why 10-11) provides memorable organisation without prescribing participant identity.

### Element 10: Governance

**Function:** Bidirectional communication infrastructure between innovation practice and broader organisation

**Inward flow:** Organisational why → innovation practitioners
- Purpose, legitimacy, permission to operate differently

**Outward flow:** Innovation requirements/contributions → organisation
- Evidence of value, articulation of needs, alignment demonstration

**Mechanisms:** Charters, decision rights, permission structures, leadership understanding

**Purpose:** Provides insulation for innovation practice from operational expectations through formal organisational agreement

### Element 11: Resources

**Definition:** Time, budget, people, and materials

**Dynamic:** Continuously contested against operational priorities, requires ongoing negotiation and defence

**Validation:** Requirements justified within governance framework established by Element 10

**Evidence base:** Lived experience of Elements 1-9 provides justification ("need X because without it, Element Y breaks")

**Sequence:** Governance establishes validation framework before resource requirements specified

### Roles

Formal role definition (beyond Facilitator) belongs in advanced innovation management contexts, not foundations work. Role clarity within Collaboration (8) covers dynamic role negotiation during innovation work.
