# What are Resources?

## Definition

Resources are the time, budget, people, and materials required for innovation work. They are continuously contested against operational priorities and require ongoing negotiation and defence.

Resources are never "secured" - they are continuously negotiated. Requirements are validated and justified within the governance framework established by Element 10, using lived experience of Elements 1-9 as evidence base.

## Why It Matters

Innovation investment is inherently vulnerable: it competes for resources with operational activities that have more immediate, more visible, and more predictable returns.

Without deliberate, ongoing practice of demonstrating value and defending requirements, innovation budgets and time allocations are first to be cut when pressure mounts. Resource allocation signals priority - organisations claiming to value innovation without allocating dedicated resources are engaging in cargo culting.

Innovation value is often diffuse, delayed, and qualitatively different from operational value. Methods used to demonstrate operational performance (quarterly financials, efficiency metrics, delivery targets) do not capture it. This requires different approach to articulating and defending resource requirements.

## How It Shows Up in Practice

**Time:** Most commonly underestimated resource requirement. Innovation requires protected time not subject to operational interruption or reallocation. "Innovation in your spare time" is not a strategy - it is an abdication. Forms include dedicated innovation days, time-boxed sprints, ring-fenced project periods.

**Budget:** For prototyping materials, tools, external expertise, and research. Not always large, but always deliberate. Absence of dedicated budget forces innovation to operate with operational resources under operational rules.

**People:** Right team composition with sufficient availability. Not innovation as afterthought added to full-time operational roles. Includes access to diverse expertise, cognitive diversity, appropriate seniority and authority.

**Materials and Access:** Physical prototyping materials, digital tools and platforms, access to users/stakeholders, access to data and subject matter expertise, physical or virtual spaces configured for innovation work.

**Evidence-Based Justification:**

By end of workshop, participants can articulate:
- "We need protected time because Behaviours (6) collapse under operational interruptions"
- "We need this budget because Tools (3) enable what manual methods can't"
- "We need facilitation support because managing all elements simultaneously requires meta-capability"
- "I need X because without it, Element Y breaks - and I know that because I experienced it"

Not theoretical advocacy - empirical justification using lived experience as validation.

**The Five Layers of Innovation Value:**

Value articulation matched to different audiences:

1. **Capability Value:** Organisation can now do things it could not before (structured experiments, rigorous problem framing, systematic opportunity evaluation, prototype and test concepts, evidence-based decisions under uncertainty). Persists even if specific projects don't reach implementation.

2. **Learning Value:** What organisation now knows that it didn't before. Validated assumptions, disproven hypotheses, mapped opportunities, identified constraints, characterised risks. Real even when - especially when - it prevents pursuing path that would have failed at greater cost. Most under-communicated category.

3. **Engagement and Retention Value:** Meaningful participation in innovation work has measurable effects on team engagement, job satisfaction, sense of purpose, and retention. Particularly pronounced among high-capability employees. In competitive labour market, offering meaningful innovation work is genuine retention tool.

4. **Pipeline Value:** Portfolio of concepts, prototypes, and validated opportunities at various stages of maturity. Represents future potential and reduces dependence on any single initiative. About optionality - having range of possibilities at different readiness stages means organisation can respond to changing conditions with prepared options rather than starting from scratch.

5. **Strategic Positioning Value:** Visible innovation activity builds credibility, relationships, and presence in emerging spaces. For organisations in R&D partnerships, technology advocacy, or market development, the act of innovating visibly is itself positioning. Signals to partners, clients, funders, and talent that organisation is forward-looking, capable, and engaged with the frontier.

**Matching Evidence to Audience:**
- Leadership and board: pipeline value, strategic positioning, capability growth, risk reduction
- Team members: engagement, skill development, meaningfulness of work, agency
- Middle management: operational relevance, resource efficiency, alignment with delivery targets
- External stakeholders and funders: learning outputs, demonstrated impact, pipeline potential
- Clients and partners: capability, credibility, responsiveness to emerging challenges
